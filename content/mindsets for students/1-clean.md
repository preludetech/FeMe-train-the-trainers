# Mindsets for students

## Growth mindset

By now you've seen how the brain changes with practice, how memory works, and how learning is built through effort. All of that points toward one idea: ability is not fixed. It grows. This is the core of what psychologist Carol Dweck calls a **growth mindset** — the belief that intelligence and skills can be developed through dedication and hard work.

As a trainer, this matters because your learners arrive with their own beliefs about what they can and can't do. Some will think they're "just not a maths person" or "not technical." Those beliefs shape how they respond to difficulty. A learner with a fixed mindset sees struggle as proof of inability. A learner with a growth mindset sees it as part of the process.

You don't change someone's mindset by telling them to have a different one. You shape it through how you teach — how you respond to mistakes, how you frame challenges, and what you praise.

## Stress-is-enhancing mindset

Growth mindset gets a lot of attention, but there is another mindset that matters just as much in a learning setting: the **stress-is-enhancing mindset**.

Most people assume that stress is purely harmful — something to avoid or minimise. But research shows that how you think about stress changes what it does to you. When people believe that stress can sharpen focus, build resilience, and fuel performance, they actually experience better outcomes under pressure. Their bodies respond differently, and they recover faster.

## Motivation

When you are teaching adult learners, motivation becomes especially important. Adults choose to be in your training — or at least, they've agreed to be there — and they bring their own goals, pressures, and prior experiences. Unlike school-age students, you can't rely on external structures (grades, attendance registers, parental involvement) to keep them engaged.

Motivation is also deeply tied to how learners perceive effort. If a task feels pointlessly difficult, motivation drops. If it feels trivially easy and boredom sets in, motivation drops. If it feels achievable and worthwhile, motivation rises. Your job as a trainer is to hit that balance — challenging enough to be meaningful, supported enough to be possible.

## A positive and safe environment

In the case study above, the learner might have put up their hand and asked for help if they believed that would lead to a positive outcome — but they didn't. This is a common pattern. When an environment feels unsafe in some way, learners behave as though they have a fixed mindset, even if they don't actually hold one. They avoid asking questions, hide confusion, and stop taking risks.

A safe learning environment is not about being soft or lowering standards. It is about creating conditions where learners feel they can be honest about what they don't yet understand, without facing judgement or embarrassment.
