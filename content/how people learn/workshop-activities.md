# Workshop Activities: How People Learn

These activities are designed for a facilitator running a live workshop based on this chapter. Each activity includes timing, group size, materials needed, and step-by-step instructions.

## Activity 1: The Illusion of Competence

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Individual, then pairs
**Materials:** None

### Instructions

1. Give participants two minutes to silently read a short passage — either a key paragraph from the chapter or a content-rich excerpt relevant to your context. Tell them to read carefully but not to take notes.
2. After two minutes, ask them to put the passage face-down (or close it).
3. Give each person one minute to write down everything they can remember from the passage.
4. Now have them pair up and compare: what did they each capture? What did they miss? Have them look back at the passage and identify gaps.
5. Debrief as a whole group. Ask: "How confident were you that you understood the passage while reading it? How did that compare to what you could actually recall?" Draw the connection to the illusion of competence — recognition feels like knowledge, but recall tells the real story.

### Facilitator notes

- The passage should be dense enough that no one captures everything, but short enough to read in two minutes (roughly 150–200 words works well).
- Some participants will feel uncomfortable about gaps in their recall. Frame this positively: the gap itself is the learning moment. The act of trying to recall just strengthened their memory.
- If time is short, skip the pair discussion and go straight from individual recall to the whole-group debrief.

## Activity 2: Bloom's Taxonomy Remix

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** Sticky notes (three colours if possible), markers, flip chart or wall space

### Instructions

1. Explain the setup: each group will take a single concept from their own training context and write three questions about it on sticky notes — one factual recall question, one connection question ("How does this relate to...?"), and one application question ("How would you use this in...?").
2. Give groups five minutes to choose their concept and write their three questions (one per sticky note, colour-coded if possible: e.g. yellow for recall, green for connection, pink for application).
3. Each group sticks their questions on the wall. Do a brief gallery walk — everyone reads the other groups' questions (three minutes).
4. Bring the group back together. Ask: "Which type of question was hardest to write? Which would tell you the most about whether a learner has the capabilities you want them to have by the end of your course?" Discuss as a whole group.
5. Then pose the challenge: "What if you started with the application question instead of the recall question? How would that change the learning experience?" Let groups discuss briefly before sharing out.

### Facilitator notes

- If groups struggle to pick a concept, suggest they use something simple from their domain — a key term, a procedure, a principle.
- The most common insight is that application questions are harder to write but far more revealing. Highlight this.
- Watch for groups that write three questions at the same cognitive level (e.g. three recall questions worded differently). Gently redirect them to aim for genuinely different levels.
- If you don't have sticky notes, this works fine on paper — just have groups present their questions verbally.

## Activity 3: Spaced Repetition Course Audit

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Pairs
**Materials:** Participants should have a course outline, syllabus, or programme schedule (their own or a sample you provide)

### Instructions

1. Ask each pair to pick one course outline to work with (one of theirs, or provide a sample for those who don't have one ready).
2. Have them identify the three most important concepts in that course.
3. For each concept, they mark every point in the programme where learners encounter it — lectures, exercises, discussions, assessments, anything.
4. After seven minutes of mapping, ask pairs to answer: How many encounters does each concept get? Are encounters spaced out across the programme, or clustered in a single session? Are there any concepts that appear only once?
5. For any concept that appears only once or is clustered, pairs brainstorm one concrete way to bring it back later — a recap question, a brief application exercise, a connection to a later topic.
6. Debrief: invite two or three pairs to share what they found. Ask the whole group: "What patterns do you notice? Is there a common gap?"

### Facilitator notes

- If participants don't have their own course outlines, prepare a simple sample syllabus (any subject). The exercise works regardless of domain.
- Pairs often discover that their most important concepts get surprisingly few encounters. This is the key insight — let it land before jumping to solutions.
- Some participants will feel defensive about their course design. Keep the tone diagnostic, not judgemental: "This isn't about what's wrong — it's about spotting opportunities."
- For a shorter version (15 minutes), limit the audit to one concept instead of three.

## Activity 4: Learning Styles Myth-Busting Debate

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Whole group, split into two sides
**Materials:** None

### Instructions

1. Announce that you're running a quick structured debate. Split the room into two sides:
   - **Side A** argues: "Educators should tailor instruction to each learner's preferred learning style."
   - **Side B** argues: "Learning styles are a myth and tailoring to them wastes time."
2. Give each side three minutes to huddle and prepare their strongest arguments (two or three points).
3. Each side presents their case (two minutes per side). No interruptions.
4. Open the floor for one round of rebuttals (one minute per side).
5. Now reveal (or remind the group of) the research: Pashler et al. (2008) found no evidence that matching instruction to self-reported learning styles improves outcomes. But mixing media *does* help — because processing the same concept in different ways strengthens understanding.
6. Debrief: "What's the practical takeaway? If learning styles don't hold up, what should we do instead?" Guide the group toward the conclusion that variety in delivery helps everyone, not because people are different "types" but because multiple formats force deeper processing.

### Facilitator notes

- Side A will often argue passionately — many people genuinely believe in learning styles. That's fine. The point isn't to embarrass anyone; it's to surface assumptions and test them against evidence.
- If the group is small (fewer than eight), run it as a think-pair-share instead: individuals take a position, discuss with a partner, then share with the group.
- Some participants may push back on the research. Acknowledge that preferences are real (people do prefer certain media), but preference and effectiveness aren't the same thing. The driving analogy from the chapter works well here: "If you're an auditory learner, how should you learn to drive?"

## Activity 5: Chunking Challenge

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** One handout per group (see below) or a projected slide

### Instructions

1. Show groups a list of 12–15 items related to a single topic. This could be steps in a process, features of a system, principles from a framework — use something relevant to your participants' context, or use a generic example (e.g. 15 tips for effective presentations, 12 steps in a research workflow).
2. Give each group three minutes to organise the items into 3–4 meaningful groups and give each group a label.
3. Have groups share their groupings. Expect different groups to chunk the same items differently — that's the point.
4. Discuss: "Why did different groups organise these differently? Which grouping would be easiest for a novice to work with?" Connect this to chunking: experts chunk automatically because they see patterns; novices need help seeing the structure.
5. Ask each group to think about their own course content: "Where do you present a long list or sequence to learners? Could you chunk it to make it easier to process?"

### Facilitator notes

- Prepare the item list in advance. Aim for items that could plausibly be grouped in multiple ways — this generates better discussion than items with one obvious structure.
- If you want a quick ready-made example: use the list of spaced-repetition and recall strategies from the chapter (practice quizzes, flash cards, revisiting topics, etc.) plus a few additional study techniques.
- The key insight is that chunking isn't just about memory tricks — it reflects how experts organise knowledge. When you structure content for learners, you're giving them expert-level chunks before they've built their own.

## Activity 6: Zone of Proximal Development Role-Play

**Time:** 25 minutes
**Group size:** Groups of 3
**Materials:** Scenario cards (see below)

### Instructions

1. Form groups of three. In each group, assign roles: **Learner**, **Educator**, **Observer**.
2. Give each group a scenario card describing a learning situation where the challenge level is wrong — either too easy or too hard. Examples:
   - "A learner in a data analysis workshop already knows basic statistics but is being walked through what an average is, step by step."
   - "A learner with no programming background is asked to debug a multi-file application on day one of an introductory course."
   - "A learner who has used spreadsheets casually is asked to build a pivot table with no guidance or examples."
3. The Learner acts out how a real learner might respond (boredom, frustration, disengagement, asking unrelated questions). The Educator tries to adjust — either raising the challenge or providing more support to bring the task into the zone of proximal development. They have four minutes to play through the scenario.
4. The Observer takes notes on what the Educator tried and whether it seemed to work.
5. After four minutes, the Observer shares their observations with the group (two minutes).
6. Rotate roles and repeat with a new scenario card (if time allows, do two rounds).
7. Whole-group debrief: "What strategies did Educators use to adjust? What signals told you the challenge level was off?" Collect strategies on a flip chart or whiteboard.

### Facilitator notes

- Write 4–6 scenario cards in advance. Mix "too easy" and "too hard" scenarios. Tailor them to your audience's domain if possible.
- The role-play doesn't need to be polished — even awkward attempts surface useful insights. Encourage groups to just try it rather than plan it perfectly.
- Common Educator strategies that emerge: breaking the task into smaller steps, asking diagnostic questions, providing a worked example, removing scaffolding, connecting to what the learner already knows. Name these explicitly in the debrief.
- If participants are uncomfortable with role-play, reframe it as a "talk-through": instead of acting it out, the group discusses what the Learner would likely do and what the Educator could try. This still works.
