# Research: Chunking

## Key findings

### Miller (1956): The Magical Number Seven

George A. Miller's 1956 paper "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two" is one of the most cited papers in psychology. Miller showed that short-term memory capacity is limited not by bits of information but by *chunks* — meaningful units. A chunk is the largest meaningful unit in presented material that a person recognises.

- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Magical_Number_Seven,_Plus_or_Minus_Two

### Cowan (2001): Revised to ~4 chunks

Nelson Cowan's 2001 paper "The Magical Number 4 in Short-Term Memory" argued that Miller's 7±2 was a rough estimate, and that the true capacity limit of working memory is closer to 4 (±1) chunks. This has become the modern consensus.

- Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11515286/
- Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2864034/

### How chunking works

Chunking is the process of grouping individual pieces of information into larger meaningful units. Examples:
- A phone number like 0823456789 is hard to remember as 10 digits, but easier as 082-345-6789 (3 chunks)
- A novice learning Morse code hears each dit-dah as a separate chunk; an expert hears whole words and phrases as single chunks
- Chess experts (Chase & Simon, 1973) remember board positions by recognising familiar patterns (chunks), not individual pieces

### Relevance to learning

- Chunking explains why experts can hold more in working memory than novices — they have larger chunks, not more capacity
- Instructional design should present new information in manageable chunks
- Building expertise involves building larger and richer chunks over time
