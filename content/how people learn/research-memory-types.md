# Research: Short-Term and Long-Term Memory

## Key findings

### Short-term/working memory vs long-term memory

- **Working memory** holds and manipulates a small amount of information (about 4 chunks — Cowan, 2001) for brief periods. It's where active thinking happens.
- **Long-term memory** has effectively unlimited capacity and stores information for days to a lifetime, but requires encoding and consolidation.

### Why cramming fails for long-term retention

1. **Cramming boosts short-term retrieval but not long-term encoding.** Massed study (cramming) increases instantaneous retrieval strength but reduces long-term memory encoding. Information crammed can disappear within days or even hours.

2. **The spacing effect advantage grows over time.** In one study, distributed study groups outperformed massed study groups by 8.9% on initial tests, but by 33.4% on retests four days later. Spacing can be up to twice as effective for long-term encoding.
   - Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8759977/

3. **Sleep is critical for consolidation.** During sleep, the brain replays and rehearses learned information, strengthening neural connections. Cramming often involves sleep deprivation, which disrupts this consolidation process.
   - Source: https://engagingmindsonline.com/blog-posts/why-spaced-study-is-far-more-effective-than-cramming

4. **Neuroscience mechanism.** Brain synapses encode memories more effectively when activated briefly and repeatedly rather than continuously. Prolonged cramming engages only one set of synapses; spaced repetition engages multiple sets.

### Why "understood in the moment" doesn't guarantee retention

Understanding something in the moment means it's in working memory. For that understanding to persist, it needs to be:
- **Encoded** into long-term memory (through effortful processing, connection to prior knowledge)
- **Consolidated** (through sleep, through revisiting)
- **Retrieved** periodically (use it or lose it — the testing effect)

This is why a learner can perfectly understand a concept during a lecture, yet fail to recall it a week later. Understanding and remembering are related but distinct cognitive processes.
