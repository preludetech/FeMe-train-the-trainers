# Designing Content

Questions:
- When you last designed a training session, did you start with the content or the outcomes?
- Have you ever sat through a course that tried to cover too much and ended up teaching too little?

## Start with the end in mind

It is tempting to dive straight into creating slides, writing notes, or choosing activities. But effective training design works backwards. This approach — often called backward design (Wiggins & McTighe, 2005) — means you start by defining what your learners should know or be able to do by the end, then figure out how you will know they have got there, and only then choose the content and activities that will get them to that point.

This matters because training without clear goals tends to drift. You add a topic because it is interesting, then another because it feels related, and before long you have a packed schedule that covers a lot but teaches very little. Clear learning objectives act as a filter: if something does not serve an objective, it probably does not belong.

| REMEMBER | _Design backwards: start with what learners should be able to do, then decide how you will check whether they got there, and only then choose content and activities. If something does not serve an objective, it probably does not belong._ |
| :---- | :---- |

Think of your training as a set of modules, each one focused on a coherent piece of the bigger picture. Every module should have its own objectives, and those objectives should connect logically to the overall goals of the programme. This modularity also makes your content easier to update, rearrange, or share with other trainers.

### Activity: Design backwards from one goal

Pick one training session you are planning or have delivered before. Write down a single sentence describing what learners should be able to do by the end of that session. Then, working backwards, jot down how you would check whether they got there (an exercise, a question, a task) and only after that, list the content and activities you would use. Compare this to how you originally planned the session — did you start from a different place?

### How activities and assessments connect to objectives

Once you have your objectives, every activity and every assessment should map back to at least one of them. If an objective says learners will be able to "apply a framework to a new scenario", then a lecture alone will not get them there — they need a hands-on task. If the assessment is a multiple-choice quiz, it is testing recognition, not application. We cover assessments in detail in the next section, but the key idea here is alignment: objectives, activities, and assessments should all point in the same direction.

This alignment also helps prevent scope creep. When someone suggests adding a topic, you can ask a simple question: which objective does this serve? If the answer is "none of them", you have your answer.

| REMEMBER | _Use your objectives as a filter for every design decision. When someone suggests adding a topic or activity, ask: "Which objective does this serve?" If the answer is none, leave it out._ |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Map your activities to your objectives

Take a training session you have designed (or are designing) and list your objectives in one column and your planned activities in another. Draw lines connecting each activity to the objective it serves. Are there objectives with no matching activity? Are there activities that do not connect to any objective? Use this map to spot gaps and trim anything that is not pulling its weight.

## Writing strong learning objectives

A learning objective should describe something observable and measurable. "Understand cognitive load" is vague — how would you know if someone understands it? Compare that with "explain how cognitive load affects learning in a short training session" or "redesign a slide deck to reduce unnecessary cognitive load". These are concrete. You can watch someone do them, and you can tell whether they have done them well.

Bloom's revised taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) offers a useful way to think about the depth of thinking you are asking for. It arranges cognitive skills in a hierarchy: remember, understand, apply, analyse, evaluate, create. Each level generally involves more complex thinking than the ones below it, though in practice the boundaries overlap — learners can engage with higher-level tasks before fully mastering lower ones. When you write objectives, choose verbs that match the level of thinking that actually matters for your learners. If they need to apply a skill in their own context, an objective pitched at "remember" or "define" is not ambitious enough.

| REMEMBER | _A strong learning objective describes something you can observe and measure. Replace vague goals like "understand" with concrete actions — "explain", "redesign", "evaluate" — that tell you whether a learner has actually got there._ |
| :---- | :---- |

Here are a few examples of weak objectives rewritten as strong ones:

- **Weak:** "Understand feedback techniques." **Strong:** "Give constructive feedback on a peer's lesson plan using a provided rubric."
- **Weak:** "Know about open educational resources." **Strong:** "Evaluate an existing OER for quality and relevance to a specific training objective."
- **Weak:** "Learn about scaffolding." **Strong:** "Sequence a set of activities so that each one builds on the skills practised in the previous one."

### Activity: Rewrite your own objectives

Look at the learning objectives for a session you are working on. For each one, ask: could I watch a learner do this? Would I know whether they did it well? If the answer is no, rewrite the objective using a concrete verb — "explain", "compare", "design", "apply" — and add enough detail that someone else could read it and know exactly what success looks like. If you do not have objectives written yet, draft three for your next session.

## Constructive alignment

Constructive alignment (Biggs, 1996) is the idea that your objectives, activities, and assessments should all reinforce each other. When they are aligned, everything in your training is pulling in the same direction. When they are not, learners get mixed signals about what matters.

Here is a simple example. Suppose your objective is: "Learners will be able to apply the backward design framework to plan a short training session."

- **Aligned activity:** Learners work in pairs to draft a session plan starting from objectives, then designing an assessment, then choosing activities.
- **Aligned assessment:** Each pair presents their plan and explains how their activities connect to their objectives.
- **Misaligned activity:** A forty-minute lecture on the history of backward design.
- **Partial assessment:** A quiz asking learners to define "backward design" in their own words.

The lecture and the quiz are not wrong — checking that learners can explain a concept is often a reasonable stepping stone toward applying it. But on their own, they do not go far enough. If the objective asks learners to apply the framework, they eventually need an activity and an assessment that give them a chance to do exactly that. A definition quiz might confirm understanding, but it does not tell you whether someone can actually use the framework to plan a session.

| REMEMBER | _If your objective says learners will "apply" or "design" something, a lecture and a definition quiz are not enough. Your activities and assessments need to give learners a chance to actually do the thing the objective describes._ |
| :---- | :---- |

This idea connects directly to the assessment section that follows. There we go deeper into how to design assessments that genuinely test what your objectives claim to teach.

### Activity: Spot the misalignment

Choose one objective from your own training and write down the activity and assessment you currently use (or plan to use) for it. Now check: does the activity give learners a chance to practise what the objective describes? Does the assessment actually test it? If your objective uses a verb like "apply", "design", or "evaluate", but your activity is a lecture and your assessment is a definition question, identify one change you could make to bring them into alignment.

## Sequencing and scaffolding

The order in which you teach things matters more than most people realise. If you introduce a concept before learners have the foundation to make sense of it, they will struggle — not because the material is too hard, but because it arrived too early.

Good sequencing generally means building from simpler to more complex, making sure prerequisite knowledge is in place before it is needed. But that is not the only approach that works. Sometimes it is useful to throw learners at a challenge that is deliberately beyond their current reach, let them struggle with it, and then circle back to fill in the gaps they just discovered. Research on productive failure (Kapur, 2008) shows that this kind of structured struggle can deepen learning — the effort creates a felt need for the knowledge that follows, so learners engage with it more actively than they would if it had arrived as an abstract prerequisite. The key is framing: learners need to know that the struggle is intentional and that not solving the problem on the first attempt is part of the process, not a sign of failure.

Either way, the underlying principle is the same. This connects back to the zone of proximal development covered in the "How People Learn" section: learners grow when they are stretched just beyond what they can already do. The difference is in how you reveal that stretch — gradually through careful sequencing, or deliberately through a well-chosen challenge that exposes what they still need to learn.

Scaffolding is the practical side of this. It means providing temporary support structures that help learners bridge the gap between what they know and what you are asking them to do. A worked example before an independent task is scaffolding. A simplified version of a problem before the full one is scaffolding. A quick recap of yesterday's key ideas before introducing today's new concept is scaffolding. As learners build competence, you gradually remove those supports.

When you are planning your sequence, map out the topics you want to cover and identify the dependencies between them. Which concepts rely on others? What needs to come first? Where might learners need a bridge — a recap, a warm-up exercise, or a simpler version of a task — before they are ready for the next step? Even a rough dependency map on the back of a napkin can save you from discovering mid-session that your learners are lost because you skipped a building block.

| REMEMBER | _When learners struggle, the problem is often not the difficulty of the material but the order it arrived in. Map the dependencies between your topics and make sure each step has the foundation it needs — whether you build up gradually or use a deliberate challenge to expose gaps first._ |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Draw a dependency map

Pick a module or session from your training. List the key concepts or skills you plan to cover, then draw arrows showing which ones depend on others. Are there concepts that need a foundation you have not built yet? Are there places where a recap or a warm-up task would help bridge the gap? Rearrange your sequence if needed, and note one place where you could add scaffolding — a worked example, a simplified version of a task, or a quick review — to help learners make the transition.

### Activity: Gradual build-up or productive failure?

Think of a specific concept or skill in your training that learners tend to find difficult. Consider two ways you could teach it: (1) breaking it into smaller steps and building up gradually, or (2) giving learners a challenge that requires the concept before you have taught it, letting them struggle, and then explaining. Write a brief plan for each approach. Which feels like a better fit for your learners and your context? What would you need to put in place — framing, time, support — to make the productive failure approach work?

## Pitching content at the right level

Your readers will be designing training for varied audiences, so pitching content at the right level is a skill worth developing deliberately. Pitch too low and capable learners disengage. Pitch too high and struggling learners shut down.

The starting point is finding out what your learners already know. A quick needs assessment before you design — even something as simple as a short survey or a few conversation questions — gives you a realistic picture instead of assumptions. You do not need a formal diagnostic test; you need enough information to make informed design choices.

Once you know your audience, consider building in flexibility. Optional "deep dive" sections let advanced learners go further without holding everyone else back. Being explicit about what prior knowledge you are assuming — stating it at the start of a module — helps learners self-assess whether they are ready, and helps you avoid the awkward moment where half the room is confused and the other half is bored.

### Activity: Write three questions to find out what your learners know

Think about the next training session you are planning. Write three short questions you could ask your learners beforehand — in a survey, an email, or at the start of the session — to find out what they already know about the topic. Aim for questions that reveal genuine gaps or strengths, not just yes/no familiarity. For example, instead of "Have you used version control before?", try "Describe the last time you used version control — what did you do and what was tricky?"

## Less is more

One of the hardest lessons in training design is that cutting content often improves learning. This goes beyond avoiding scope creep. Cognitive load theory, covered in the "How People Learn" section, explains why: working memory is limited, and every additional piece of information competes for attention. If something does not directly serve a learning objective, it is a candidate for removal — even if it is genuinely interesting.

This does not mean your training should be thin. It means it should be focused. A session that covers three concepts well, with time for practice and questions, will produce more learning than one that races through ten concepts with no time to absorb any of them.

| REMEMBER | _Cutting content often improves learning. A focused session that covers three concepts well beats one that races through ten. Ask "what can I remove?" as often as you ask "what should I add?"_ |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: The cutting exercise

Take the outline or agenda for a session you are working on. For each item — each topic, slide, or activity — ask yourself: which learning objective does this serve? If it does not clearly serve one, mark it. Now look at everything you have marked. Could you remove it without weakening the session? Try removing at least one thing and see whether the session feels tighter and more focused as a result.

## Using and adapting existing content

Not everything needs to be written from scratch. Open educational resources (OER) — freely available teaching materials that you can use, adapt, and redistribute — are a practical starting point for many topics. Published tutorials, videos, case studies, and other materials can save you significant design time while giving your learners exposure to different voices and perspectives.

This fits the philosophy of this workbook: open training benefits from shared, reusable resources. You do not need to reinvent every explanation or create every diagram yourself.

### Finding and evaluating resources

When looking for existing content, consider repositories like [OER Commons](https://oercommons.org/), [MIT OpenCourseWare](https://ocw.mit.edu/), or discipline-specific collections in your field. For any resource you find, ask yourself:

- Does it align with my learning objectives?
- Is it pitched at the right level for my learners?
- Is it accurate and up to date?
- Can I adapt it to fit my context, or does it only work as-is?

When you do use or adapt someone else's work, credit them properly. This is both an ethical obligation and a practical one — it models good practice for your own learners and contributes to the culture of open sharing that makes OER possible.

### Activity: Find and evaluate one resource

Pick one topic from your training and spend ten minutes searching for an existing resource you could use or adapt — a tutorial, a video, an exercise, a diagram. Use one of the repositories mentioned above or search within your discipline. Once you find something promising, evaluate it against the four questions listed above. Write a short note: would you use it as-is, adapt it, or skip it? Why?

## Common pitfalls

Even with clear objectives and good intentions, certain mistakes show up repeatedly in training design. Watching for these can save you from learning them the hard way.

**Designing around your interests rather than the objectives.** Your passion for a subtopic is not a reason to include it. Every piece of content should earn its place by serving a learning objective, not by being something you find fascinating.

**Confusing coverage with learning.** Covering a topic is not the same as learners actually learning it. If you have "covered" something but learners cannot do anything with it afterwards, the coverage was not useful. Depth beats breadth.

**Front-loading theory before practice.** Learners lose momentum when they sit through extended theoretical input before getting to do anything with it. They struggle to connect abstract ideas to anything concrete because they have no experience to anchor them to yet. Interleave theory and practice wherever you can.

| REMEMBER | _Covering a topic is not the same as teaching it. If learners cannot do anything with the material afterwards, the coverage was not useful. Interleave theory and practice — do not front-load all the theory before letting learners work with it._ |
| :---- | :---- |

**Designing in isolation.** Getting feedback on your content from a colleague or a sample learner before you deliver it catches problems you cannot see yourself. A fresh pair of eyes will spot assumptions you did not realise you were making.

**Assuming one format fits all.** A wall of text works for some topics, but not all. Mix in visuals, examples, discussions, and hands-on tasks where they serve the objectives. Variety is not decoration — it is a response to the reality that different ideas are best communicated in different ways.

### Activity: Review your own design for pitfalls

Take a session you have designed and review it against the pitfalls listed above. For each one, ask honestly whether it applies. Is there a section driven more by your enthusiasm than by the objectives? A block of theory with no practice alongside it? A topic you "covered" but never gave learners a chance to work with? Pick the most significant issue you find and write down one concrete change you would make to address it.

## Glossary

**Backward design** — A way of planning training that starts with the end: you define what learners should be able to do, then decide how you will check whether they got there, and only then choose the content and activities. The opposite of starting with slides or topics and hoping it all comes together.

**Bloom's revised taxonomy** — A framework that organises thinking skills into six levels — remember, understand, apply, analyse, evaluate, create — roughly from simpler to more complex. It is useful for writing learning objectives that target the right depth of thinking.

**Cognitive load** — The amount of mental effort your working memory is handling at any given moment. When you pile on too much new information at once, learners run out of processing capacity and stop absorbing effectively.

**Cognitive load theory** — The body of research explaining how working memory limits affect learning. The practical upshot: keep things focused, cut what is not essential, and introduce complexity gradually.

**Constructive alignment** — The principle that your learning objectives, activities, and assessments should all reinforce each other. When they are aligned, everything in your training pulls in the same direction.

**Learning objective** — A clear statement of what learners should be able to do after your training. Good objectives are observable and measurable — "explain", "design", "evaluate" rather than vague goals like "understand" or "appreciate".

**Needs assessment** — Any method you use to find out what your learners already know before you design your training. It can be as simple as a short survey or a few questions in conversation — the goal is to replace assumptions with actual information.

**Open educational resources (OER)** — Teaching materials that are freely available for you to use, adapt, and share. They range from full courses to individual diagrams or exercises, and they save you from building everything from scratch.

**Productive failure** — A teaching approach where you deliberately give learners a challenge that is beyond their current ability, let them struggle with it, and then teach the underlying concepts afterwards. The struggle creates a felt need for the knowledge, so learners engage with it more deeply.

**Scaffolding** — Temporary support you provide to help learners tackle something they cannot yet do on their own. Examples include worked examples, simplified versions of a task, or a quick recap of prerequisite ideas. You remove the supports gradually as learners build competence.

**Scope creep** — The tendency for training content to expand beyond what the objectives require. It usually happens one "interesting" addition at a time, until the session is packed but unfocused.

**Sequencing** — The order in which you present topics and activities. Good sequencing ensures that learners have the foundation they need before you build on it — whether that means a careful step-by-step progression or a deliberate challenge followed by explanation.

**Working memory** — The part of your mind that holds and processes new information in the moment. It has a limited capacity, which is why overloading a session with too much content at once makes it harder — not easier — to learn.

**Zone of proximal development** — The space between what a learner can do independently and what they can do with guidance. This is where the most productive learning happens — tasks that stretch learners just beyond their current ability, with enough support to keep them from getting stuck.
