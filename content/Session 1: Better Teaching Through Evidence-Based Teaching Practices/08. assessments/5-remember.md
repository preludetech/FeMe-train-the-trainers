# Assessment

Questions:
- How can assessing your students make your job easier?
- Have you ever taken part in an assessment that you thought was badly designed, pointless, or misleading?

## Assessing like a scientist

What if you used assessments to genuinely understand your learners — where they are, what they're struggling with, and what's working? Instead of treating assessment as a final verdict, you could use it as a measurement tool to adjust your own approach in real time.

Assessments can also guide your learners directly. A well-designed assessment gives someone a clear picture of where to focus their effort and which learning strategies to adjust. In this sense, assessment becomes feedback — not just for you, but for them.

| REMEMBER | _Assessment is a measurement tool, not a verdict. Used well, it tells you what to adjust in your teaching and tells your learners where to focus their effort._ |
| :---- | :---- |

## Signal and noise

In traditional schooling, students often sit a single end-of-year exam. If someone fails, what does that actually tell you? It could mean they didn't study, went blank under stress, used ineffective study methods, lacked motivation, or never understood the material when it was taught months earlier. A single exam result bundles all of these possibilities into one number, making it nearly impossible to know what the learner needs or where the teaching fell short.

Then there are trick questions. If a student gets one wrong, did they misunderstand the material — or did the question mislead them? The signal you get back is muddied by the noise your assessment design introduced.

| REMEMBER | _A single exam result bundles too many variables into one number. If you want to know what a learner actually understands, you need assessments that separate the signal from the noise._ |
| :---- | :---- |

### Ask yourself three things

When designing any assessment, keep these questions front and centre:

1. What are you actually trying to assess?
2. What do you want to know about your students?
3. What do you want them to know about themselves?

When assessments are designed to support learning rather than catch people out, they look very different.

## Assessments designed to support learning

Here are practical principles for designing assessments that genuinely help your learners:

- **Avoid trick questions.** There may occasionally be a reason to include one, but be intentional about it — know exactly what you're testing and why.
- **Assess often.** A single high-stakes test tells you far less than frequent, low-stakes checks spread across the course. Frequent retrieval practice produces better long-term learning than single high-stakes exams — a finding confirmed across hundreds of studies (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006; Adesope et al., 2017).
- **Assess the actual skill.** Test whether someone can do the thing, not just whether they can recite memorised definitions.
- **Be cautious with multiple-choice.** These are easy to mark, but they often test recognition rather than recall — two very different things. When learners see the correct answer among options, they only need to recognise it. Free-recall or short-answer formats require them to produce the answer from memory, which demands deeper processing (Little & Bjork, 2015). Multiple-choice tests still have value for quick formative checks and identifying misconceptions, but they shouldn't be the sole measure of deep understanding.
- **Don't assume mastery from a single correct answer.** Memories fade. A learner who answers correctly once may not retain that knowledge a week later. Spaced repetition — revisiting material at increasing intervals over time — is one of only two learning techniques rated "high utility" in a major review of the evidence (Dunlosky et al., 2013). The spacing effect has been demonstrated in hundreds of studies (Cepeda et al., 2006). Building in spaced review opportunities throughout your training helps learners retain what they've learned.

| REMEMBER | _Frequent, low-stakes assessments tell you far more than a single high-stakes exam — and they strengthen learning in the process. Assess often, and space it out over time._ |

| REMEMBER | _Multiple-choice questions test recognition — spotting the right answer when you see it. If you want to know whether someone can produce the answer from memory, use recall-based formats like short answers or explanations._ |

## Handling perceptions

In academic settings, assessments can carry a bad reputation. They act as judges and gatekeepers — deciding who is worthy and who is not. Standardised testing encourages learners to study for the format rather than for genuine understanding — high-stakes pressure narrows the curriculum and shifts teaching toward test preparation rather than deeper learning (Popham, 2001).

Many of your learners will arrive with these associations. Test anxiety is real — a review of 238 studies found it significantly hurts performance, and the more high-stakes a test feels, the worse the anxiety gets (von der Embse et al., 2018). People who have been burned by traditional assessment systems may feel anxious the moment you mention the word "test" or "assessment". Making your intentions clear up front — explaining that assessments in your training are designed to help, not to rank — goes a long way toward easing that tension. When learners understand the purpose, task, and criteria of an assessment, their anxiety drops and their self-regulated learning improves (Jönsson & Svingby, 2018).

| REMEMBER | _Test anxiety is real and it hurts performance. Be transparent about why you are assessing — when learners understand the purpose is to help them, not rank them, anxiety drops and learning improves._ |
| :---- | :---- |

## Assessments can take many forms

Assessment does not have to mean sitting at a desk with an exam paper. You can assess skills through conversation, role-play, projects, peer teaching, and many other approaches.

Think about a few out-of-the-box ways you could assess your students' skills without it looking like a standard test. Then ask yourself: how many of those could a learner do on their own, to assess their own progress?

Two examples worth considering:

- **Projects.** In software development, the ultimate test is "can you build it?" When students take on projects and struggle, they see their own gaps clearly — no marking rubric required.
- **Teaching others.** When learners teach their peers, they quickly discover where their understanding is shaky. Students ask unexpected questions, and that pressure-tests knowledge in ways a written exam rarely does. Research on this *protégé effect* shows that learners who teach others put in more effort and learn more than those studying only for themselves (Chase et al., 2009) — with a meta-analysis of peer tutoring studies finding a meaningful positive effect on tutors' own achievement (Leung, 2019).

| REMEMBER | _Assessment doesn't have to look like a test. Projects, peer teaching, and conversations can reveal understanding just as well — and they feel less threatening to learners._ |
| :---- | :---- |

## Glossary

**Formative assessment** — An assessment used during the learning process to check understanding and guide next steps, rather than to assign a final grade. The frequent, low-stakes checks described in this chapter are formative assessments.

**High-stakes assessment** — A test or exam where the result carries significant consequences — a pass/fail decision, a grade, or a gatekeeping function. High-stakes assessments tend to increase anxiety and encourage studying for the format and various desperate measures rather than for understanding.

**Low-stakes assessment** — A check on understanding where the result carries little or no consequence for the learner. The purpose is to support learning, not to judge. Quizzes, quick knowledge checks, and informal questions during a session are all examples.

**Meta-analysis** — A study that combines results from many individual studies on the same topic to find overall patterns. When this chapter says "hundreds of studies confirm" something, that conclusion typically comes from a meta-analysis.

**Protégé effect** — The finding that people who teach material to others learn it more deeply themselves. Teaching forces you to organise your knowledge and reveals gaps you might not otherwise notice.

**Recall** — Producing an answer from memory without any cues or options to choose from. Writing a short answer or explaining a concept aloud both require recall. Compare with *recognition*.

**Recognition** — Identifying the correct answer when you see it among options, as in a multiple-choice test. Recognition is easier than recall because the answer is right in front of you — you just need to spot it.

**Retrieval practice** — The act of pulling information out of memory, typically through testing or self-quizzing. Retrieval strengthens memory more effectively than re-reading or re-studying the same material.

**Self-regulated learning** — A learner's ability to plan, monitor, and adjust their own learning strategies. When learners understand what an assessment is for and how they are doing, they get better at directing their own study.

**Signal and noise** — Borrowed from engineering: the *signal* is the useful information an assessment gives you about a learner's understanding; the *noise* is everything else that muddies the picture — stress, trick questions, bad timing, unclear wording.

**Spaced repetition** — Revisiting material at increasing intervals over time rather than cramming it all at once. Spacing out review is one of the most effective techniques for long-term retention.

**Test anxiety** — Anxiety triggered by the prospect of being assessed. It is strongly linked to how high-stakes a test feels, and it measurably hurts performance. Transparency about the purpose of an assessment helps reduce it.
