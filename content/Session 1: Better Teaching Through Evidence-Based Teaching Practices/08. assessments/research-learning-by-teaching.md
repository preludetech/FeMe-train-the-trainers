# Research: Learning by Teaching and the Protege Effect

## Summary

There is strong, converging evidence from cognitive science and educational psychology that teaching others helps learners discover gaps in their own understanding. This phenomenon is sometimes called the **protege effect** or **learning by teaching** (German: *Lernen durch Lehren*, developed by Jean-Pol Martin in the 1980s). The core mechanism is straightforward: when you have to explain something clearly enough for someone else to understand it, you are forced to organise your own thinking, notice where your understanding is incomplete, and fill those gaps.

This is not just folk wisdom. Multiple experimental studies and meta-analyses support the claim.

---

## Key Studies and Findings

### 1. The Original "Expectancy to Teach" Study

**Bargh, J. A., & Schul, Y. (1980).** On the cognitive benefits of teaching. *Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior*, 19(5), 593-604.

- Two groups of undergraduates studied written passages. One group expected to teach the material; the other expected to be tested on it.
- The teaching-expectancy group significantly outperformed the test-expectancy group on recall and recognition.
- Bargh and Schul argued that the cognitive benefits come from three stages: preparation before teaching, the initial presentation, and subsequent interaction (answering questions, giving feedback).

URL: https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/On-the-Cognitive-Benefits-of-Teaching-Bargh-Schul/6062c346f4f39f83424faf7c8e91f20776f029da

### 2. Teachable Agents and the Protege Effect

**Chase, C. C., Chin, D. B., Oppezzo, M. A., & Schwartz, D. L. (2009).** Teachable agents and the protege effect: Increasing the effort towards learning. *Journal of Science Education and Technology*, 18(4), 334-352.

- This Stanford study coined the term **"protege effect."**
- 8th-grade students used "Betty's Brain," a computer-based learning environment where they taught a virtual agent about biology.
- Students who believed they were teaching their agent put in more effort and learned more than students who believed they were learning for themselves.
- The benefits were most pronounced for lower-achieving students.
- A follow-up with 5th graders confirmed the effect and explored its causes.

URL: https://aaalab.stanford.edu/assets/papers/2009/Protege_Effect_Teachable_Agents.pdf
Also: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10956-009-9180-4

---

### 3. Teaching vs. Merely Expecting to Teach

**Fiorella, L., & Mayer, R. E. (2013).** The relative benefits of learning by teaching and teaching expectancy. *Contemporary Educational Psychology*, 38(4), 281-288.

- Ran two experiments to disentangle the effects of *preparing* to teach versus *actually* teaching.
- Experiment 1: Students who prepared to teach performed better on an immediate comprehension test compared to those who prepared for a test, but this advantage faded over time.
- Experiment 2: The strongest and most persistent learning gains occurred when students both prepared to teach *and* actually taught the material (effect size d = 0.56), compared to those who only prepared for a test and then taught (d = 0.22).
- Conclusion: Actually teaching -- not just anticipating it -- produces deeper, longer-lasting learning.

URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0361476X13000209

---

### 4. Knowledge-Building vs. Knowledge-Telling in Peer Tutoring

**Roscoe, R. D., & Chi, M. T. H. (2007).** Understanding tutor learning: Knowledge-building and knowledge-telling in peer tutors' explanations and questions. *Review of Educational Research*, 77(4), 534-574.

- Reviewed the literature on how peer tutors learn through the act of tutoring.
- Identified two modes: **knowledge-building** (where tutors actively reflect on and restructure their own understanding) and **knowledge-telling** (where tutors merely restate what they know without deeper processing).
- Tutors benefit most when they engage in knowledge-building -- monitoring their own comprehension, integrating new information with prior knowledge, and constructing new understanding.
- Important caveat: tutors can fall into a "knowledge-telling bias," simply reciting facts without genuine reflection. The learning benefit is not automatic.

URL: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0034654307309920

**Roscoe, R. D., & Chi, M. T. H. (2008).** Tutor learning: The role of explaining and responding to questions. *Instructional Science*, 36, 321-350.

- Follow-up study confirming that self-monitoring is essential: tutors must actively notice gaps in their own understanding to benefit from the teaching process.

URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-013-9283-4

---

### 5. Meta-Analysis: Peer Tutoring Benefits for Tutors

**Leung, K. C. (2019).** An updated meta-analysis on the effect of peer tutoring on tutors' achievement. *School Psychology International*, 40(2), 200-214.

- Analysed 16 studies on the academic outcomes for tutors (not tutees).
- Found a weighted mean effect size of **0.43** (p < 0.001), indicating a meaningful positive effect on tutors' own learning.
- This is notable because most peer tutoring research focuses on the tutee. This meta-analysis confirms the tutor benefits too.

URL: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0143034318808832

---

### 6. Retrieval Practice as a Mechanism

**Koh, A. W. L., Lee, S. C., & Lim, S. W. H. (2018).** The learning benefits of teaching: A retrieval practice hypothesis. *Applied Cognitive Psychology*, 32(3), 401-410.

- Proposed that much of the benefit of teaching comes from **retrieval practice** -- the act of pulling information from memory in order to explain it.
- This connects learning-by-teaching to the well-established "testing effect," where actively recalling information leads to stronger retention than passive re-study.

URL: https://www.bps.org.uk/research-digest/learning-teaching-others-extremely-effective

## Why Teaching Reveals Knowledge Gaps

The research points to several overlapping mechanisms:

1. **Forced organisation**: To explain something coherently, you must organise it into a logical structure. This process exposes areas where your mental model is incomplete or contradictory.

2. **Self-monitoring / metacognition**: Preparing to teach triggers self-assessment. You ask yourself, "Do I actually understand this well enough to explain it?" -- and you often discover the answer is "not quite."

3. **Retrieval practice**: Explaining from memory (rather than re-reading notes) strengthens recall and highlights what you cannot retrieve.

4. **Audience questions**: When a learner asks a question you cannot answer, that gap becomes immediately and concretely visible.

5. **Sense of responsibility**: The protege effect specifically shows that people try harder when they feel responsible for someone else's learning, not just their own.

---

## Practical Implications for Training Design

- **Peer teaching activities** are not just filler -- they are one of the most effective ways for learners to consolidate and deepen their understanding.
- The benefit is **not automatic**. Learners need to engage in genuine knowledge-building (reflection, restructuring), not just recite facts. Design activities that push learners beyond surface-level repetition.
- Even **preparing** to teach has some benefit, but **actually teaching** produces stronger, more lasting learning.
- These activities are especially effective for **lower-achieving learners**, who may benefit most from the increased effort and motivation the protege effect provides.
