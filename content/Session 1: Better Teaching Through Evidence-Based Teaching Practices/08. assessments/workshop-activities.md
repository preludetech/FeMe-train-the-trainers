# Workshop Activities: Assessment

These activities are designed for a facilitator running a live workshop based on the assessment chapter. Each activity includes timing, group size, materials needed, and step-by-step instructions.

## Activity 1: The Bad Assessment Autopsy

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** None

### Instructions

1. Ask each participant to think of an assessment they experienced — as a learner or as an instructor — that felt unfair, confusing, or pointless. Give them one minute to pick one.
2. In their groups, each person briefly describes their bad assessment (2 minutes each). The group then picks one to dissect together.
3. For the chosen assessment, the group answers three questions on paper or a shared note:
   - What was the assessment supposedly measuring?
   - What was it *actually* measuring? (Stress tolerance? Ability to guess? Memory of trivia?)
   - What one change would have made it more useful?
4. Each group shares their example and their proposed fix with the room (1 minute per group).

### Facilitator notes

This works well as an opening activity because everyone has a strong opinion about bad assessments. It surfaces the signal-vs-noise concept before you formally introduce it. If a group struggles to pick one example, suggest they think about school exams, job interviews, or certification tests. Keep the sharing round tight — one minute per group is enough.

## Activity 2: Signal or Noise?

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Pairs, then whole group

**Materials:** Printed or projected scenario cards (see below)

### Scenario cards

Prepare 4–6 short scenarios on cards or slides. Each describes an assessment situation and a learner who performed poorly. Examples:

- *A learner fails a timed multiple-choice quiz on networking concepts. She knew the material well in yesterday's session but says she panicked when she saw the countdown timer.*
- *A learner gets 3 out of 10 on a short-answer test about data analysis. He recently joined the course and missed the first two sessions.*
- *A learner scores perfectly on a multiple-choice test about project management, but cannot explain any of the concepts when asked.*
- *Two learners give identical wrong answers to a free-text question. The question used terminology that was never introduced in the course.*

### Instructions

1. Give each pair two scenario cards.
2. For each scenario, the pair identifies: what is signal (genuine evidence about understanding) and what is noise (other factors muddying the result)?
3. They then write down one design change to the assessment that would reduce the noise.
4. Bring the whole group together. Go through each scenario and ask pairs to share what they identified. Highlight where pairs disagree — that's where the richest discussion happens.

### Facilitator notes

The goal is for participants to see that poor performance can have many causes, and that assessment design choices either clarify or obscure the picture. If time is short, assign one scenario per pair instead of two. You can create scenarios relevant to your audience's domain — the more specific to their context, the better the discussion.

## Activity 3: Recognition vs. Recall — Live Experiment

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Whole group, then pairs
**Materials:** Two printed handouts (or two slides)

### Instructions

1. Pick a concept the group has already covered in the workshop. Prepare two versions of a question about it:
   - **Version A:** A multiple-choice question with four options (one correct, three plausible).
   - **Version B:** A short-answer question asking the learner to explain the same concept in their own words.
2. Split the room roughly in half. Give one half Version A and the other Version B. Don't let them see each other's version. Give everyone 90 seconds to answer.
3. Collect a quick show of hands: how confident are you in your answer? (Very confident / somewhat / not at all.)
4. Reveal both versions on screen. Ask the Version A group: "How many of you got it right?" Then the Version B group. Compare the results.
5. In pairs, participants discuss: what does a correct answer on each version actually prove? Could someone get the multiple-choice version right without truly understanding the concept?
6. Debrief as a whole group. Draw out the distinction between recognition and recall, and when each format is appropriate.

### Facilitator notes

This works best when the multiple-choice distractors are genuinely plausible — if the wrong answers are obviously wrong, the point falls flat. You'll often see higher scores and higher confidence on the multiple-choice version, which makes the discussion land naturally. If participants push back with "but multiple-choice is easier to mark," acknowledge that — the point isn't that multiple-choice is bad, but that you should know what it's actually testing.

## Activity 4: Assessment Design Sprint

**Time:** 25 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** Large paper or whiteboards, markers

### Instructions

1. Each group picks a specific topic or skill from their own training context. If participants are from different backgrounds, they pick one person's topic and work with that.
2. Set the challenge: design three different ways to assess that topic. At least one must not look like a traditional test (no quizzes, no written exams). Encourage them to think about projects, peer teaching, conversations, role-plays, or practical demonstrations.
3. For each assessment, the group writes down:
   - What the learner does
   - What it reveals about their understanding
   - Whether a learner could use it to self-assess
4. Groups present their three assessments to the room (2 minutes per group). After each presentation, the audience calls out one strength and one potential blind spot.

### Facilitator notes

This is where participants move from analysis to creation. Push groups past their first idea — the first assessment people design is usually a quiz, and the interesting thinking starts with the second and third. If a group is stuck on non-test formats, prompt them: "What would convince you that someone actually understood this, if you couldn't give them a written test?" The peer feedback round is important — it models the kind of constructive assessment culture the chapter advocates.

## Activity 5: Defuse the Anxiety — Role Play

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Pairs
**Materials:** None

### Instructions

1. Set the scene: "You're about to give your learners their first assessment. Some of them look nervous. You have 30 seconds to explain what the assessment is for and why they shouldn't stress about it."
2. In pairs, one person plays the facilitator and the other plays a sceptical learner. The facilitator delivers their 30-second introduction. The learner then responds with one of these reactions (assign or let them choose):
   - "Last time someone said 'don't worry about the test,' it was worth 50% of my grade."
   - "I'm terrible at tests. I always go blank."
   - "Why are we being tested if this isn't graded?"
3. The facilitator responds to the pushback. Then partners swap roles and repeat with a different reaction.
4. Bring the group together. Ask: what worked? What language helped? What made things worse? Collect the best phrases and approaches on a shared board.

### Facilitator notes

This activity builds a practical script participants can take home. The sceptical-learner reactions are deliberately challenging — they represent real responses. Watch for facilitators who over-explain or get defensive; gently redirect them toward simpler, more honest language. The debrief is the most valuable part: capture the phrases that resonated and make sure everyone leaves with at least one they'd actually use. If the group is large, have a few pairs demonstrate for the room before the debrief.
