# Research Notes: Test Anxiety, Assessment Transparency, and Teaching to the Test

## 1. High-Stakes Assessment Causes Test Anxiety

The relationship between high-stakes testing and test anxiety is well-established across decades of research.

**Von der Embse et al. (2018)** conducted a 30-year meta-analytic review synthesising 238 studies. They found that test anxiety was significantly and negatively related to a wide range of educational performance outcomes, including standardised tests, university entrance exams, and GPA. The perceived high-stakes nature or consequences of a test was itself a predictor of higher test anxiety. Test anxiety effects were most pronounced at the middle grades level. Beyond performance, test anxiety is associated with higher disengagement from schoolwork, absenteeism, increased negative self-perceptions, slower academic progress, low self-efficacy, and a greater likelihood of dropping out.
- Source: [Test anxiety effects, predictors, and correlates: A 30-year meta-analytic review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29156362/) (Journal of Affective Disorders, 2018, vol. 227, pp. 483-493)

**Von Haaren-Mack et al. (2024)** conducted an RCT using cortisol biomarkers and found that large cortisol responses during high-stakes exams were associated with worse test performance, potentially introducing a "stress bias" that makes test results a less reliable indicator of actual student learning.
- Source: [The effect of test anxiety on high-stakes exams: evidence from an RCT using biomarkers](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09645292.2024.2391801) (Education Economics, 2024)

**Putwain & Daly (2022)** examined whether test anxiety is associated with performance in high-stakes examinations and found supporting evidence for the negative relationship, with higher test anxiety accounting for approximately 2-15% of the variance in test performance.
- Source: [Test anxiety: Is it associated with performance in high-stakes examinations?](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/03054985.2022.2079616) (Oxford Review of Education, 2022)

**Edutopia** summarises the broader wellbeing effects: anxiety caused by imminent high-stakes tests correlates with poor health behaviours including dysregulated sleep patterns and poor sleep quality. Young students experience panic, irritability, frustration, crying, headaches, and loss of sleep around high-stakes testing periods.
- Source: [The Psychological Toll of High-Stakes Testing](https://www.edutopia.org/article/psychological-toll-high-stakes-testing/) (Edutopia)

**Ofqual / UK Government** commissioned a literature review that found test anxiety does not have a single cause; rather, many intra-personal, inter-personal, and assessment-specific factors can increase or protect against it. The review was prompted by increasing public concern about the impact of test anxiety on student attainment, health, and wellbeing.
- Source: [A review of the literature concerning anxiety for educational assessments](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/5e45825340f0b677bf6eb3ea/A_review_of_the_literature_concerning_anxiety_for_educational_assessment.pdf) (Ofqual, UK Government)

### Nuance

**Theobald, Breitwieser & Brod (2022)** found that test anxiety did not predict exam performance when knowledge level was controlled for (i.e., the "interference hypothesis" was not supported). However, high trait test anxiety *did* predict smaller gains in knowledge over the exam-preparation phase -- meaning anxiety still undermines learning, just at the preparation stage rather than during the exam itself.
- Source: [Test Anxiety Does Not Predict Exam Performance When Knowledge Is Controlled For](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/09567976221119391) (Psychological Science, 2022)


## 2. Explaining Assessment Purpose Up Front Can Reduce Anxiety

Research on assessment transparency supports the claim that communicating purpose, task, and criteria reduces student anxiety.

**Frontiers in Education editorial on assessment transparency** reports that explicit criteria may positively affect student performance, reduce anxiety, and support self-regulated learning strategies. Rubrics in particular clarify expectations, focus student attention, increase perceived control and confidence, and reduce anxiety about completing assessments.
- Source: [Editorial: Transparency in Assessment -- Exploring the Influence of Explicit Assessment Criteria](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2018.00119/full) (Frontiers in Education, 2018)

**Winkelmes and the TILT framework (Transparency in Learning and Teaching):** Developed in 2009, this framework centres on communicating three things to students before they begin work: the **purpose** (skills and knowledge), the **task** (specific steps), and the **criteria** (metrics for success). A national study by the AAC&U identified TILT as an equitable teaching intervention that significantly enhances student success, with greater gains for historically underserved students. TILT's documented impact includes improved confidence, sense of belonging, metacognitive awareness, and persistence.
- Source: [Transparency in Learning and Teaching in Higher Education](https://www.tilthighered.com/about/about-tilt) (TILT Higher Ed)
- Source: [TILT -- Indiana University CITL](https://citl.indiana.edu/teaching-resources/evidence-based/transparency.html)
- Source: [Q&A with Mary-Ann Winkelmes](https://cpe.ky.gov/news/stories/qa-mary-ann-winkelmes.html) (Kentucky Council on Postsecondary Education)

**Frontiers in Education on rubrics and self-regulation** argues that transparency is not "spoon-feeding" but rather supports student self-regulation. When students understand what is expected and why, they can direct their own learning more effectively.
- Source: [Transparency Isn't Spoon-Feeding: How a Transformative Approach to the Use of Explicit Assessment Criteria Can Support Student Self-Regulation](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2018.00069/full) (Frontiers in Education, 2018)

**Instructor strategies to alleviate stress and anxiety (Ballen et al., 2017):** Research on STEM courses identifies several instructor strategies for reducing anxiety, including explaining the purpose and format of assessments, introducing regular low-stakes formative assessments, and creating a supportive classroom atmosphere.
- Source: [Instructor Strategies to Alleviate Stress and Anxiety among College and University STEM Students](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8108494/) (CBE -- Life Sciences Education)


## 3. Standardised Testing Encourages "Teaching to the Test" / Studying for Format

**Popham (2001)** made the foundational distinction between "curriculum-teaching" (teaching the body of knowledge and skills represented by test items -- acceptable) and "item-teaching" (teaching to specific test items or formats -- problematic). Item-teaching inflates scores without increasing genuine understanding, making assessment results misleading.
- Source: [Teaching to the Test?](https://www.ascd.org/el/articles/teaching-to-the-test) (Educational Leadership, ASCD, 2001)

**Curriculum narrowing:** When high-stakes tests drive accountability, teachers reallocate time toward tested content and away from untested subjects and deeper exploration. Shepard (2000) describes this as "instructional inertia" -- teachers under pressure to improve scores narrow their instructional focus to exam content at the expense of broader learning.
- Referenced in: [Teaching to the Test: A Controversial Issue in Quantitative Measurement](https://www.iiisci.org/journal/pdv/sci/pdfs/HEA561DK.pdf)

**Amrein & Berliner (2002)** found that assessment-heavy systems often show improved short-term scores but long-term declines in conceptual retention and creativity.
- Referenced in: [Teaching to the Test: A Controversial Issue in Quantitative Measurement](https://www.iiisci.org/journal/pdv/sci/pdfs/HEA561DK.pdf)

**Reading Rockets / Calkins, Montgomery & Santman (1998)** argue that teaching to the test does not accurately gauge understanding and provides only a narrow scope of information, overlooking important concepts and context necessary for deeper comprehension. Students who memorise vocabulary for a reading test may perform well on that specific assessment but struggle to apply vocabulary in broader contexts.
- Source: [Teach to the Test? Just Say No](https://www.readingrockets.org/topics/curriculum-and-instruction/articles/teach-test-just-say-no) (Reading Rockets)

**UC Davis School of Education** notes that excessive test preparation takes away opportunities for students to practise higher-order skills, solve problems, and think critically.
- Source: [Quit Teaching to the Test](https://education.ucdavis.edu/post/quit-teaching-test) (UC Davis School of Education)

**Britannica ProCon overview** summarises the debate: standardised tests are criticised for encouraging teaching to the test, narrowing curriculum, and failing to measure important skills like creativity and critical thinking, while proponents argue they provide objective, comparable data.
- Source: [Standardized Tests -- Pros and Cons](https://www.britannica.com/procon/standardized-tests-debate) (Britannica)


## Summary of Claims and Evidence Strength

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Sources |
|-------|------------------|-------------|
| High-stakes assessment causes test anxiety | Strong -- supported by meta-analysis of 238 studies and multiple RCTs | Von der Embse et al. (2018); Putwain & Daly (2022); Von Haaren-Mack et al. (2024) |
| Explaining assessment purpose reduces anxiety | Moderate-to-strong -- supported by transparency research and the TILT framework, though more direct experimental studies would strengthen the claim | Winkelmes / TILT; Frontiers in Education (2018); Ballen et al. (2017) |
| Standardised testing encourages teaching to the test | Strong -- well-documented phenomenon with broad consensus in education research | Popham (2001); Shepard (2000); Amrein & Berliner (2002) |
