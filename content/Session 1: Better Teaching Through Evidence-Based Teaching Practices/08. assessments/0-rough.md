## Assessing like a scientist 

What is you used assessments to really understand your learners and where they are at? What if you used it to measure them so you could adjust your own approach? 

What if assessments were designed to help guide your learners, to give them a glimpse into where they might want to put in effort or what learning strategies they might need to adjust? How can assessments be good feedback?

## Signal and noise 

In a standard schooling system it's pretty common practice for a student to have to sit through an end of year exam. 

If they fail the exam it could mean:
- they were lazy and didn't study
- they were so stressed out that they went blank
- they used bad study methods
- they were totally unmotivated
- they didn't understand the material when it was taught 6 months ago...

If you, as an educator, mark someone's end of year exam it's very hard to know what the learner needs. It's hard to see if and how the learning system fell short. 

Another standard practice is trick questions in exams. If a student got a trick question wrong then that might mean that they didn't understand the material, or it could mean that you tricked them. 

When designing assessments then it is critical to keep this in mind:
What are you actually trying to assess? What do you want to know about your students? What do you want them to know about themselves? 

When assessments are designed to support learning instead of catch people out then they get set up in very different ways.

## Assessments designed to support learning 

What are some things you could do or should avoid when making assessments?

- Avoid trick questions. Sometimes there are reasons to include them but be intentional!
- Assess often
- Assess the actual skill (not just memoised words)
- Multiple choice tests are easy to mark but often test recognition more than recall
- Don't assume a person has mastered something because they answered a question right only once. Memories fade and spaced repetition matters
- frequent low-stakes tests help a lot

## Handling perceptions

Assessments, in an academic setting, can have a bad reputation.
They are a judge and a gate, they decide who is worthy and who is not.
They get standardised and so people study to answer questions in standard ways instead of gaining deep understanding. 
If a learner fails an exam then they are often get blame and criticism rather than support. 

It's important to know that assessments can take many forms, and that they can be really good for people. It's also important to know that many people have been burned by standard schooling systems and that they will get stressed out by assessments unless you make your intentions clear up front.

## Assessments can take many forms

You can assess people's skills in a conversation, in role-play, projects and many other situations. 

Try to think of a few out-of-the box ways to assess your student's skills without it looking like a standard assessment? 

Now, how many of those things can a student do on their own to assess their own skills?

- Projects: In software development, the ultimate test is "can you build it?". If you get students to take on projects and they struggle then they are very able to see their own deficiencies. 
- Teaching others: Students can ask really weird questions. This bulletproof's knowledge
