# The Testing Effect: Why Frequent, Low-Stakes Tests Beat High-Stakes Exams

## What is the testing effect?

The **testing effect** (also called the **retrieval practice effect**) is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology. It refers to the substantial gains in long-term retention that result from actively retrieving information from memory, compared to passively re-reading or re-studying material.

The core insight: the act of *trying to remember* something strengthens memory far more than simply looking at the material again. A test is not just a measurement tool -- it is a learning event.

## Key research findings

### The foundational studies: Roediger & Karpicke (2006)

The landmark research by Henry Roediger and Jeffrey Karpicke demonstrated that students who took practice retrieval tests retained significantly more material than those who simply re-studied.

- Students who took brief retrieval tests before a high-stakes test remembered **60%** of material, while those who only studied remembered **40%**.
- Re-studying produced better results on an *immediate* test, but retrieval practice produced significantly better results after a **2-day** and **1-week** delay -- the crossover point where testing wins.
- This finding is counterintuitive: students often *feel* like re-reading is more effective, even when testing produces better long-term outcomes.

**Sources:**
- Roediger, H. L., & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). Test-enhanced learning: Taking memory tests improves long-term retention. *Psychological Science, 17*(3), 249-255. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16507066/) | [Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x)
- Roediger, H. L., & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). The power of testing memory: Basic research and implications for educational practice. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 1*(3), 181-210. [PDF](http://psychnet.wustl.edu/memory/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/Roediger-Karpicke-2006_PPS.pdf)

### Meta-analysis: Rowland (2014)

Rowland conducted a meta-analytic review examining dozens of studies comparing testing to restudying. The analysis found a **medium-to-large effect size (g = 0.50)** in favour of testing over restudying across 159 comparisons.

Key moderators:
- Initial *recall* tests yielded larger benefits than recognition tests (i.e., effortful retrieval matters more).
- The effect held across a wide variety of materials and contexts.

**Source:**
- Rowland, C. A. (2014). The effect of testing versus restudy on retention: A meta-analytic review of the testing effect. *Psychological Bulletin, 140*(6), 1432-1463. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25150680/)

### Meta-analysis: Adesope, Trevisan & Sundararajan (2017)

This large-scale meta-analysis examined **217 independent studies** and found consistent, strong support for the testing effect.

- Practice testing vs. all other conditions: **g = 0.61**
- Practice testing vs. restudying alone: **g = 0.51**
- Practice testing vs. no activity or filler: **g = 0.93**
- The effect works equally well in real classrooms as in laboratory settings.
- Benefits are stronger when the interval between study and test exceeds one day.
- Secondary students showed the highest effect sizes, but the effect held for all age groups.

**Source:**
- Adesope, O. O., Trevisan, D. A., & Sundararajan, N. (2017). Rethinking the use of tests: A meta-analysis of practice testing. *Review of Educational Research, 87*(3), 659-701. [Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0034654316689306) | [The Learning Scientists summary](https://www.learningscientists.org/blog/2017/2/9-1)

### Classroom evidence: McDaniel, Roediger, Agarwal and colleagues (2011)

These researchers took the testing effect out of the lab and into real classrooms:

- In **middle school science** (8th grade), low-stakes multiple-choice quizzes with feedback produced **13-25% gains** on unit exams compared to material that was not quizzed.
- In **middle school social studies** (6th grade), quizzed content outperformed non-quizzed content on both chapter exams and end-of-semester exams.
- In **college settings**, facts tested via weekly in-class quizzes were remembered better on course exams than facts that were merely re-read.

[PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22082095/) | [PDF](https://pdf.retrievalpractice.org/guide/Roediger_Agarwal_etal_2011_JEPA.pdf)

## Why does the testing effect work?

Several mechanisms have been proposed:

1. **Effortful retrieval strengthens memory traces.** The harder you work to retrieve something, the stronger the resulting memory becomes.
2. **Retrieval creates additional and different memory pathways** compared to re-reading, making the information accessible through more routes.
3. **Testing identifies gaps** in knowledge, letting learners focus effort where it is most needed.
4. **Retrieval practice mimics real-world use.** In most situations outside of studying, people need to *produce* knowledge, not simply recognise it.

## Low-stakes vs. high-stakes: what the research says

The research strongly favours **frequent, low-stakes testing** over infrequent, high-stakes exams:

- **Better learning outcomes.** Students who took all quizzes in a course performed significantly better than those who took none, and this held for high-, middle-, and low-performing students.
- **Reduced anxiety.** Frequent low-stakes assessments decrease overall student anxiety because no single test is a make-or-break event. High-stakes exams, by contrast, can trigger test anxiety and stereotype threat that *impair* performance.
- **More retrieval opportunities.** Each low-stakes quiz is another chance to strengthen memory through retrieval, multiplying the learning benefit across the course.
- **Better calibration.** Frequent testing helps students (and instructors) identify what they do and do not know, enabling timely corrective action rather than discovering gaps only at the end.

## Practical implications for training design

Based on this body of evidence, trainers and instructional designers should:

1. **Build in frequent retrieval opportunities** -- short quizzes, reflection prompts, practice problems -- throughout a training programme, not just at the end.
2. **Keep the stakes low.** The learning benefit comes from the act of retrieval, not from the grade. Low-stakes or no-stakes formats maximise learning while minimising anxiety.
3. **Space the practice out.** Benefits are strongest when retrieval is spaced over time rather than massed in a single session.
4. **Provide feedback** after retrieval attempts, though even retrieval without feedback produces meaningful benefits.
5. **Use a mix of question formats.** Both multiple-choice and short-answer questions produce the effect, with short-answer (recall) questions being particularly effective for deeper processing.
6. **Do not rely on a single final assessment** as the only evaluation point. Distribute assessment throughout the learning journey.

## Summary

The testing effect is not a fringe finding. It is supported by over a century of research, multiple large-scale meta-analyses, and real-world classroom studies. The evidence is clear: **frequent, low-stakes retrieval practice produces better long-term learning than repeated studying or single high-stakes exams**. This is one of the most practical, evidence-based strategies available to anyone designing training or education.
