# Research: Multiple-Choice Tests and Recognition vs. Recall

## The Core Claim

Multiple-choice tests primarily test **recognition memory** rather than **recall memory**. This is a well-established finding in cognitive psychology and educational research.

## What Are Recognition and Recall?

- **Recognition** is identifying previously encountered information when it is presented to you again. You see an item and judge whether it is familiar. It involves verifying familiarity.
- **Recall** is retrieving information from memory without being presented with it. You must actively search memory and generate the answer yourself. It involves two steps: generating a candidate answer and then evaluating whether it seems correct.

Recognition is generally easier than recall because the answer is already present -- you just need to identify it among alternatives. Recall demands more from the retrieval system because nothing is provided; you must produce the answer from scratch.

> "Recognition memory tests resemble multiple-choice exams, where the answer is present but must be identified from false lures, whereas a recall test resembles a fill-in-the-blank exam, where the subject must retrieve the learned information from long-term memory in order to generate the answer."
> -- [Lumen Learning: Retrieval, Introduction to Psychology](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/waymaker-psychology/chapter/reading-retrieval/)

## Key Research Findings

### 1. MC questions rely on recognition, not generative retrieval

When a learner takes a multiple-choice test, they scan the provided options and identify which one "looks right." This is fundamentally a recognition task. Free-recall or short-answer formats require the learner to generate the answer, engaging deeper retrieval processes.

- **Source:** [Lumen Learning -- Retrieval (Introduction to Psychology)](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/waymaker-psychology/chapter/reading-retrieval/)
- **Source:** [PsychCentral -- Recognition vs Recall](https://psychcentral.com/blog/always-learning/2010/01/recognition-vs-recall)

### 2. Recognition requires less cognitive effort and brain activity

Research from the University of Alberta found that recognition memory requires less brain activity than recall. The retrieval demands differ substantially: recall requires active search, while recognition relies on familiarity signals.

- **Source:** [Cabeza et al. -- Recognition Memory Requires Less Brain Activity Than Recall (University of Alberta)](https://web.psych.ualberta.ca/~varn/bc/Cabeza.html)

### 3. Students who fail short-answer items can often answer the same items in MC format

Research comparing short-answer and multiple-choice performance on the same content shows that students who cannot produce an answer from memory can frequently recognise the correct answer when options are provided. This demonstrates the gap between what learners can recognise and what they can actually recall.

- **Source:** [Sally Jordan -- Multiple choice vs short answer questions (Open University)](https://www.open.ac.uk/blogs/SallyJordan/?p=809)
- **Source:** [The Effortful Educator -- Recall or Recognition Questions When Retrieving?](https://theeffortfuleducator.com/2019/12/04/rorqwr/)

### 4. MC tests produce smaller "testing effects" than recall-based tests

The testing effect is the finding that taking a test on material improves later memory for that material. Multiple-choice tests produce this effect, but to a smaller degree than recall-based tests (short answer, cued recall). This is attributed to the shallower processing that recognition requires compared to recall.

- **Source:** Little, J. L. & Bjork, E. L. (2015). Optimizing multiple-choice tests as tools for learning. *Memory & Cognition, 43*, 14-26. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25123774/) | [Bjork Lab PDF](https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2017/01/LittleBjorkMC2014.pdf)
- **Source:** [The role of retrieval in answering multiple-choice questions (PubMed)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30113206/)

### 5. MC testing can reinforce incorrect information through lure exposure

Because MC questions present wrong answers alongside the correct one, learners can inadvertently encode the lures. Roediger and Marsh (2005) found that MC testing increased the intrusion of lure answers on later tests, even when subjects were warned not to guess.

- **Source:** Roediger, H. L. & Marsh, E. J. (2005). The positive and negative consequences of multiple-choice testing. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition.* [PDF (Washington University)](http://psychnet.wustl.edu/memory/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/Roediger-Marsh-2005_JEPLMC.pdf)
- **Source:** Marsh, E. J., Roediger, H. L., Bjork, R. A., & Bjork, E. L. (2007). The memorial consequences of multiple-choice testing. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review.* [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4094137/) | [Bjork Lab PDF](https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/Marsh_Roediger_BjorkBjork2007PBR.pdf)

### 6. MC questions often target lower levels of Bloom's Taxonomy

Traditional MC items tend to sit at the "Remembering" level of the revised Bloom's Taxonomy -- recognising and retrieving information. Research examining how students approach MC questions found that higher-performing students often rely on pattern recognition, while less confident students may engage in more analytical thinking regardless of the question's intended level.

- **Source:** [Examining Bloom's Taxonomy in Multiple Choice Questions: Students' Approach to Questions (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8368900/)
- **Source:** [Bloom's Taxonomy of Cognitive Learning Objectives (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4511057/)

### 7. MC questions also allow test-taking strategies beyond memory

Multiple-choice questions permit strategies that bypass genuine knowledge: eliminating obviously wrong answers, detecting patterns in answer keys, exploiting "longest answer" heuristics, and educated guessing. This means MC performance can overestimate what a learner truly knows.

- **Source:** [The Psychology of Multiple-Choice Tests -- Brainy Behavior](https://www.brainybehavior.com/blog/2008/02/the-psychology-of-multiple-choice-tests/)
- **Source:** [Sally Jordan -- Multiple choice vs short answer questions (Open University)](https://www.open.ac.uk/blogs/SallyJordan/?p=809)

## Important Nuances

### MC tests are not worthless -- they can still promote learning

Smith and Karpicke (2014) found that retrieval practice with MC questions, short-answer questions, and hybrid formats all enhanced retention relative to a study-only control. The benefit comes from the act of retrieval itself, not only from the format.

- **Source:** Smith, M. A. & Karpicke, J. D. (2014). Retrieval practice with short-answer, multiple-choice, and hybrid tests. *Memory, 22*(7), 784-802. [Purdue Learning Lab PDF](https://learninglab.psych.purdue.edu/downloads/2014/2014_Smith_Karpicke_Memory.pdf) | [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24059563/)

### Well-designed MC questions can target higher-order thinking

When MC alternatives are carefully constructed to be competitive and plausible, they can require analysis and application rather than simple recognition. Little and Bjork (2015) showed that MC tests with competitive alternatives enhanced learning for related, untested information as well.

### MC questions are useful for formative assessment and diagnostics

MC questions remain valuable for quick knowledge checks, identifying misconceptions (through diagnostic distractors), and providing immediate feedback at scale. The key is not to rely on them as the sole measure of deep understanding.

## Summary for Instructional Designers

| Assessment format | Primary memory process | Cognitive demand | Best suited for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Multiple-choice | Recognition | Lower | Quick checks, formative feedback, identifying misconceptions |
| Short-answer / fill-in-the-blank | Cued recall | Medium | Testing whether learners can produce key information |
| Essay / open-ended | Free recall + construction | Higher | Assessing deep understanding, synthesis, application |

The claim that "multiple-choice tests often test recognition rather than recall" is **well-supported by research**. This does not make MC questions useless, but it does mean they should not be the only form of assessment if the goal is to verify that learners can independently retrieve and apply knowledge.
