# Workshop Activities: Learning Objectives

These activities are designed for a facilitator running a live workshop based on this chapter. Each activity includes timing, group size, materials needed, and step-by-step instructions.

## Activity 1: The Backwards Brief

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Pairs
**Materials:** None (pen and paper optional)

### Instructions

1. Ask each participant to think of a training session they've designed or delivered — or one they're currently planning. Give them one minute to pick one.
2. In pairs, Partner A describes their training in two sentences. Partner B then asks: *"What should learners be able to do after this session that they couldn't do before?"* Partner A answers without referring to any notes.
3. Partner B's job is to listen and push for specificity. If Partner A says something vague ("They should understand feedback"), Partner B asks: *"What does that look like? What would they actually do differently?"*
4. Swap roles after four minutes.
5. Debrief as a whole group: ask two or three pairs to share what they noticed. Did the objectives come easily, or was it harder than expected to pin down what learners should walk away with?

### Facilitator notes

Most people find this harder than they expect — that's the point. The activity surfaces the gap between "I know my subject" and "I know what my learners need to be able to do." If pairs finish quickly, prompt them to check whether their objectives describe something observable (something you could see a learner do) rather than something internal ("understand," "appreciate").

## Activity 2: Backward Design Relay

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3
**Materials:** Three different-coloured sticky notes (or index cards) per group, markers

### Instructions

1. Each group picks a single, specific training topic — something one member is actually working on. Write it at the top of a sheet of paper.
2. **Round 1 (3 minutes):** Person A writes one clear learning objective for that topic on a sticky note (colour 1) and places it on the table.
3. **Round 2 (4 minutes):** Person B reads the objective and writes an assessment that would genuinely test whether a learner had met it — on a sticky note (colour 2). They place it next to the objective.
4. **Round 3 (4 minutes):** Person C reads both the objective and assessment, then designs a learning activity that would prepare someone for that assessment — on a sticky note (colour 3).
5. The group now reviews all three sticky notes together. Do they form a coherent chain? Does the assessment actually match the objective? Does the activity prepare learners for the assessment? Discuss for five minutes and note any misalignments.
6. Whole-group debrief: ask each group to hold up their three sticky notes and describe one thing they'd change. Where did the chain break?

### Facilitator notes

The relay format forces each person to build on someone else's work, which makes misalignment visible in a way that solo design doesn't. Common issues: objectives that are too vague for anyone to design an assessment against, or assessments that test recall when the objective calls for application. If a group gets stuck at the objective stage, suggest they start their sentence with "After this session, learners will be able to..." followed by an action verb.

## Activity 3: Alignment Audit

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** Printed or projected copies of one sample training outline (facilitator prepares this in advance — a realistic one-page outline with 4–5 objectives, a few assessments, and a list of activities, with at least two deliberate misalignments built in)

### Instructions

1. Hand out the sample training outline. Explain that it contains some alignment problems — places where assessments don't match objectives, or activities don't connect to any stated objective.
2. Groups have 10 minutes to review the outline and identify as many misalignments as they can. For each one, they should note: what's misaligned, why it's a problem, and how they'd fix it.
3. Each group shares their findings. As they report, track the issues on a whiteboard or shared screen.
4. Debrief: which misalignments were easy to spot? Which were subtle? What made them hard to catch?

### Facilitator notes

Prepare your sample outline using a topic your participants will find relatable — a generic "communication skills" or "data management" workshop works well. Build in at least one objective-assessment mismatch (e.g., an objective that says "analyse" paired with a multiple-choice recall quiz) and one orphan activity (something engaging but not connected to any objective). If you want to increase the challenge, include an objective that's too vague to assess meaningfully — groups often disagree about whether that counts as a misalignment, which makes for good discussion.

## Activity 4: The Scope Creep Debate

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Whole group, split into two sides
**Materials:** None

### Instructions

1. Present this scenario: *"You're running a two-day workshop on research data management. Your learning objectives are set and your programme is designed. A senior colleague emails you the week before and says: 'You really should add a session on research ethics — it's so closely related, and the participants would benefit.' Adding it would mean cutting one of your existing modules or running 45 minutes over time."*
2. Split the room in half. One side argues for adding the session. The other argues against. Give each side three minutes to prepare their case.
3. Run the debate: each side gets two minutes to present, then one minute for rebuttal.
4. After the debate, drop the adversarial format. Ask the whole group: *"What would you actually do?"* Guide the discussion toward the key principle — does the addition serve your existing objectives? If not, is it important enough to revise the objectives (and realign everything else), or is it genuinely out of scope?
5. Close by asking: *"What's one question you could ask yourself next time you're tempted to add something to a course?"*

### Facilitator notes

This activity works because most participants have been on both sides of this situation. The debate format gives people permission to argue a position they might not naturally take, which surfaces assumptions. During the debrief, resist the urge to declare a "right answer" — the point is that scope decisions depend on whether additions serve the stated objectives, and that revising objectives is legitimate as long as it's deliberate rather than reactive. If your group is small (under 10), run it as a fishbowl discussion rather than a formal debate.

## Activity 5: Module Mapping Gallery Walk

**Time:** 25 minutes
**Group size:** Individual work, then whole group
**Materials:** Large paper (flipchart or A3), markers, sticky dots or stickers (optional)

### Instructions

1. Each participant takes a sheet of large paper and maps out a training programme they're working on. They write each module or section as a box, and underneath each box they list the learning objectives it serves. Give them 10 minutes for this.
2. Everyone posts their maps on the wall around the room.
3. Participants do a silent gallery walk (8 minutes). As they look at each map, they place a sticky dot next to any module that seems to lack a clear objective, or next to any objective that doesn't have a clear home. If no sticky dots are available, they can write a brief question on a small sticky note and attach it.
4. Everyone returns to their own map and reviews the feedback. Whole-group discussion (5 minutes): ask three or four people to share one thing they noticed — either a gap in their own map or something they saw on someone else's that made them think differently about structure.

### Facilitator notes

The gallery walk format works well here because it's low-pressure — participants give feedback by placing dots rather than critiquing out loud, which makes it easier to flag issues without anyone feeling put on the spot. Some participants won't have a full programme to map; suggest they sketch a programme they'd like to build, even if it's rough. Watch for maps where a single module carries four or five objectives — that's usually a sign it needs splitting, and it's worth highlighting during the debrief.
