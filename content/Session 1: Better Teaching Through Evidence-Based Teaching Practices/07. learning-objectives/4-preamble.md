# Learning Objectives

Before we dive in, a few questions to set the scene:

- Have you ever sat through a training session that felt scattered — like it was trying to cover everything at once but never quite landed?
- Have you ever designed a course and realised halfway through that you weren't sure what learners were supposed to walk away with?
- How do you currently decide what goes into your training and what stays out?

## Start with the end in mind

It's tempting to jump straight into creating content. You know your subject well, so you start writing slides, gathering resources, and planning activities. But without a clear picture of what your learners should be able to do by the end, you risk building something sprawling and unfocused.

Learning objectives are the anchor for everything else in your course. They answer a simple question: *what should someone know or be able to do after completing this training?*

Getting clear on that question first — before you write a single slide or plan a single exercise — changes the way you build the rest of your programme.

### Backward design

This approach has a name: *backward design*. The idea, developed by Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe in their book *Understanding by Design* (Wiggins & McTighe, 2005), is that effective course design works in reverse from where most people start. Instead of beginning with content and hoping it leads to the right outcomes, you begin with the outcomes and work backwards:

1. **Define your learning objectives.** What should learners know or be able to do?
2. **Design your assessments.** How will you (and your learners) know they've met those objectives?
3. **Plan your activities and content.** What learning experiences will get them there?

Each step flows from the one before it. Your assessments exist to measure whether objectives have been met. Your activities exist to prepare learners for those assessments. Nothing is there by accident.

This doesn't mean the process is purely linear — you'll often loop back and adjust as your thinking develops. But starting with objectives gives you a foundation that holds the rest of the design together.

## Tying assessments to objectives

Once you have clear objectives, your assessments should map directly onto them. If an objective says learners should be able to *apply* a technique in a new context, then your assessment needs to ask them to do exactly that — not just define the technique from memory.

This is where Bloom's taxonomy (covered earlier in the workbook) becomes practical. The verb in your objective tells you what level of understanding you're targeting, and that tells you what kind of assessment is appropriate. "Define X" calls for a different assessment than "evaluate whether X is the right approach in situation Y."

When assessments and objectives are aligned, you get a clear signal about whether your training is working. When they're misaligned — say, you want learners to analyse problems but you only test recall — you end up measuring the wrong thing.

## Using activities to meet objectives

The same logic applies to your learning activities. Every activity in your programme should connect to at least one learning objective. If you can't explain how an activity helps learners reach an objective, it probably doesn't belong — no matter how engaging it is.

This doesn't mean every moment needs to be grimly purposeful. Icebreakers and energisers serve real functions (building trust, resetting attention). But the core learning activities — the ones that take up most of your programme time — should earn their place by moving learners toward your stated objectives.

## Keep things modular

Well-designed training is modular: it's built from distinct sections, each with its own coherent focus. When you've defined clear learning objectives, modularity follows naturally. Each module addresses a specific set of objectives, and each objective sits clearly within a module.

This makes your programme easier to maintain, easier to adapt for different audiences, and easier for learners to navigate. It also means you can rearrange, update, or remove a module without the whole programme unravelling.

## Guarding against scope creep

One of the biggest risks in course design is trying to include too much. When you're passionate about your subject, everything feels essential. But a course that tries to cover everything covers nothing well.

Clear learning objectives are your best defence against scope creep. When you're tempted to add a new topic, a new reading, or a new activity, you can ask: *does this serve one of my learning objectives?* If the answer is no, it stays out — or it means you need to revisit your objectives.

This discipline is hard to maintain, especially when feedback from colleagues or learners suggests adding more. But a focused programme where learners genuinely master the objectives will always serve them better than an overstuffed one where they skim the surface of everything.

That said, objectives themselves aren't set in stone. Sometimes feedback reveals that an objective was pitched at the wrong level, that learners need a prerequisite skill you hadn't anticipated, or that a topic you left out genuinely matters. In those cases, the right move is to revise your objectives deliberately — and then realign your assessments and activities to match. The key difference is between adjusting your objectives with intention and letting your scope drift because something seemed interesting in the moment.

## Glossary

**Assessment** — Any method you use to check whether learners have met your objectives. This doesn't have to mean a test or exam — it could be a practical exercise, a group discussion, a demonstration, or a short reflection task.

**Backward design** — A course design approach where you start by defining what learners should be able to do, then decide how you'll check they can do it, and only then plan your content and activities. Developed by Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe.

**Bloom's taxonomy** — A framework that classifies different levels of thinking, from basic recall ("define this term") through to higher-order skills like analysis and evaluation. Useful for writing learning objectives with the right level of challenge.

**Learning activity** — Any structured experience designed to help learners build toward an objective — a group exercise, a case study, a practice task, a discussion, and so on.

**Learning objective** — A clear statement of what someone should know or be able to do after completing your training. Good objectives are specific enough to guide your design and assess your learners against.

**Module** — A self-contained section of your programme that focuses on a specific set of learning objectives. Modular design makes courses easier to maintain, adapt, and navigate.

**Scope creep** — The gradual, often unintentional expansion of what a course tries to cover. It usually happens when new topics or activities are added without checking whether they serve the stated learning objectives.
