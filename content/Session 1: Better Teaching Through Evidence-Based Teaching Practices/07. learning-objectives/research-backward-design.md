# Research: Backward Design (Wiggins & McTighe)

**Date:** 2026-03-13
**Purpose:** Verify claims about backward design for the workbook chapter on learning objectives.

---

## Summary of Findings

### 1. Attribution

**Confirmed.** Backward design was introduced to curriculum design in 1998 by Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe in their book *Understanding by Design*. They are correctly credited with developing and popularising the framework. The concept of starting with the end in mind has older roots (e.g., Ralph Tyler's work in the 1940s on curriculum rationale), but Wiggins and McTighe formalised it into the structured three-stage process widely used today.

Grant Wiggins (1950--2015) held a doctorate from the Harvard Graduate School of Education and was president of Authentic Education. Jay McTighe holds a Master's degree from the University of Maryland, with post-graduate studies at Johns Hopkins University. He has co-authored 18 books and continues to consult on curriculum design.

### 3. Accuracy of the Three-Stage Process

The three stages as described in the source text are **substantially accurate but use slightly different terminology** than Wiggins and McTighe's own labels. Here is a comparison:

| Source text description | Wiggins & McTighe's original terminology |
|---|---|
| (1) Define learning objectives | **Stage 1: Identify Desired Results** |
| (2) Design assessments | **Stage 2: Determine Assessment Evidence** |
| (3) Plan activities and content | **Stage 3: Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction** |

**Key nuances:**

- **Stage 1** is broader than just "learning objectives." Wiggins and McTighe describe it as identifying desired results, which includes: established goals/standards, enduring understandings ("Students will understand that..."), essential questions, and knowledge and skills. The term "learning objectives" captures part of this but misses the emphasis on *enduring understandings* and *essential questions*, which are central to the UbD framework.
- **Stage 2** is accurately described. The original term is "determine acceptable evidence," which includes performance tasks, projects, observations, quizzes, tests, and other evidence -- not only formal assessments.
- **Stage 3** is accurately described. Wiggins and McTighe use the phrase "plan learning experiences and instruction."

**Recommendation:** If the workbook simplifies the stages for its audience, this is reasonable -- but consider using language closer to "identify desired results" for Stage 1 rather than just "define learning objectives," since the distinction between objectives and enduring understandings is a meaningful part of the framework.

### 4. Common Misconceptions

The following misconceptions are worth noting for the workbook audience:

1. **"Backward design means you must use formal tests or exams."** Stage 2 calls for *evidence* of learning, not necessarily formal assessments. Performance tasks, observations, portfolios, conversations, and practical demonstrations all count. This is especially relevant for our workbook's audience, who are designing practical training -- not academic courses.

2. **"It is a rigid, linear process."** Wiggins and McTighe describe backward design as iterative. Designers move back and forth between the three stages as their thinking develops. It is a planning logic, not a strict sequence.

3. **"Backward design is only for formal education."** The framework is widely adapted for professional training, workshops, and other non-academic settings. The core principle -- start with what learners should be able to do, then determine how you will know they can do it, then plan experiences -- applies broadly.

4. **"Activity-focused planning is the same thing."** One of Wiggins and McTighe's central arguments is that many educators plan "forward" by starting with activities or content ("What shall I teach? What textbook should I use?") rather than starting with desired outcomes. This activity-focused approach is what backward design explicitly pushes against. Simply having good activities is not the same as designing backward from clear goals.

5. **"Backward design requires detailed, measurable objectives for everything."** While clarity of goals is essential, Wiggins and McTighe emphasise *understanding* (which is inherently harder to measure than recall). Their framework includes "essential questions" that are open-ended and exploratory by nature.

### Criticisms (for awareness, not necessarily for inclusion in workbook)

- Critics note that the framework can be difficult to apply when curriculum standards are vague or when textbooks do not highlight key concepts clearly.
- Some argue backward design focuses too heavily on assessment outcomes and may undervalue the role of student interests, social dynamics, and emergent learning.
- The framework's roots in measurement and evaluation mean it works best when goals can be articulated clearly upfront, which is not always the case in more exploratory or creative domains.
