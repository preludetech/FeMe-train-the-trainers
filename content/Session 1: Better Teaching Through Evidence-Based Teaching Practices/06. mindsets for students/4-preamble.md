# Mindsets for students

## Growth mindset

By now you've seen how the brain changes with practice, how memory works, and how learning is built through effort. All of that points toward one idea: ability is not fixed. It grows. This is the core of a **growth mindset** — the belief that intelligence and skills can be developed through effort, effective strategies, and support from others (Dweck, 2006).

As a trainer, this matters because your learners arrive with their own beliefs about what they can and can't do. Some will think they're "just not a maths person" or "not technical." Those beliefs shape how they respond to difficulty. A learner with a fixed mindset sees struggle as proof of inability. A learner with a growth mindset sees it as part of the process.

You don't change someone's mindset by telling them to have a different one. You shape it through how you teach — how you respond to mistakes, how you frame challenges, and what you praise.

### A common misconception: "just try harder"

One of the most widespread misunderstandings of growth mindset is that it means praising effort alone. Telling a struggling learner "at least you tried hard" is not helpful if their approach isn't working. Growth mindset is not about trying *harder* — it is about trying *better*. That means using effective strategies, seeking help when stuck, and adapting your approach when something isn't working (Dweck, 2015). As a trainer, praise the process — the strategies, the persistence, the willingness to try a new approach — not just the effort.

### A note on the research

Growth mindset is one of the most widely discussed ideas in education, and it has also attracted significant debate. Some large-scale studies have found that growth mindset interventions — short programmes designed to shift learners' beliefs — produce only small effects on academic performance. Other researchers argue that these effects, while modest, are meaningful for a low-cost intervention, particularly for learners who are struggling.

What is not in dispute is this: the way you teach shapes how your learners think about their own ability. Whether or not a brief intervention can shift a mindset on its own, the principles behind growth mindset — framing challenges as opportunities, responding constructively to mistakes, and praising process over innate talent — are well-supported practices that make a real difference in learning environments (Yeager & Dweck, 2020).

### Failure as a learning opportunity

When learners encounter something they cannot yet do, that moment of struggle is not wasted time — it is where some of the deepest learning happens. Research on **productive failure** shows that learners who attempt to solve problems *before* being taught the solution develop stronger conceptual understanding than those who receive instruction first (Kapur, 2024). The initial failure activates prior knowledge, reveals gaps, and creates a readiness to learn that direct instruction alone does not.

This connects to a broader principle called **desirable difficulties** — conditions that make learning feel harder in the short term but produce stronger, more durable learning in the long term (Bjork & Bjork, 2011). Spacing out practice, mixing up problem types, and testing yourself rather than re-reading are all examples. They feel less comfortable than massed, blocked, passive study — but they work better.

There is an important caveat: failure is only productive when the learner can make sense of what went wrong. Sometimes that happens through support — feedback, consolidation, or guided instruction. But it can also happen when a learner recognises a gap and pursues that knowledge independently, whether by reading, experimenting, or seeking out resources on their own.

The key ingredient is not the trainer's intervention; it is the learner's awareness of the gap and the ability to act on it. Your role as a trainer is to design the struggle deliberately — set a challenge that is within reach but not yet solved, let learners wrestle with it — and then make sure they have what they need to close the gap, whether that comes from you or from their own resourcefulness.

The connection to mindset is direct. A learner with a growth mindset treats failure as information — "What can I learn from this?"A learner with a fixed mindset treats it as a verdict — "I'm not smart enough."

### Failure and the growth mindset

Learners do not need to believe in struggle before they can benefit from it — often it works the other way around. A learner who pushes through a difficult problem and comes out the other side with real understanding starts to see struggle differently. That lived experience of "this was hard, and I grew from it" is more convincing than any pep talk. As a trainer, you can reinforce this by helping learners notice their own progress and by responding to their mistakes as a normal, useful part of the process.

Even when learners push through a series of challenges successfully, they often remember the experience as pure struggle and frustration. People tend to overlook their own wins. Make a habit of pointing out progress — your learners might not notice it themselves.

## Stress-is-enhancing mindset

Growth mindset gets a lot of attention, but there is another mindset that matters just as much in a learning setting: the **stress-is-enhancing mindset**.

Most people assume that stress is purely harmful — something to avoid or minimise. But research shows that how you think about stress changes what it does to you (Crum et al., 2013). When people believe that stress can sharpen focus, build resilience, and fuel performance, they actually experience better outcomes under pressure. Their bodies respond more adaptively — producing hormonal profiles associated with challenge rather than threat.

This is not about pretending stress doesn't exist or pushing people to breaking point. It is about recognising that moderate stress in a learning context — the kind that comes from grappling with something difficult — can be a signal that you are growing. A large study tracking nearly 29,000 adults found that people who experienced high stress *and* believed stress was harmful had a significantly higher risk of poor health outcomes. People who experienced the same level of stress but did not view it as harmful showed no such increase (Keller et al., 2012).

### How does this relate to growth mindset?

The stress-is-enhancing mindset and growth mindset are distinct ideas, but they reinforce each other. Growth mindset says: "I can improve with effort and good strategies." The stress-is-enhancing mindset says: "The discomfort I'm feeling right now is part of that improvement." Together, they help learners stay engaged when things get hard, rather than interpreting difficulty as a sign that something is wrong.

### Why does this matter for trainers?

Your learners will experience stress during training — when they encounter unfamiliar material, when they make mistakes in front of others, when they face a task they are not sure they can complete. If they interpret that stress as a sign of failure or harm, they will disengage. If they interpret it as a normal, even useful, part of learning, they are more likely to persist.

You can help shape this interpretation. Normalise the experience of productive struggle: "If this feels hard, that's because you're learning something new — and that's exactly what this session is for." Avoid language that frames difficulty as something to get through or get past, and instead frame it as evidence that they are growing.

## Motivation

When you are teaching adult learners, motivation becomes especially important. Adults choose to be in your training — or at least, they've agreed to be there — and they bring their own goals, pressures, and prior experiences. Unlike school-age students, you can't rely on external structures (grades, attendance registers, parental involvement) to keep them engaged. Even in mandatory training, where a learner may not have chosen to attend, you still need to earn their engagement through relevance and respect rather than compliance.

Motivation is also deeply tied to how learners perceive effort. If a task feels pointlessly difficult, motivation drops. If it feels trivially easy and boredom sets in, motivation drops. If it feels achievable and worthwhile, motivation rises. This pattern shows up across several frameworks. Research on **flow** shows that people are most engaged when the challenge of a task matches their current skill level (Wikipedia, "Flow (psychology)"), and the concept of the **zone of proximal development** describes the productive space just beyond what a learner can do independently.

Your job as a trainer is to hit that balance — challenging enough to be meaningful, supported enough to be possible.

### What drives motivation in adult learners?

One of the most useful frameworks for understanding motivation is **Self-Determination Theory** (Ryan & Deci, 2000). It identifies three psychological needs that, when met, sustain motivation:

- **Autonomy** — the feeling of having choice and ownership over your learning. When learners feel controlled or micromanaged, motivation drops. When they have some say in what or how they learn, motivation rises.
- **Competence** — the feeling that you are capable and making progress. This is not about being the best — it is about sensing that you are getting better. Feedback that highlights growth supports competence; feedback that highlights deficiency undermines it.
- **Relatedness** — the feeling of connection and belonging. Learners are more motivated when they feel part of a group, when they trust their trainer, and when they believe others are invested in their success.

When these needs are met, learners develop **intrinsic motivation** — they engage because the learning itself is meaningful, not because of external rewards or consequences. Intrinsic motivation is a far stronger predictor of engagement and long-term retention than extrinsic motivators like certificates, grades, or penalties (Ryan & Deci, 2000).

That said, learners also need to see the *point*. Expectancy-value theory puts it simply: motivation depends on two things — "Can I do this?" (expectancy) and "Is it worth my time?" (value) (Wigfield & Eccles, 2000). If either answer is no, motivation collapses. This means you need to do two things simultaneously: build your learners' confidence that they can succeed, and make the relevance of what they are learning unmistakably clear.

### Keeping motivation alive in practice

- **Make relevance explicit.** Don't assume learners will see why something matters — tell them. Connect each topic to their real-world work and goals. Adults are problem-centred: they learn best when they can see how knowledge applies to situations they actually face.
- **Give learners choices.** Even small choices — which problem to tackle first, whether to work alone or in pairs, which example to focus on — support autonomy and increase engagement.
- **Calibrate difficulty.** Pitch tasks within reach but not yet mastered. Provide scaffolding for harder tasks and increase the challenge as skills develop. Watch for signs of both frustration (too hard) and disengagement (too easy).
- **Provide frequent, specific feedback.** Learners need to see their own progress. Generic praise ("good job") does little; specific feedback ("your analysis of X was much stronger this time — here's why") supports both competence and growth mindset.
- **Build connection.** Use collaborative activities, pair work, and group discussions. A sense of shared purpose and mutual support sustains motivation through difficult stretches.

- Why are learners joining your course? What is motivating them in the first place?
- What are some ways you can build choices into your course?
- What are some ways you can keep them motivated?

## A positive and safe environment

In the case study above, the learner might have put up their hand and asked for help if they believed that would lead to a positive outcome — but they didn't. This is a common pattern. When an environment feels unsafe, learners behave as though they have a fixed mindset, even if they don't actually hold one. They avoid asking questions, hide confusion, and stop taking risks.

A safe learning environment is not about being soft or lowering standards. It is about creating conditions where learners feel they can be honest about what they don't yet understand, without facing judgement or embarrassment. In fact, safety and high standards go hand in hand. Research on **psychological safety** shows that the most productive learning happens when both safety *and* accountability are high — this is sometimes called the "learning zone" (Edmondson, as cited in NeuroLeadership Institute). High standards alone produce anxiety. Safety alone produces comfort. You need both.

### Building safety in your classroom

#### Engage with all your learners

In any group, a small number of confident, vocal people tend to dominate discussions. If you only interact with those learners, everyone else receives the implicit message: this space is not for me. Use structured participation methods — think-pair-share, written responses before discussion, round-robin sharing — so that quieter learners contribute too. After asking a question, wait. Most trainers wait only a second or two, which favours fast processors and extroverts. Give people time to think.

#### Create space for questions

How you respond to the first few questions in a session sets the tone for everything that follows. If a question is met with genuine engagement — "That's a really important point, let me address it properly" — learners will keep asking. If it is met with impatience or dismissal, they will stop.

Model question-asking yourself. When you openly say "I'm not sure — let me check" or "I hadn't thought of it that way," you normalise not-knowing. Provide multiple channels for questions: not everyone is comfortable asking aloud. Written question cards, digital Q&A tools, or breaks where learners can approach you privately all help.

#### Understand who is in your classroom

Your learners bring different levels of prior experience, different cultural backgrounds, different power dynamics, and different anxieties. A junior researcher in a room full of professors will behave differently than one among peers. If supervisors and supervisees are in the same training, the supervisees may self-censor. Invisible diversity matters too — disability, neurodivergence, language background, and caring responsibilities all shape a learner's experience in ways that are not always visible.

Where possible, learn about your learners before the session. Pre-training surveys about experience levels, goals, and concerns help you design for the actual room rather than the room you imagined. During the session, pay attention to who is engaging and who has gone quiet — that silence often tells you something important.

#### Stereotype threat

When learners belong to a group that is negatively stereotyped in a given domain — for example, women in a technical field, or people from a particular background in an academic setting — they may experience additional anxiety about confirming that stereotype. This phenomenon, known as **stereotype threat**, can impair performance and reduce engagement, even in learners who are otherwise confident.

Research has shown that simply changing how a task is framed — describing it as a "problem-solving exercise" rather than a "test of intellectual ability" — eliminated the performance gap between groups (Steele & Aronson, 1995). The task was identical; only the framing changed.

As a trainer, you can reduce stereotype threat by framing activities as learning opportunities rather than evaluations, by emphasising that ability is something that develops with practice, and by avoiding unnecessary attention to demographic characteristics before assessments or challenging tasks. Presenting diverse role models and examples also helps — it signals that success in this domain is not limited to one kind of person.

#### Normalise mistakes

This connects back to everything in this chapter. If mistakes are met with embarrassment — from you or from other learners — people stop taking risks. If mistakes are met with curiosity and constructive feedback, people keep trying.

Model this yourself. Share your own mistakes and what you learned from them. Reframe errors explicitly: instead of "that's wrong," try "that's a really common misconception — let's unpack why." Separate practice from evaluation so learners know when it is safe to experiment. And celebrate the moments where a mistake led to deeper understanding — those moments are worth pointing out, because they reinforce the core message of this chapter: learning is built through effort, struggle, and the willingness to get things wrong on the way to getting them right.

## Glossary

**Autonomy** — One of the three needs in Self-Determination Theory. It is the feeling of having choice and ownership over your own learning, rather than being told exactly what to do and how to do it.

**Competence** — One of the three needs in Self-Determination Theory. It is the sense that you are capable and making progress — not that you are the best, but that you are getting better.

**Desirable difficulties** — Learning conditions that feel harder in the moment but lead to stronger, longer-lasting learning. Examples include spacing out practice sessions, mixing up problem types, and testing yourself instead of re-reading.

**Expectancy-value theory** — A model of motivation built on two questions: "Can I do this?" (expectancy) and "Is it worth my time?" (value). When either answer is no, motivation drops sharply.

**Fixed mindset** — The belief that abilities and skills are set traits that cannot meaningfully change. Learners with a fixed mindset tend to interpret struggle as proof that they lack ability.

**Flow** — A state of deep engagement that occurs when the challenge of a task closely matches your current skill level — not so easy that you lose interest, not so hard that you become anxious.

**Growth mindset** — The belief that abilities and skills can be developed through effort, effective strategies, and support. In this workbook, growth mindset is less about a one-off intervention and more about how you teach day to day.

**Intrinsic motivation** — Motivation that comes from the learning itself — because it is interesting, meaningful, or satisfying — rather than from external rewards like grades, certificates, or penalties.

**Productive failure** — A teaching approach where learners attempt to solve a problem *before* receiving instruction. The initial struggle activates prior knowledge and creates a readiness to learn that direct instruction alone does not.

**Psychological safety** — A shared belief that it is safe to take interpersonal risks — asking questions, admitting confusion, making mistakes — without fear of judgement or punishment. In this workbook, it refers to the classroom conditions that allow learners to be honest about what they do not yet understand.

**Relatedness** — One of the three needs in Self-Determination Theory. It is the feeling of connection and belonging — sensing that you are part of a group and that others are invested in your success.

**Scaffolding** — Temporary support structures that help a learner tackle a task they cannot yet manage independently. As the learner's skill grows, the scaffolding is gradually removed.

**Self-Determination Theory** — A framework identifying three psychological needs that sustain motivation: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. When these needs are met, learners are more likely to develop intrinsic motivation.

**Stereotype threat** — The anxiety that arises when a learner fears confirming a negative stereotype about a group they belong to. It can impair performance even in learners who are otherwise confident and capable.

**Stress-is-enhancing mindset** — The belief that stress can sharpen focus, build resilience, and fuel growth, rather than being purely harmful. How you think about stress shapes how your body and mind respond to it.

**Zone of proximal development** — The space between what a learner can do independently and what they cannot yet do at all. It is the productive zone where learning happens — with the right level of challenge and support.
