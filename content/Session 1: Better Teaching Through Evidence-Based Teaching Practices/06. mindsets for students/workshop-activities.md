# Workshop Activities: Mindsets for Students

These activities are designed for a facilitator running a live workshop based on this chapter. Each activity includes timing, group size, materials needed, and step-by-step instructions.

## Activity 1: Praise Makeover

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Pairs
**Materials:** None (pen and paper optional)

### Instructions

1. Ask each participant to think of something they might say to a struggling learner — the kind of encouraging remark that comes naturally. Give them one minute to write it down or hold it in mind.
2. In pairs, each person shares their phrase. Their partner's job is to identify whether the phrase praises *effort alone* ("Great job trying!"), *innate ability* ("You're so smart"), or *process* (the strategy, the adaptation, the specific thing the learner did). Most phrases will land in the first two categories — that's the point.
3. Together, the pair rewrites each phrase so it praises process. If they can't identify what was effective in the learner's approach, they should note that too — it may mean the learner needs a different strategy, not just encouragement.
4. Bring the group back together. Ask two or three pairs to share their before-and-after versions. Highlight the pattern: process praise gives the learner something concrete to repeat or build on, while effort praise can feel hollow.

### Facilitator notes

- People often feel defensive when they realise their go-to praise is vague. Keep the tone light — this is about sharpening a skill, not exposing bad habits.
- If a pair gets stuck rewriting, prompt them: "What specifically did the learner *do* that you could name?" If the answer is "nothing effective," that's a useful insight — it means the learner needs guidance on strategy, not more encouragement.
- With larger groups, you can do this as a think-pair-share: individual reflection first, then pairs, then a few shares with the whole room.

## Activity 2: The Productive Failure Lab

**Time:** 25 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** Flipchart paper or large sticky notes, markers

### Instructions

1. Explain the concept briefly: learners who attempt a problem *before* being taught the solution often develop stronger understanding — but only if they can make sense of what went wrong afterwards. This is productive failure.
2. Each group picks one topic or skill from their own training context. Their task: design a short challenge that learners would attempt *before* receiving any instruction on the topic. The challenge should be hard enough that most learners won't get it right, but accessible enough that they can meaningfully engage.
3. Give groups 10 minutes to design their challenge and write it on the flipchart paper. They should also note: what would you do *after* the attempt? How would you help learners close the gap between what they tried and what works?
4. Groups rotate around the room (gallery walk style) and read each other's designs. Each group leaves one sticky note of feedback on the others: either a strength ("This would really surface the misconception") or a question ("What if learners don't have enough background to even start?").
5. Debrief with the whole group. Ask: "What made it hard to design the struggle? What are you worried about when letting learners fail?" Draw out the tension between wanting to protect learners and trusting them to learn through difficulty.

### Facilitator notes

- The most common pitfall is designing a challenge that's too hard — where learners have nothing to work with. Push groups to consider what prior knowledge their learners *do* have, and build the challenge around that.
- Some participants will resist the idea of letting learners fail. That's worth naming openly. The discomfort is real and mirrors what their own learners feel.
- If groups struggle to pick a topic, suggest they choose something they currently teach by explaining first and then practising — and simply flip the order.

## Activity 3: Stress Reframe Role-Play

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Pairs, then whole group
**Materials:** None

### Instructions

1. Set the scene: "Your learners are about to do something that makes them visibly anxious — a hands-on exercise, a presentation, a challenging problem set. You can see the stress in the room. What do you say?"
2. In pairs, one person plays the trainer and the other plays a stressed learner. The "trainer" has 60 seconds to say something that reframes the stress as a normal and useful part of learning. The "learner" responds naturally — they can push back, ask questions, or stay quiet.
3. Swap roles and repeat.
4. Bring the group together. Ask for volunteers to share what they said. Write key phrases on a whiteboard or flipchart. Then ask: "Which of these would actually land with a stressed learner? Which ones sound like empty reassurance?"
5. As a group, identify what makes a stress reframe effective versus hollow. The key: connect the discomfort to something specific about what is happening in their learning ("You're working at the edge of what you know — that's exactly where learning happens") rather than generic comfort ("Don't worry, you'll be fine").

### Facilitator notes

- Watch for participants who default to minimising stress ("It's not that hard" or "Just relax"). Name the pattern: minimising tells the learner their experience is wrong. Reframing validates the experience and gives it a different meaning.
- This activity surfaces the difference between the stress-is-enhancing mindset and toxic positivity. If it comes up, lean into the distinction — it's one of the most important takeaways.
- For groups who find role-play uncomfortable, you can make it lower-stakes by having them write their reframe on paper first and then read it aloud to their partner.

## Activity 4: The Motivation Audit

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** One printed or projected copy of the three needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness)

### Instructions

1. Display the three needs from Self-Determination Theory: autonomy (choice and ownership), competence (a sense of progress), and relatedness (connection and belonging).
2. Each participant silently picks one session or module from their own training. They spend 3 minutes writing quick notes: where does this session support each need? Where does it fall short?
3. In small groups, each person shares their session and their honest assessment. The group's job is not to fix it — it's to ask sharpening questions. For example: "When do your learners actually get a choice?" or "How do they know they've improved?"
4. After everyone has shared, each group identifies one pattern they noticed across their sessions. Common patterns: lots of competence-building but little autonomy, or plenty of autonomy but weak relatedness.
5. Debrief with the whole group. Ask each group to share their pattern. Then ask: "Which of the three needs is hardest to design for in your context? Why?"

### Facilitator notes

- Autonomy is usually the hardest for trainers to build in — especially those working in structured or compliance-driven settings. Acknowledge this. Even small choices count (which problem to tackle first, whether to work alone or in pairs).
- If participants struggle to assess their own sessions, suggest they think about it from the learner's perspective: "If I were sitting in your session, when would I feel like I had a choice? When would I feel like I was getting better? When would I feel connected to other people in the room?"
- Keep the energy focused on diagnosis, not solutions. The goal is to see the gaps clearly. Solutions come next, in their own design work.

## Activity 5: Spot the Threat

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4, then whole group
**Materials:** Printed handout with 6–8 example task instructions (prepare in advance), markers or highlighters

### Instructions

1. Distribute the handout. Each example is a task instruction a trainer might give to learners — some frame the activity as an evaluation ("Let's test your understanding of…"), others frame it as a learning opportunity ("Let's practise applying…"). Mix in some ambiguous ones.
2. In small groups, participants read each instruction and mark it: does this feel like an evaluation or a learning opportunity? They should pay attention to their own gut reaction — if it feels like a test, it probably feels like a test to their learners too.
3. For each instruction marked as evaluative, the group rewrites it so it frames the same activity as a learning opportunity. Small changes count: "Let's see how well you can…" becomes "Let's practise…"
4. Groups share their most interesting rewrites with the whole room.
5. Debrief by connecting this to stereotype threat: when a task feels like a test of ability, learners who belong to negatively stereotyped groups in that domain experience additional anxiety — and their performance drops. Simply reframing the task can eliminate that gap. The task itself doesn't change; only the framing does.

### Facilitator notes

- Prepare the handout in advance. Use realistic examples from common training settings — software exercises, group presentations, problem sets, case study discussions. Include some that are genuinely ambiguous so groups have something to debate.
- Participants sometimes argue that learners "should" be able to handle evaluative framing. Redirect: this isn't about what learners should tolerate — it's about what actually helps them learn. The research is clear: framing matters.
- If you don't have time to prepare a handout, you can run a simpler version: ask each group to pull out their own training materials and find one place where their framing might feel evaluative to a learner. This takes longer but uses real examples.

## Activity 6: First Mistake Response Plan

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Individual, then pairs, then whole group
**Materials:** None (pen and paper optional)

### Instructions

1. Ask everyone to close their eyes (or look down) and picture this: you are running a training session. A learner gives a wrong answer in front of the whole group. There's a pause. Everyone is watching. What do you say?
2. Give participants 2 minutes to write down their actual, honest response — not the ideal one, but what would probably come out of their mouth in the moment.
3. In pairs, share what you wrote. Then work together to craft a better version — one that treats the mistake as useful, preserves the learner's dignity, and keeps the group engaged. Write two versions: one for a mistake made publicly, and one you'd use during individual work.
4. Ask three or four pairs to share their "public mistake" response. Write them on the board.
5. Debrief: "What patterns do you notice in the effective responses?" Draw out the key ingredients: curiosity instead of correction ("Tell me more about your thinking"), normalisation ("That's a really common place to get stuck"), and redirection toward learning ("Let's use that to look at why this is tricky").

### Facilitator notes

- The honesty step (writing what you'd *actually* say) is important. Most people's instinctive response is either too blunt ("No, that's wrong") or too vague ("Good try!"). Neither helps.
- Some participants will write something close to perfect on the first try. Push them: "Now imagine you're tired, behind schedule, and this is the third wrong answer in a row. What comes out then?" The goal is to plan for the real moment, not the ideal one.
- This activity connects growth mindset, psychological safety, and motivation in a single practical skill. Name that connection in the debrief — responding well to mistakes is where all three come together.
