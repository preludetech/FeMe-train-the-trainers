Research compiled for the workbook chapter on mindsets, aimed at trainers and educators working with adult learners.

## 1. Psychological Safety in Learning Contexts (Amy Edmondson)

### Definition

Amy Edmondson, Novartis Professor of Leadership and Management at Harvard Business School, defines **psychological safety** as "a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk taking." More specifically, it is "a belief that one will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes."

- Source: [Amy Edmondson - Psychological Safety](https://amycedmondson.com/psychological-safety/)

### The Original Research

Edmondson's 1999 study of 51 work teams in a manufacturing company found that team psychological safety was significantly associated with learning behavior. Teams with higher psychological safety engaged more in behaviors like asking questions, seeking feedback, experimenting, reflecting on results, and discussing errors.

- Source: Edmondson, A. (1999). Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams. *Administrative Science Quarterly*, 44(2), 350-383. [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.2307/2666999) | [MIT PDF](https://web.mit.edu/curhan/www/docs/Articles/15341_Readings/Group_Performance/Edmondson%20Psychological%20safety.pdf)

### The Four Zones Model (Safety x Accountability Matrix)

Edmondson maps psychological safety against accountability/standards in a 2x2 matrix that produces four zones:

| | Low Accountability | High Accountability |
|---|---|---|
| **High Psychological Safety** | **Comfort Zone** -- People feel safe but are not challenged. Low motivation, coasting. | **Learning Zone** -- The sweet spot. People collaborate, take risks, learn from mistakes, and are held to high standards. |
| **Low Psychological Safety** | **Apathy Zone** -- People do the bare minimum. No engagement, no ownership. | **Anxiety Zone** -- People are afraid to take risks, stay quiet, hide mistakes. High stress, low learning. |

**Key insight for trainers:** High standards alone do not produce learning -- they produce anxiety. Psychological safety alone does not produce learning -- it produces comfort. You need both together.

- Source: [Neuroleadership Institute - Psychological Safety and Accountability: Insights From Amy Edmondson](https://neuroleadership.com/your-brain-at-work/psychological-safety-and-accountability-insights-from-amy-edmondson)
- Source: [The Good Boss - The Four Zones of Psychological Safety](https://www.thegoodboss.com/p/the-four-zones-of-psychological-safety)

### Application to Training and Classrooms

Edmondson's quote: "High psychological safety means a high learning quotient -- these are teams that are engaged, leaning in, honest, and working hard to create value."

In learning environments specifically, psychological safety enables learners to:
- Raise their hands even when unsure
- Challenge ideas respectfully
- View errors as learning opportunities rather than sources of shame
- Engage in active learning strategies
- Ask for help without fear of judgment

- Source: [UNLEASH - Amy Edmondson Interview](https://www.unleash.ai/company-culture/amy-edmondson-high-psychological-safety-means-a-high-learning-quotient-what-hr-leaders-need-to-know/)
- Source: [Harvard Medical School - Psychological Safety and Accountability in Health Professions Education](https://learn.hms.harvard.edu/insights/all-insights/psychological-safety-and-accountability-secret-sauce-health-professions-education)

## 2. Unsafe Environments and Fixed Mindset Behaviour

### The Claim

"When an environment feels unsafe, learners behave as though they have a fixed mindset, even if they don't actually hold one."

### Verdict: Well-Supported

This claim is supported by converging lines of evidence from psychology and neuroscience:

### Evidence from Mindset and Psychological Safety Research

- People with a fixed mindset "believe that human qualities such as intellectual skills are carved in stone," which creates "an urgency to prove yourself." In psychologically unsafe environments, even growth-minded individuals adopt these same **protective behaviours**: avoiding challenges, hiding mistakes, not asking questions, and refusing to take risks.
- When people feel their position in a group is insecure, "the learning centers of our brains shut down." They avoid learning behaviours, are less likely to take risks, don't ask for help or draw attention to themselves, and "stop doing the things that will help them learn and grow."
- Participants who fear making mistakes or receiving negative feedback behave identically to those with a fixed mindset -- they avoid risk because mistakes would "highlight their perceived innate and permanent deficiencies."

- Source: [Skip Bowman - How to Develop a Growth Mindset via Psychological Safety](https://www.skip-bowman.com/how-to-develop-a-growth-mindset-via-psychological-safety)
- Source: [APM - Cultivating Psychological Safety and a Growth Mindset Culture](https://www.apm.org.uk/blog/cultivating-psychological-safety-and-a-growth-mindset-culture/)
- Source: [Advances in Simulation - From Safety Net to Trampoline: Growth Mindset in Healthcare Simulation](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s41077-023-00264-1)

### Neuroscience Basis

The connection has a neurological basis. Social threats (feeling judged, excluded, or incompetent) activate the same neural pathways as physical threats:

- The **amygdala** (the brain's alarm system) activates the fight-flight-freeze response.
- This triggers release of **cortisol** and other stress hormones.
- The **prefrontal cortex** -- responsible for executive functions like complex thinking, problem-solving, creativity, and learning -- becomes less efficient as resources are diverted to threat management.
- The result: people in threatening learning environments literally have reduced cognitive capacity for learning, regardless of their beliefs about intelligence.

- Source: [BrainWorks Institute - The Neuroscience Behind Psychological Safety at Work](https://www.brainworksinstitute.org/post/the-neuroscience-behind-psychological-safety-at-work)
- Source: [PeopleKult - The Neuroscience of Fearless Work](https://www.peoplekult.com/post/neuroscience-of-psychological-safety-the-brain-s-blueprint-for-a-fearless-workplace)

### Implication for Trainers

You cannot simply tell learners to "have a growth mindset." If the environment feels threatening, their brains will override their beliefs. Creating safety is a prerequisite for growth mindset behaviour, not a bonus.

## 3. Stereotype Threat

### What It Is

**Stereotype threat** is the risk of confirming, as a self-characteristic, a negative stereotype about one's group. Coined by Claude Steele and Joshua Aronson in 1995, it describes the anxiety and impaired performance that can occur when a person is aware of a negative stereotype that applies to them in a given situation.

### The Foundational Study (Steele & Aronson, 1995)

Steele and Aronson gave Black and White college students the same difficult verbal test under two conditions:

1. **Stereotype threat condition:** Told the test measured intellectual ability (activating the stereotype).
2. **Non-threat condition:** Told the test was a problem-solving exercise (no stereotype activated).

Result: Black students performed significantly worse when told the test measured ability, but performed comparably to White students when the framing was neutral. The test content was identical -- only the framing changed.

- Source: Steele, C.M. & Aronson, J. (1995). Stereotype threat and the intellectual test performance of African Americans. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 69(5), 797-811. [APA PsycNet](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1996-12938-001) | [PDF via Berkeley](https://greatergood.berkeley.edu/images/uploads/Claude_Steele_and_Joshua_Aronson,_1995.pdf)

### Further Key Research

Steele's 1997 paper, "A Threat in the Air," expanded the theory, arguing that stereotype threat could affect any group for whom a negative stereotype exists in a given domain. This has been demonstrated with:
- Women in mathematics
- Elderly people on memory tasks
- White men compared to Asian men on maths tests
- Low-socioeconomic-status students on academic tasks

- Source: Steele, C.M. (1997). A threat in the air: How stereotypes shape intellectual identity and performance. *American Psychologist*, 52(6), 613-629. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9174398/)

### How Stereotype Threat Impairs Performance

Research has identified several mechanisms:
- **Disrupts working memory and executive function** -- cognitive resources are consumed by anxiety and self-monitoring
- **Increases physiological arousal** -- stress hormones impair thinking
- **Increases self-consciousness** -- attention diverted from the task to monitoring one's own performance
- **Triggers thought suppression** -- trying to push away negative thoughts uses cognitive resources
- **Causes anxiety** -- further impairing performance

- Source: [EBSCO Research Starters - Stereotype Threat](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/social-sciences-and-humanities/stereotype-threat)
- Source: [Psychology in Action - Claude Steele's Stereotype Threat Paradigm](https://www.psychologyinaction.org/2013-01-23-classic-psychology-experiments-claude-steeles-stereotype-threat-paradigm/)

### Important Caveats on the Research

The stereotype threat literature has faced significant replication challenges in recent years:

- Some large-scale preregistered replications have failed to reproduce the effect, particularly when moving from laboratory settings to real-world classrooms.
- Meta-analyses have found evidence of **publication bias** inflating effect sizes -- studies showing the effect were more likely to be published than those that did not.
- One analysis estimated that publication bias inflated the percentage of studies supporting gender stereotype threat from approximately 14% to 84%.
- The effect appears strongest in highly controlled laboratory studies and weakens in more naturalistic settings.

**However:** The core insight -- that how you frame a task, and the identity-related anxiety a learner brings into the room, matters for performance -- remains well-supported and practically important. Even critics acknowledge that situational cues and belonging concerns affect learner behaviour.

- Source: [Replicability Index - Replication Failures in the Stereotype Threat Literature](https://replicationindex.com/2017/04/07/hidden-figures-replication-failures-in-the-stereotype-threat-literature/)
- Source: [Michael Inzlicht - Revisiting Stereotype Threat](https://www.speakandregret.michaelinzlicht.com/p/revisiting-stereotype-threat)
- Source: [Russell Warne - Stereotype Threat Needs Replication Studies](https://russellwarne.com/2021/08/07/send-in-the-clones-stereotype-threat-needs-replication-studies/)
- Source: Picho-Kiroga, K., Turnbull, A., & Rodriguez-Leahy, A. (2021). Stereotype Threat and Its Problems. *Journal for the Education of the Gifted*. [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1932202X20986161)

### What Trainers Can Do About Stereotype Threat

Evidence-based strategies to reduce stereotype threat in training settings:

1. **Frame tasks as learning opportunities, not evaluations of ability.** The original Steele & Aronson finding was driven entirely by how the task was framed.

2. **Values affirmation exercises.** Having learners write brief reflections (15-60 minutes) on topics that are personally important to them has been shown to buffer against stereotype threat. This strengthens their sense of identity beyond the threatening domain.

3. **Emphasise that intelligence/skill is malleable.** Explicitly teaching that abilities grow with effort (growth mindset framing) reduces the power of stereotypes that imply fixed deficiency.

4. **Provide "wise feedback."** Combine critical, constructive feedback with explicit assurance that you believe the learner can meet high standards. This signals that the feedback stems from high expectations, not bias.

5. **Highlight role models.** Presenting examples of people from stereotyped groups who have succeeded in the domain reduces threat.

6. **Avoid unnecessary identity salience.** Do not ask learners to identify their gender, race, or other demographic information before a task if it is not necessary.

7. **Present stereotype-fair information before learning.** Research shows that providing information challenging stereotypes is more effective when presented before the learning task rather than after.

- Source: [Stanford CTL - Stereotype Threat Handout](https://ctl.stanford.edu/sites/g/files/sbiybj17446/files/media/file/stereotype_threat_handout.pdf)
- Source: [Smith College - Empirically Validated Strategies to Reduce Stereotype Threat](https://www.smith.edu/sites/default/files/media/Office%20Images/Sherrerd%20Center/How%20to%20Reduce%20Stereotype%20Threat.pdf)
- Source: [Rutgers - How Teachers Can Reduce Stereotype Threat](https://njalternateroute.rutgers.edu/blog/how-teachers-can-reduce-stereotype-threat-classroom)
- Source: [University of Colorado Boulder - Stereotype Threat](https://www.colorado.edu/center/teaching-learning/inclusivity/stereotype-threat)

## 4. Strategies for Building Safety in Training Environments

### 4.1 Engaging With All Learners (Not Just the Vocal Ones)

- **Use structured participation methods** rather than relying on voluntary contributions. Techniques like think-pair-share, written responses before discussion, or round-robin sharing ensure that quieter learners contribute.
- **Wait time matters.** After asking a question, wait at least 5-10 seconds. Research shows that most trainers wait only 1-2 seconds, which favours fast processors and extroverts.
- **Create "dialogic teaching"** -- extended exchanges that explore thinking rather than merely test knowledge (Robin Alexander's term). This shifts from a pattern where only confident learners volunteer.
- **Use anonymous participation tools** (e.g., sticky notes, digital polls, shared documents) so that contributions are not linked to identity.

- Source: [Harvard GSE Instructional Moves - Creating Psychological Safety](https://instructionalmoves.gse.harvard.edu/inclusivity-and-belonging/step-2-creating-psychological-safety)
- Source: [Teachers Flow - How to Build Psychological Safety and Encourage Participation](https://www.teachersflow.com/faq/psychological-safety-participation)

### 4.2 Creating Space for Questions

- **Model question-asking yourself.** When trainers openly say "I'm not sure about this -- let me check" or "That's a great question, I hadn't considered that," they normalise not-knowing.
- **Establish norms early.** Explicitly tell learners: "Questions are a sign of engagement, not a sign of weakness."
- **Provide multiple channels for questions.** Not everyone is comfortable asking aloud. Offer written question cards, digital Q&A tools, or breaks where learners can approach you privately.
- **Respond to questions with genuine engagement,** never dismissal. How you respond to the first few questions sets the tone for the entire session.

- Source: [ISTE - 7 Ways of Creating Psychological Safety for Students](https://iste.org/blog/7-ways-of-creating-psychological-safety-for-students)
- Source: [Learning Rebels - 7 Strategies for Creating a Supportive Learning Environment](https://www.learningrebels.com/2023/03/08/psychological-safety-7-strategies-for-creating-a-supportive-learning-environment/)

### 4.3 Understanding Who Is in Your Classroom

- **Know your learners' backgrounds.** Diversity in prior experience, discipline, seniority, culture, language, and confidence level all affect how safe people feel. A junior researcher in a room full of professors will behave differently than one among peers.
- **Be aware of power dynamics.** If supervisors and supervisees are in the same training, the supervisees may self-censor. Consider whether mixed groups are appropriate, or whether additional safety structures are needed.
- **Recognise invisible diversity.** Disability, neurodivergence, mental health, caring responsibilities, and socioeconomic background are not always visible but significantly affect a learner's experience.
- **Ask, don't assume.** Pre-training surveys about learners' experience levels, learning preferences, and concerns can help you design for the actual room rather than the room you imagined.

- Source: [PMC - Key Tips for Providing a Psychologically Safe Learning Environment](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9706932/)
- Source: [Inclusive Teach - Building Psychological Safety: Essential Strategies for Schools](https://inclusiveteach.com/2024/11/17/building-psychological-safety-essential-strategies-for-schools/)

### 4.4 Normalising Mistakes

- **Model vulnerability as a trainer.** Share your own mistakes and what you learned from them. When leaders acknowledge their own learning journeys, including mistakes and uncertainties, they create permission for learners to do the same.
- **Reframe errors explicitly.** Instead of "that's wrong," try "that's a really common misconception -- let's unpack why it's tempting to think that way."
- **Use learning portfolios or process-focused assessment.** When learners collect evidence of their progress over time, including drafts and revisions, the focus shifts from performance moments to learning journeys.
- **Celebrate productive failure.** Explicitly point out moments where mistakes led to deeper understanding.
- **Separate practice from evaluation.** Make it clear when learners are practising (where mistakes are expected and valuable) versus when they are being assessed.

- Source: [TIE Online - Nurturing Psychological Safety in Classrooms](https://www.tieonline.com/article/5708/nurturing-psychological-safety-in-classrooms)
- Source: [LeaderFactor - Creating a Psychologically Safe Environment](https://www.leaderfactor.com/learn/creating-a-psychologically-safe-environment)

### 4.5 Inclusive Practices

- **Use inclusive language.** Avoid jargon or in-group references that signal "this isn't for you."
- **Vary your teaching methods.** Different learners thrive with different modalities. Mixing lecture, discussion, hands-on activities, and individual reflection ensures more learners can engage.
- **Make the implicit explicit.** Unwritten rules and assumed knowledge exclude people who are new to a field or culture. Spell out expectations, norms, and processes.
- **Actively promote belonging cues.** Small signals that say "you belong here" -- using learners' names, referencing their contributions, acknowledging their expertise -- accumulate and create safety.
- **Address microaggressions and exclusionary behaviour promptly.** Silence in the face of disrespect signals that the environment is not safe.

- Source: [EdWeek - Promoting Psychological Safety in Classrooms](https://www.edweek.org/education/opinion-promoting-psychological-safety-in-classrooms/2016/12)
- Source: [The Learner Lab - A Guide to Psychological Safety](https://thelearnerlab.com/a-guide-to-psychological-safety/)

## 5. Important Caveat: Safety Does Not Mean Comfort

### The Core Distinction

**Psychological safety is not the absence of discomfort.** This is one of the most common misconceptions about the concept.

- **Comfort** = the absence of challenge or tension. Remaining within habitual behaviours and established patterns.
- **Safety** = the foundation that enables risk-taking, vulnerability, and growth. Feeling secure enough to be uncomfortable.

Edmondson's definition -- "a belief that one will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes" -- says nothing about avoiding discomfort. In fact, true learning almost always requires discomfort: confusion, struggle, the vulnerability of not knowing, having your assumptions challenged.

- Source: [Lean Blog - Psychological Safety vs. Psychological Comfort](https://www.leanblog.org/2024/06/psychological-safety-vs-psychological-comfort-understanding-the-distinction/)
- Source: [Phoenix Perform - Safety vs Comfort: Understanding the Difference](https://www.phoenixperform.com/single-post/safety-vs-comfort-understanding-the-difference)

### Productive Discomfort

The goal is to create environments where people feel **safe enough to be uncomfortable** -- to stretch, struggle, and grow beyond their current capabilities:

- In Edmondson's Learning Zone (high safety + high accountability), "mistakes are seen as opportunities to improve, but not as acceptable outcomes in themselves. It's a productive tension -- people are both supported and challenged."
- Psychological safety ensures that discomfort is **productive** -- directed at the content and the learning, not at social survival.
- Without safety, discomfort becomes **destructive** -- learners focus on protecting themselves rather than engaging with difficult material.

- Source: [Joanne Ostler - Psychological Safety is Not Comfort](https://www.joanneostler.com/insights/psychological-safety-is-not-comfort)
- Source: [Next Gen Learning - Intersection of Discomfort, Safety, Peace](https://www.nextgenlearning.org/articles/intersection-of-discomfort-safety-peace)
- Source: [Center for Sales Strategy - Safety + Discomfort = Growth](https://uyc.thecenterforsalesstrategy.com/blog/safety-discomfort-growth-psychological-safety)

### What This Means for Trainers

- Do not avoid difficult topics, challenging feedback, or high expectations in the name of "safety."
- Do not confuse learner discomfort with learner harm. Being confused by new material is productive. Being humiliated in front of peers is not.
- The formula is: **Safety + Challenge = Growth.** Remove either element and learning suffers.
- Be transparent about this with learners: "This training will be challenging. You will encounter things you don't know. That discomfort is part of the process, and this is a safe space to experience it."

## Summary of Key Takeaways for the Workbook Chapter

1. **Psychological safety is a precondition for learning**, not a nice-to-have. Without it, learners cannot engage in the risk-taking behaviours that learning requires (Edmondson, 1999).

2. **Unsafe environments produce fixed-mindset behaviour regardless of actual beliefs.** The brain's threat response overrides mindset. You cannot teach growth mindset without first creating safety.

3. **Stereotype threat is real and relevant to training**, though its effect sizes may be smaller than originally reported. The practical lesson stands: how you frame tasks, and whether learners feel their identity is "on trial," affects their performance.

4. **Safety must be actively built**, not assumed. It requires deliberate strategies: structured participation, multiple question channels, understanding your learners, normalising mistakes, and inclusive practices.

5. **Safety is not comfort.** The goal is not to remove all challenge, but to create conditions where productive struggle can happen without social threat.
