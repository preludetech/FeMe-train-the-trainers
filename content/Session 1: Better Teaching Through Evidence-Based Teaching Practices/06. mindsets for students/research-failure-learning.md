# Research: Failure as a Learning Opportunity

Research compiled for the workbook chapter on learner mindsets.

---

## 1. What Does Research Say About the Role of Failure/Mistakes in Learning?

### Errors enhance learning when followed by feedback

Generating one's own errors activates internal mental structures and facilitates encoding and integration of corrective feedback. Errorful learning followed by corrective feedback is beneficial to learning -- making errors can greatly facilitate new learning and enhance the generation of correct responses.

- Source: Metcalfe, J. (2017). Learning from Errors. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 68, 465-489. https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-psych-010416-044022

### Reversal of instruction sequence produces deeper understanding

A meta-analysis of 53 studies (166 experimental comparisons) found that problem-solving *before* instruction has nearly twice the effect size of receiving a year of instruction from a good teacher. When implemented with high fidelity to productive failure design principles, the effects can be up to three times that of a good teacher.

- Source: Kapur, M. Productive Failure research overview. https://www.manukapur.com/productive-failure/

### Individual and contextual factors determine whether failure helps or harms

Contextual factors (classroom climate, scale of failure, learner perception) and individual factors (self-worth, emotion regulation, prior knowledge) all influence whether learners interpret failure as a learning opportunity or as a shaming event. Not all failure experiences lead to productive learning.

- Source: Narciss, S. et al. (2025). Learning from errors and failure in educational contexts: New insights and future directions for research and practice. *British Journal of Educational Psychology*. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11803059/

## 2. Key Concepts

### Productive Failure (Manu Kapur)

**What it is:** A learning design where learners attempt to solve complex, novel problems *before* receiving instruction on the relevant concepts. Learners generate and explore multiple representations and solution methods. Though they initially "fail" (cannot arrive at the canonical solution), this struggle has a hidden efficacy for deeper learning.

**How it works -- two phases:**

1. **Generation phase:** Learners collaboratively tackle a novel problem without instructional support. They generate multiple solution approaches, activating and differentiating their prior knowledge.
2. **Consolidation phase:** The instructor provides explicit instruction, helping learners compare their generated solutions with canonical methods, building on the knowledge structures activated during the struggle.

**Four mechanisms (the "4 As"):**

- **Activation** -- prior knowledge is activated and engaged
- **Awareness** -- learners become aware of knowledge gaps
- **Affect** -- the emotional experience of struggle creates engagement and motivation to resolve understanding
- **Assembly** -- learners assemble new knowledge onto the activated structures

**Key finding:** Students who engaged in problem solving before being taught demonstrated significantly greater conceptual understanding and ability to transfer to novel problems than those who were taught first.

- Source: Kapur, M. Productive Failure. https://www.manukapur.com/productive-failure/
- Source: Kapur, M. (2014). Productive failure in learning math. *Cognitive Science*, 38(5), 1008-1022. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24628487/
- Source: Kapur, M. *Productive Failure: Unlocking Deeper Learning Through the Science of Failing*. Wiley, 2024. https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Productive+Failure:+Unlocking+Deeper+Learning+Through+the+Science+of+Failing-p-9781394219995
- Source: ETH Zurich, Productive Failure research group. https://lse.ethz.ch/research/productive-failure.html

### Desirable Difficulties (Robert & Elizabeth Bjork)

**What it is:** A theory introduced by Robert Bjork (1994) proposing that conditions which make learning feel harder in the short term are precisely the conditions that produce the most durable, transferable long-term learning. Conditions that feel easy and fluent often produce weak retention.

**Core insight:** When a learner partially forgets a concept and must exert more mental effort to recall it, the extra cognitive effort during successful retrieval causes a large increase in storage strength, making the memory much more durable.

**Examples of desirable difficulties:**

- **Spaced practice** -- distributing study over time rather than massing it
- **Interleaving** -- mixing different types of problems or topics rather than blocking them
- **Retrieval practice / testing** -- actively recalling information rather than re-reading
- **Varying conditions** -- changing the context of learning (location, format, examples)

**Key distinction:** The difficulty must be *desirable* -- the learner must ultimately be able to succeed with effort. A difficulty that the learner cannot overcome (due to lack of background knowledge, for instance) is not desirable but simply frustrating.

- Source: Bjork, E. L. & Bjork, R. A. (2011). Making Things Hard on Yourself, But in a Good Way. https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/04/EBjork_RBjork_2011.pdf
- Source: Bjork, R. A. & Bjork, E. L. (2020). Desirable Difficulties in Theory and Practice. https://www.researchgate.net/publication/347931447_Desirable_Difficulties_in_Theory_and_Practice
- Source: Bjork Learning and Forgetting Lab, UCLA. https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/research/

### Learning from Errors (broader research)

Research on learning from errors shows that educators can help by:

- Talking openly in class about errors and the reasons for them
- Providing constructive, specific, and timely corrective feedback
- Teaching learners how to maintain motivation and reflect on their mistakes
- Modeling positive error-handling themselves

A professional development programme based on video reflections helped educators focus more on their own instructional failures (rather than blaming environment or learner differences), leading to improved practice.

- Source: Steuer, G. et al. (2025). Learning from errors and failure in educational contexts. *British Journal of Educational Psychology*. https://bpspsychub.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bjep.12723
- Source: Waterford.org. Why Failure Is Good for Learning. https://www.waterford.org/blog/why-failure-is-better-than-success-for-learning/

## 3. How Does Responding to Failure Connect to Growth Mindset?

### Dweck's growth mindset framework

Carol Dweck's research distinguishes between two mindsets:

- **Fixed mindset:** The belief that abilities are innate and unchangeable. Learners with a fixed mindset avoid challenges, see effort as a sign of inadequacy, and interpret failure as proof of a lack of ability. They repeatedly reject opportunities to learn if they might make mistakes.
- **Growth mindset:** The belief that abilities can be developed through effort, strategy, and learning. Learners with a growth mindset see failure as feedback and a chance to improve. They view effort as a positive force that develops their intelligence.

### The connection to failure

A learner's mindset predicts how they respond to failure:

- **Growth mindset learners** treat failure as information -- "What can I learn from this?" They persist longer, seek feedback, and correct errors.
- **Fixed mindset learners** treat failure as identity -- "I'm not smart enough." They withdraw, avoid challenge, and hide mistakes.

Research finds that having a growth mindset predicts persistence and success, especially in STEM areas. More broadly, people who hold a growth mindset are more likely to thrive in the face of difficulty and continue to improve.

**For trainers, this means:** Before learners can benefit from failure, they need to hold (or be helped to develop) beliefs that make failure feel safe and informative rather than threatening.

- Source: Dweck, C. S. Growth and Fixed Mindsets summary. https://fs.blog/carol-dweck-mindset/
- Source: Dweck, C. S. et al. (2014). Academic Tenacity: Mindsets and Skills that Promote Long-Term Learning. Stanford University. https://ed.stanford.edu/sites/default/files/manual/dweck-walton-cohen-2014.pdf
- Source: Yeager, D. S. & Dweck, C. S. (2021). What Can Be Learned from Growth Mindset Controversies? *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8299535/

## 4. Practical Strategies Trainers Can Use

### Create psychological safety

- Establish clear expectations that mistakes are a normal, expected part of learning -- not a sign of inadequacy.
- Model vulnerability: openly acknowledge your own errors and what you learned from them.
- Never judge or reprimand a learner for making a mistake. Reinforce their effort, highlight how it can be improved, and ask how you can support them.
- Use inclusive norms that make all learners feel safe to take risks regardless of background.

Source: ISTE. 7 Ways of Creating Psychological Safety for Students. https://iste.org/blog/7-ways-of-creating-psychological-safety-for-students
Source: Harvard Graduate School of Education. Why Psychological Safety Matters in Class. https://www.gse.harvard.edu/ideas/news/22/09/why-psychological-safety-matters-class

### Use a "problem first, instruction second" sequence (productive failure approach)

- Present a challenging problem related to the topic *before* teaching the concept.
- Let learners struggle, generate ideas, and attempt solutions (even incorrect ones).
- Follow with direct instruction that builds on and consolidates what they discovered during their struggle.

Source: Times Higher Education. Using productive failure to activate deeper learning. https://www.timeshighereducation.com/campus/using-productive-failure-activate-deeper-learning

### Build in desirable difficulties

- Use spaced practice: revisit key concepts across multiple sessions rather than covering them once.
- Use interleaving: mix problem types rather than practising one type repeatedly.
- Use retrieval practice: ask learners to recall information from memory rather than simply reviewing notes.

### Normalise and debrief errors explicitly

- After activities, hold debriefs focused on "What went wrong? What did we learn from it?"
- Share examples of how errors led to breakthroughs (in the field, in past cohorts, in your own experience).
- Provide specific, constructive feedback on errors -- not just "that's wrong" but "here's why, and here's what to try next."

### Frame failure as data, not verdict

- Use language like "That didn't work yet -- what information does that give us?" rather than evaluative language.
- Separate the person from the performance: critique the work, not the learner.
- Celebrate the process of problem-solving, not just correct answers.

## 5. Important Caveats

### Failure without support is NOT productive

Productive failure requires deliberate instructional design. The struggle phase must be followed by structured consolidation/instruction. Simply letting learners fail and leaving them to figure things out is not effective and can be harmful.

- Teachers and materials that don't guide students through the process of exploring, struggling, and learning don't improve student performance.
- Source: EdWeek. What Teachers Get Wrong About Productive Failure -- and How to Get It Right. https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/what-teachers-get-wrong-about-productive-failure-and-how-to-get-it-right/2024/09

### Domain and age matter

- The productive failure effect has been most robustly demonstrated in STEM domains. Research has not consistently replicated the effect in non-STEM domains.
- Younger learners (grades 2-5) did not show the same benefit from problem-solving before instruction, likely because instruction in those grades did not tend to incorporate early problem-solving into a systematic process.

Source: Nachtigall, V. & Serova, K. (2020). When failure fails to be productive: probing the effectiveness of productive failure for learning beyond STEM domains. *Instructional Science*. https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-020-09525-2

### Difficulty must be desirable, not destructive

- A difficulty is only "desirable" if the learner can ultimately succeed with effort. If the task exceeds the learner's capacity (due to lack of background knowledge, for example), the difficulty is simply frustrating and counterproductive.
- Learners need sufficient prior knowledge to engage meaningfully with the problem, even if they cannot fully solve it.

### Emotional and identity costs of failure

- Whether failure is experienced as beneficial or detrimental depends on the learner's mindset, self-worth, and emotion regulation capacity.
- Learners who have been repeatedly told (implicitly or explicitly) that mistakes equal incompetence will not suddenly benefit from a "failure is good" message. Changing these beliefs takes time, conversation, and consistent demonstration.
- Source: Advance HE. Psychologically safe learning environment. https://www.advance-he.ac.uk/teaching-and-learning/curricula-development/education-mental-health-toolkit/social-belonging/psychologically-safe-learning-environment

### Growth mindset is not a magic bullet

- Simply telling learners to "have a growth mindset" is insufficient. The environment, feedback practices, and task design must all align to support growth-oriented beliefs.
- Growth mindset interventions have shown variable effect sizes, and their impact depends heavily on context and implementation fidelity.
- Source: Yeager, D. S. & Dweck, C. S. (2021). What Can Be Learned from Growth Mindset Controversies? https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8299535/
