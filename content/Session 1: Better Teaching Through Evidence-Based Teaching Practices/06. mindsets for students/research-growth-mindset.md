# Research: Growth Mindset Claims

## Claim-by-Claim Verdicts

| Claim | Verdict |
|-------|---------|
| Dweck coined/developed growth mindset | **Correct** — Stanford psychologist, concept from late 1980s research, popularised in 2006 book |
| Growth mindset = belief intelligence can be developed through dedication and hard work | **Partially correct** — Core idea is right, but "hard work alone" is a known misconception Dweck herself warns against. Should include strategies, support, and persistence |
| Fixed mindset: struggle = proof of inability | **Correct** — Central to the theory and well-supported |
| Growth mindset: struggle = part of the process | **Correct** — Central to the theory and well-supported |
| You cannot change mindset by telling someone to change it | **Correct** — Strongly supported by Dweck's own statements, the NSLM study design, and meta-analytic evidence showing most didactic interventions fail |

## Key Controversies

- **Effect sizes are small:** Macnamara & Burgoyne's 2023 meta-analysis (63 studies, N=97,672) found d=0.05 overall, nonsignificant after correcting for publication bias
- **Financial incentive bias:** Authors selling growth mindset programmes reported effects ~2.5x larger than independent researchers
- **The nuanced defence:** Yeager et al. argue effects are real but context-dependent — 0.11 SD for lower-achieving students in supportive school environments, which is meaningful for a low-cost intervention
- **Concept vs. intervention distinction:** Beliefs about intelligence *do* correlate with achievement (r=.27-.34 in large datasets), but changing those beliefs via interventions has proven much harder than expected

## Common Misconceptions (from Dweck herself)

1. Growth mindset = effort alone (it also requires strategies, help-seeking, adaptation)
2. It is a fixed personality trait (everyone is a mixture, shifting by context)
3. Anything is possible with enough effort (structural barriers and aptitude differences are real)
4. Posters and slogans can instil it (environment and feedback practices matter, not declarations)
