# Research: Stress-is-Enhancing Mindset

## Claim-by-Claim Verdicts

| Claim | Verdict |
|-------|---------|
| "Stress-is-enhancing mindset" exists as a concept | **Correct** — Formally defined construct with a validated 8-item measure (SMM), introduced by Crum, Salovey, & Achor (2013) in *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* |
| "How you think about stress changes what it does to you" | **Correct** — Supported by experimental studies (Crum et al. 2013, 2017) and an epidemiological study (Keller et al. 2012) tracking ~29,000 adults where believing stress is harmful + high stress = 43% higher mortality risk |
| "Better outcomes under pressure" | **Correct, with modest effect sizes** — A 2024 meta-analysis (Liu et al., *Scientific Reports*) found d = 0.23 for task performance across RCTs. Crum et al. (2017) showed greater cognitive flexibility, positive affect, and attentional focus under challenge stress |
| "Bodies respond differently, recover faster" | **Correct on "respond differently", partially supported on "recover faster"** — Different DHEA-S and cortisol profiles measured directly. Recovery not directly measured in stress mindset studies. Suggest softening to "their bodies respond more adaptively" |

## Key Researchers

- **Alia Crum** (Stanford) — primary developer of the construct
- **Peter Salovey** (Yale) — co-author on foundational paper
- **Shawn Achor** — co-author on foundational paper
- **Kelly McGonigal** — populariser (TED talk, book "The Upside of Stress")

## Relationship to Growth Mindset

Conceptually related (beliefs shape outcomes) but distinct constructs with different measures and mechanisms. Both fall under the broader umbrella of "mindset interventions" — the idea that people's lay theories about psychological phenomena shape their experiences and outcomes.

## Criticisms and Limitations

- Small effect sizes (d = 0.23)
- Limited sample diversity (mostly US college students)
- Tested mainly with acute stressors, not chronic stress
- The Keller mortality study is correlational, not causal
- Risk of dismissing legitimate distress
