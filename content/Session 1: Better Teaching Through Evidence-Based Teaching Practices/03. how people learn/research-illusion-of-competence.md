# Research: Illusion of Competence and Retrieval Practice

## Claims verified

1. **"Illusion of competence" is a real term** — CORRECT. Used by Robert and Elizabeth Bjork. Also called "illusion of knowing" or "fluency illusion" in the literature. Popularised by Barbara Oakley in *Learning How to Learn*.

2. **"Recognition is easier than recall"** — CORRECT. One of the oldest and most robust findings in memory research. Supported by dual-process theory (Yonelinas, 2002) and encoding specificity (Tulving & Thomson, 1973).

3. **"Re-reading with highlighting leads to overconfidence"** — CORRECT. Dunlosky et al. (2013) rated both highlighting and re-reading as "low utility" strategies. Re-reading creates familiarity, which the brain mistakes for the ability to recall independently.

4. **"Flash cards and quizzes help with recall/retrieval"** — CORRECT. The testing effect is one of the most replicated findings in educational psychology. Roediger & Karpicke (2006) showed that repeated testing substantially increased retention compared to restudying. The advantage grows over time — restudying helps short-term, but retrieval practice produces better results at 2 days and 1 week.

5. **"Getting an answer wrong is a signal, not a failure"** — CORRECT framing. Karpicke & Roediger (2007) showed that retrieval attempts — even unsuccessful ones — enhance subsequent learning.
