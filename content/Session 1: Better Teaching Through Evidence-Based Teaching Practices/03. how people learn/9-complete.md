# How People Learn

This section covers some of the core mechanisms of learning — how people build knowledge and understanding. As an educator, you don't just deliver content. You build human capabilities, and that means plugging into how learning actually works.

Before we dive in, a few questions to set the scene:

- Have you ever studied for a test and then gone completely blank when it was time to take it?
- Have you ever felt sure you aced a test, only to find out you did poorly?
- What's more important in the courses you'll be delivering: knowledge or understanding? (This one's a trick question — we'll come back to it.)

Many educators default to "memorise first, understand later." This chapter argues the opposite. When you focus on helping learners understand — truly grasp how ideas connect and apply — much of what they need to remember sticks as a by-product. That doesn't mean memorisation is useless; it has its place. But it's rarely the right starting point, and it's never the whole story.

| REMEMBER | *When you focus on understanding first, memorisation often follows as a by-product. Helping learners see how ideas connect and apply is often more effective than asking them to memorise facts up front.* |
| :---- | :---- |

## Understanding things

Here's something that happens regularly in teaching:

A student is asked about a concept — say, supply and demand. They give a perfect textbook definition. Then you present a short real-world scenario: a drought hits a farming region, and you ask what happens to the price of grain. The student can't reason through it. They focused on memorisation rather than understanding.

As an educator, it's important not to mistake memorisation for understanding. Many people would hear the perfect definition and stop there, accepting that the student understood. (We'll talk more about assessment later.)

So what *is* understanding?

### Bloom's taxonomy

You don't need to memorise Bloom's taxonomy, but it's a useful framework for thinking about the relationship between knowledge and understanding:

- Understanding is about building *connected* knowledge, not standalone facts.
- Understanding is about applying knowledge in different situations.
- Understanding allows you to analyse new information and form your own opinions.
- Understanding unlocks creativity.

### Activity: Memorisation vs. understanding in your own course

Pick one key concept from a course you're designing or delivering. Write down two questions about it:

1. A question that tests **memorisation** — something a learner could answer by recalling a definition or fact.
2. A question that tests **understanding** — something that requires the learner to apply, explain, or reason about the concept in a new context.

Look at the difference between the two. Which one would tell you more about whether your learner is actually ready to use this knowledge?

### Anti-pattern: progressing through Bloom's taxonomy from bottom to top

Bloom's taxonomy is often presented as a strict hierarchy — first you memorise, then you understand, then you apply, and so on up to creating. But research suggests the hierarchy is more relaxed than it first appears. Empirical studies have found direct connections between non-consecutive levels (Seddon, 1978), and mastery of lower levels isn't always required before engaging with higher ones (Agarwal, 2019). The revised taxonomy by Anderson and Krathwohl (2001) explicitly acknowledged this relaxed structure.

Can you build memory by focusing on understanding? Absolutely. Consider three questions about the same concept:

1. What is the definition of X?
2. How does X relate to Y?
3. How can X come into play in situation Z?

Understanding helps with memory because:

- Seeking understanding leads to memorisation of relevant facts as a side effect.
- A strong mental framework makes it easier to recall the component parts.
- When you see the relevance of new knowledge and connect it to what you already know, you're more likely to remember it.

| REMEMBER | *You don't need to work through Bloom's taxonomy from the bottom up. Learners can engage with higher-order thinking — applying, analysing, creating — without first mastering rote recall. In fact, doing so often builds stronger memory than starting with memorisation alone.* |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Three questions, one concept

Choose a concept from your training programme. Write three questions about it, following the pattern above:

1. A factual recall question ("What is...?")
2. A connection question ("How does this relate to...?")
3. An application question ("How would you use this in...?")

Now imagine giving these questions to your learners in sequence. Notice how each question forces the learner to revisit the same concept from a different angle — that's an example of spaced repetition and deepening understanding happening simultaneously. (We'll explore spaced repetition in detail shortly.)

This is one of the reasons you're being asked throughout this workbook to think about how to apply these ideas to your own course. It helps you process the information from different perspectives, build stronger mental frameworks, and form a better memory of what you've learned.

Notice what happens when you engage with material this way — you revisit the concept, retrieve it in different contexts, and space out your encounters with it over time. These aren't accidents. They map onto some of the most robust findings in learning science. Let's look at why.

Now imagine asking the questions out of sequence. For example, starting with a connection question. How could that be used to get learners to focus on the right takeaways, to build curiosity, to get them to seek out knowledge without waiting for you to tell them everything?

## How memory works — and why understanding helps

The science of memory explains *why* understanding-first works so well. When learners engage deeply with material — connecting ideas, applying concepts, asking questions — they're activating the same mechanisms that memory research has shown to be most effective.

### Working memory and long-term memory

Your brain handles memory in two broad ways. *Working memory* (sometimes called short-term memory) is where active thinking happens — it's what you're using right now to read and make sense of this sentence. It holds a small amount of information for a short time. *Long-term memory* has effectively unlimited capacity and can store information for years, but getting information there takes effort.

When you understand something perfectly in a lecture or workshop, that understanding lives in working memory. For it to persist, it needs to be *encoded* into long-term memory — through effortful processing, connection to things you already know, and revisiting the material over time. Sleep also plays a critical role: during sleep, your brain replays and consolidates what you've learned, strengthening the neural connections that form memories.

This is why a learner can perfectly understand a concept during a session and fail to recall it a week later. Understanding and remembering are related but distinct. If you want learners to retain what they've understood, you need to build in opportunities for them to revisit and retrieve the material — not just hear it once and move on.

| REMEMBER | *Understanding something in the moment doesn't mean your learners will remember it later. Understanding needs effortful processing, connection to prior knowledge, and revisiting over time to make it into long-term memory.* |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Spot the one-shot concept

Think about the last training session you ran or attended. Identify one concept that was covered only once — explained, perhaps discussed briefly, and then never revisited.

Now ask yourself: would you expect learners to remember that concept two weeks later? What's one concrete thing you could add to the programme to give learners a second or third encounter with it? Write it down — it could be a recap question at the start of the next session, a short application exercise later in the course, or a follow-up assignment.

### Chunking

Working memory doesn't just have a time limit — it has a capacity limit too. Research suggests we can hold roughly four items in working memory at once (Cowan, 2001). That's not a lot.

But there's a trick: the limit is on *chunks*, not on individual pieces of information. A chunk is the largest meaningful unit you can recognise as a single thing. For example, the string 0823456789 is ten separate digits — far too many to hold at once. But 082-345-6789 is three chunks, which is manageable.

Experts aren't better at remembering because they have bigger brains. They're better because they've built bigger chunks. A chess grandmaster doesn't memorise individual piece positions — they recognise familiar patterns as single units. A musician reads phrases, not individual notes. As your learners develop expertise, they'll naturally build larger chunks. You can help by structuring content into meaningful groups and making connections between ideas explicit. This is also why understanding matters for memory: when learners grasp the underlying patterns, they can chunk more information into fewer, more meaningful units.

### Activity: Chunk your content

Pick a section of your course where you present several related pieces of information — a list of steps, a set of principles, a group of related terms.

How is that information currently organised? Could you group it into meaningful chunks that would be easier for a novice to hold in working memory? Try reorganising the material into 3–4 groups, each with a clear label or theme. For example, if you teach ten data analysis steps, could you group them into "preparation," "exploration," and "interpretation"?

### Spaced repetition

If someone tells you something once, you might remember it — or you might not. Hearing it three times in a row doesn't help much either. But hearing it three times with gaps between each repetition? That makes a real difference.

The principle is straightforward: memory improves with repetition spread over time. This is called *spaced repetition*, and it's one of the most reliable findings in learning science. The spacing effect was first documented by Hermann Ebbinghaus in 1885 and has been demonstrated in hundreds of studies since (Cepeda et al., 2006).

Think back to those three questions — definition, relation, application. Each one revisits the same concept at a different level of Bloom's taxonomy, spaced across different moments in a lesson or programme. That's spaced repetition built into the structure of your teaching.

That said, think back to the anti-pattern we mentioned a bit earlier. It is not necessary to progress through Bloom's taxonomy from the bottom to the top. In fact, it's often sub-optimal!

| REMEMBER | *Repetition only works when it's spaced out over time. Revisiting a concept three times with gaps between each encounter is far more effective than covering it three times in a row. Build this into how you structure your course.* |
| :---- | :---- |

### Why cramming doesn't work

Cramming — studying intensively in a single session — can produce short-term results. A learner who crams the night before an exam may pass it. But the information typically fades within days or even hours. That's because cramming loads information into working memory without giving the brain time to consolidate it into long-term storage.

Research consistently shows that spacing study across multiple sessions is far more effective for long-term retention. In one study, learners who spaced their vocabulary practice across three days performed about as well as crammers on an immediate test — but dramatically outperformed them on a retest four days later, retaining roughly 75% of what they'd learned compared to the crammers' 56% (Bloom & Shuell, 1981). The advantage of spacing grows with time.

### Activity: Map the repetition in your programme

Take your course outline or syllabus and pick your three most important concepts. For each one, mark every point in the programme where learners encounter that concept — whether through a lecture, an exercise, a discussion, or an assessment.

How many encounters does each concept get? Are they spaced out, or clustered together? If a concept only appears once, brainstorm one way to bring it back later in the programme. Even a brief recap question at the start of a later session counts.

### Anti-pattern: reading and re-reading

A common study habit is to read and re-read material, sometimes with a highlighter. Eventually, the material starts to feel familiar and predictable. But when the learner is asked to recall the information — on a test or even a practice quiz — they can't.

This is an example of an *illusion of competence*. When you see something and recognise it, you jump to the conclusion that you know it and can recall it when you need to. Recognition is easy. Because it feels easy, people often become overconfident about what they actually know. A major review of ten learning techniques (Dunlosky et al., 2013) rated both re-reading and highlighting as "low utility" strategies — they feel productive but don't reliably improve learning.

Pulling knowledge out of memory is much harder. It uses your brain differently and demands more cognitive effort.

| REMEMBER | *Familiarity is not the same as knowledge. When material feels easy to recognise, learners often assume they know it — but they can't recall it when it matters. Re-reading and highlighting feel productive but are among the least effective study strategies.* |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Test yourself

Without looking back at the earlier sections, try to answer these questions from memory:

1. What's the approximate capacity of working memory (in chunks)?
2. Why does understanding-first often lead to better memorisation than memorisation-first?
3. What's the difference between spaced repetition and cramming?

Now check your answers against the text. Were there gaps between what you thought you knew and what you could actually recall? That gap is the illusion of competence in action — and the act of trying to recall just now was retrieval practice, which strengthens your memory of these concepts going forward.

Since so many learners focus on low-utility learning strategies, it's worth asking: is it an educator's responsibility to guide learners towards effective strategies? We think so — and the techniques in this chapter give you a starting point.

### Recall

If you want to remember something, practice remembering it. If you want to retrieve information when you need it, practice retrieval. Flash cards and quizzes are well-known ways to do this.

Research by Roediger and Karpicke (2006) showed that learners who practised retrieving information significantly outperformed learners who simply restudied the same material — and the advantage grew over time. Re-reading helped more in the short term, but retrieval practice produced better results two days and one week later.

Flash cards and quizzes can be uncomfortable — getting answers wrong isn't fun. But getting an answer wrong is a signal, not a failure. It tells the learner there's a gap in their knowledge that they can fill. If we give learners the opportunity to be wrong about something, we give them the opportunity to spot gaps and grow — to be less wrong in the future.

Applying a concept to a new problem is also retrieval practice — you have to pull the concept from memory, hold it in mind, and use it. Activities that ask learners to *do something* with what they've learned serve double duty: they build understanding and strengthen recall at the same time.

| REMEMBER | *Retrieval practice — pulling information from memory rather than reviewing it — is one of the most effective memorisation strategies. Getting answers wrong isn't failure; it's a signal that helps learners identify gaps and grow.* |
| :---- | :---- |

### Putting it into practice: spaced repetition and recall

What are some ways you can build spaced repetition and recall into the courses you design? A few ideas:

- Practice quizzes
- Flash cards
- Explaining spaced repetition and recall to your learners so they can use the techniques themselves
- Revisiting topics at different points in the programme
- Asking learners to build on concepts previously covered, so they're forced to think back
- Asking learners to explain a concept in their own words
- Having learners apply a concept to a new scenario they haven't seen before

That said, memorisation on its own is rarely useful. If your end goal is a real-world capability, keep that goal in mind when choosing your techniques.

### Activity: Design a retrieval moment

Choose one session from your course. Right now, it probably ends with the content being delivered and perhaps a brief discussion. Design a short retrieval activity — something that takes no more than five minutes — that you could add at the start of the *next* session.

This could be: three quick questions learners answer from memory before you recap; a "write down everything you remember about X" exercise; or a short scenario where learners have to apply a concept from last time. Write it out so it's ready to use.

## The zone of proximal development

You may have heard of "the zone of proximal development." The name sounds fancy, but the idea is practical. Proposed by psychologist Lev Vygotsky (1978), it describes the gap between what a learner can do independently and what they can achieve with guidance. Learning is most effective when it happens in this zone — where the challenge is real but not insurmountable, and the right support is available.

In practice, this means:

- If a course is too hard, learners become discouraged and may not be able to succeed.
- If a course is too easy, learners get bored — and, at best, they grow slowly.

Courses need to aim for a sweet spot: not too easy, not too hard.

A common problem is that learners avoid hard things. When something is demanding, many think, "I'm not good at this — I should do something easier." This reaction often reflects what psychologist Carol Dweck calls a *fixed mindset* — the belief that ability is innate rather than developed. Dweck's research on *growth mindset* (2006) shows that learners who see ability as something they can build through effort and strategy are more likely to persist through difficulty. As an educator, you can help by normalising struggle as part of learning — not a sign of failure.

| REMEMBER | *Learning works best when it's challenging but achievable with support. If learners avoid difficulty, help them see struggle as a normal part of growth, not a sign that they're not capable.* |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Calibrate the challenge

Think about one exercise or assignment in your course. Ask yourself:

- Could a complete beginner succeed at this without any guidance? If yes, it might be too easy.
- Would a learner with no prior knowledge fail even with support? If yes, it might be too hard.
- What knowledge or skills does a learner need *before* attempting this? Are those covered earlier in your programme?

If the exercise falls outside the sweet spot, sketch out one adjustment. You might add a worked example before a hard task, break a complex problem into smaller steps, or remove scaffolding from an exercise that's too guided.

### Example: learning by watching

Many learners decide, "I'm a visual learner, so I'll learn by watching YouTube videos." They watch diligently. The material feels comfortable and familiar, and they can follow along fine. But when they're asked to solve a problem on their own or apply what they've watched to a new situation, they struggle.

Some respond by watching more videos. Some lose confidence and think, "I'm just not cut out for this." And some adapt their strategy and start practising — working through problems, making mistakes, and trying again. That third group makes progress.

## Anti-pattern: learning styles

The example above shows a learner clinging to a specific "learning style" that actually harmed their progress.

You may have heard people describe themselves as visual learners, auditory learners, or kinesthetic learners. The idea is appealing, but research does not support it. A major review by Pashler, McDaniel, Rohrer, and Bjork (2008) found no evidence that matching instruction to a learner's self-reported style improves outcomes. People certainly prefer certain media over others, but preference doesn't predict results.

Consider:

- If you're an auditory learner, how should you learn to drive a car?
- If you learn best by reading and writing, does that mean music is out of bounds?
- If you wanted to become a theoretical physicist but identified as a kinesthetic learner, would you give up?

Holding onto learning styles can:

- Discourage learners from using materials and resources outside their preferred style
- Waste educator time on accommodations that don't improve outcomes
- Lead learners to lean into what feels easy rather than what actually works
- Discourage learners from pursuing subjects, skills, and career paths that they would excel at

Learning styles are not supported by evidence. Promoting them actively harms learners by limiting their options and reinforcing false beliefs about how they can learn.

That said, mixing media *is* useful — but not because of learning styles. Our brains process verbal and visual information through separate but connected systems. Revisiting the same concept through different formats — reading, listening, doing — forces us to process it in different ways, and that strengthens understanding. Research on multimedia learning (Cavanagh & Kiersch, 2022) confirms that people learn more deeply from words and pictures together than from words alone.

| REMEMBER | *Learning styles are not supported by evidence. But mixing media — combining words, images, and hands-on practice — genuinely helps, because it forces learners to process material in different ways. Don't match the medium to what the learner thinks is easiest for them; focus on what is broadly effective.* |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Diversify your delivery

Pick one concept from your course that you currently deliver in a single format — for example, a lecture, a reading, or a demonstration.

Brainstorm two additional ways learners could engage with the same concept using a different medium. For example: if you currently explain it verbally, could you add a diagram or a short hands-on exercise? If it's a reading, could learners discuss it in pairs or apply it to a case study?

Write down your three formats (original + two new ones). The goal isn't to cater to "learning styles" — it's to give learners multiple passes at the same idea through different cognitive pathways.

## When memorisation is the right tool

Sometimes memorisation really is the starting point. Medical students need to know the names of bones. Pilots need checklists committed to memory. Language learners need vocabulary. Safety protocols need to be recalled under pressure, not looked up. In cases like these, the facts themselves are the foundation, and there's no shortcut around learning them.

But even then, memorisation works best when it's paired with understanding. Knowing *why* a safety protocol exists makes it easier to remember and harder to skip. Vocabulary sticks better when you've used the words in conversation. The mechanics of memory — spaced repetition, retrieval practice, chunking — still apply.

So what's the answer to the trick question from the opening? Knowledge or understanding? You need both. But if you have to choose a starting point, lead with understanding. The memorisation will follow more naturally — and it will last.

### Activity: Memorisation audit

Review the content in your course. Identify any topics where pure memorisation is genuinely necessary — where learners need specific facts, terms, or procedures committed to memory before they can do anything useful with them.

For each one, ask: how are you currently helping learners memorise this material? Are you using any of the techniques from this chapter (spaced repetition, retrieval practice, chunking)? Pick one topic and sketch out a plan that combines memorisation with understanding — for example, a set of flash cards paired with a short exercise where learners explain *why* each fact matters.

## Glossary

**Bloom's taxonomy** — A framework that describes different levels of thinking, from remembering facts to creating something new. It's useful for designing activities that go beyond memorisation, though research shows you don't need to work through the levels in strict order.

**Chunking** — Grouping individual pieces of information into larger, meaningful units so they're easier to hold in working memory. For example, a phone number split into segments is easier to remember than ten separate digits. It's easier for a practiced chess player to remember the positions of groups of pieces rather than individual pieces.

**Encoding** — The process of transferring information from working memory into long-term memory. It requires effort — things like connecting new ideas to what you already know, practising retrieval, and revisiting material over time.

**Fixed mindset** — The belief that ability is innate and unchangeable. Learners with a fixed mindset tend to avoid challenges and interpret struggle as a sign they're not capable. Contrast with *growth mindset*.

**Growth mindset** — The belief that ability can be developed through effort and strategy. Learners with a growth mindset are more likely to persist through difficulty and see mistakes as part of the learning process.

**Illusion of competence** — The false sense that you know something well, usually because the material feels familiar (e.g. after re-reading it). Recognition feels easy, which tricks you into thinking you could recall the information — but you often can't.

**Learning styles** — The popular but unsupported idea that people learn best when instruction matches their preferred style (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, etc.). Research has found no evidence that matching instruction to self-reported learning styles improves outcomes.

**Long-term memory** — The memory system that stores information for extended periods, potentially for years. It has effectively unlimited capacity, but getting information into long-term memory requires effortful processing and revisiting.

**Retrieval practice** — The act of pulling information from memory rather than simply reviewing it. Techniques like quizzes, flash cards, and applying concepts to new problems all count. Research shows this is one of the most effective ways to strengthen learning.

**Spaced repetition** — Spreading practice or review across multiple sessions with gaps in between, rather than doing it all at once. One of the most reliable findings in learning science — spacing dramatically improves long-term retention.

**Working memory** — The mental workspace where active thinking happens. It holds a small amount of information (roughly four chunks) for a short time. When you understand something in the moment, that understanding lives in working memory until it's encoded into long-term memory.

**Zone of proximal development** — The gap between what a learner can do on their own and what they can achieve with guidance. Learning is most effective when it targets this zone — challenging enough to stretch the learner, but with enough support to make success possible.
