# How People Learn



In this section, we’ll be talking about some of the mechanisms of learning - how people build up knowledge and understanding.  This will help because, as educators, you don’t just deliver content to people - education is about building human capabilities so you’ll need to plug into those mechanisms. 
Quick show of hands
Have you ever studied for a test and then gone completely blank when it was time to take the test? 
Have you ever thought that you aced a test and then, when you got the results back, you did very poorly? 
Trick question: What’s more important in the courses you will be delivering: Knowledge or Understanding 

Remembering things

Memorization is often necessary when learning new things; it usually is not the end goal 
There are different techniques - some work, some don’t.

Memorization has a bad reputation because rote memorization and cramming don’t build long-term capabilities.  But if you wanted to understand something novel about a system, you should know about its component parts. Memorization is about building mental building blocks that you can use to solve bigger problems.
Spaced repetition
If I tell you something once, you might remember it. Maybe not 
If I tell you something 3 times in a row… not much change 
If I tell you something 3 times but leave a space, you are more likely to remember 
=> Memory improves with repetition over time 
Anti-pattern: reading and re-reading
Sometimes people try to study for a test by reading and re-reading material, maybe with a highlighter. Eventually, the material feels familiar and predictable.  But when the learner is asked to recall the information (e.g., on a test or even a practice test), they can’t.

This is an example of an illusion of competence. 

If you see something and recognise it, you jump to the conclusion that you know it and can recall it when you need to. Recognising information is easy. Since it feels easy, people can often become overconfident.

If you are asked to pull knowledge out of your memory, that is much harder.  It uses your brain differently. There is more cognitive load (it’s harder). 
Recall
If you want to remember something, then practice remembering it. If you want to retrieve information from your brain when you need it, you need to practice retrieval. Some well-known ways to practice recall are flash cards and quizzes. 

Flash cards and quizzes can be uncomfortable… 
Getting answers wrong isn’t fun 
But it is a signal

When learners get answers wrong, then this is valuable feedback: It tells them that there is a gap in their knowledge that they can plug.
This concept as a whole is important: If we give a learner the opportunity to be wrong about something, then that is an opportunity to spot gaps and fill them. It’s an opportunity to grow.

Cognitive load

Who has heard of “the zone of proximal development”? Sounds fancy… 
If a course is too hard, then learners can become discouraged, and they might not be able to succeed
If a course is too easy then the learners might get bored and discouraged. Best case is that they will grow, but slowly
Courses need to aim to be not too easy and not too hard. 

A common problem is that learners avoid hard things. They are often sure that their zone of proximal development is lower than it really is. 

If something is cognitively demanding, many think, “I’m not good at this and should do something easier”.  But growth comes from effort - learning works best when it’s challenging (enough). 
Example from real life
I have had many students who have decided, “I’m a visual learner, so I’ll learn how to code by watching YouTube videos”. They diligently watch lots of videos, the material feels comfortable and familiar, and they can follow along fine. But when they are asked to navigate a code base that they are unfamiliar with, or write their own code, they struggle.  

Some respond by watching more videos.
Some lose confidence and think, “I’m just not good at this code stuff”
Some adapt their strategy and practice writing code
Anti-pattern: Learning styles 
We just saw an example of a learner clinging to a specific “learning style” that harmed their progress.  

Audience participation: Who here has a specific learning style?

Sorry to say, it’s pseudoscience. People prefer certain media over others, but that doesn’t mean their preferences will lead to results.
If you are an auditory learner, how should you learn to drive a car?
If you learn by reading and writing, then is music out of bounds?
If you wanted to become a theoretical physicist but were a Kinesthetic learner, would you give up? 

Holding onto learning styles:
Can discourage learners from making use of learning materials and resources not in their preferred style
Can waste the time of educators who could be putting effort into things that work 
Can waste the time of learners who lean into mechanisms that feel easy for them, instead of mechanisms that work 


That said, mixing media is useful: We have evolved to use all our senses to understand the world, and our brains are interconnected. Visiting the same concept in different ways forces us to process it in different ways. 
Understanding things
Here’s another thing that has happened a lot: 

I’ll ask a student about a code concept (e.g., a return statement or a for loop), I’ll ask them to explain what it is, what it does, etc. 
The student will then give me a perfect textbook definition.  
I then write a small code snippet demonstrating the concept. I keep it super simple. The student is unable to reason about what the code does.  They focused on memorization rather than understanding.  

As an educator, it’s important not to fall into the trap of mistaking memorization for understanding. Many people would have heard the perfect definition and stopped there, accepting that the student understood.  (We’ll talk about assessment later on)

Audience participation: What is understanding? 

Slide: Bloom’s taxonomy 
You don’t need to memorize this thing, but it’s a useful way to think about the relationship between knowledge and understanding. 
Understanding is about building connected knowledge, not standalone facts. 
Understanding is about the application of knowledge in different situations 
Understanding allows you to analyse new knowledge, you get an opinion
Understanding unlocks creativity 
Anti-pattern: Progressing through Bloom’s taxonomy from bottom to top

Can you build memory by focusing on understanding?

Q1: What is the definition of X
Q2: How does X relate to Y 
Q3: How can X come into play in situation Z

Understanding helps with memory because:

Seeking understanding leads to memorization of relevant facts
If you have a strong mental framework, it’s easier to recall the component parts
If you see the relevance of new knowledge and can connect it to knowledge you already have, you are more likely to remember it 

This is one of the reasons why I keep saying you should think about how to apply this stuff to your course - it’s to help you process the information from different perspectives and build stronger mental frameworks and a better memory.
