# Research: Spaced Repetition

## Claims verified

1. **"Memory improves with repetition spread over time"** — CORRECT. The spacing effect has been demonstrated in over 200 research studies spanning more than a century of research.

2. **"One of the most reliable findings in learning science"** — CORRECT. Dunlosky et al. (2013) rated distributed/spaced practice as a "high utility" learning technique.

3. **"Hearing it three times in a row doesn't help much, but with gaps it does"** — CORRECT. Massed repetition (cramming) increases short-term retrieval strength but reduces long-term memory encoding. Spaced repetition engages multiple sets of synapses and promotes consolidation.

## Key sources

- Ebbinghaus, H. (1885). *Über das Gedächtnis* (On Memory). Ebbinghaus demonstrated the forgetting curve using nonsense syllables, showing memory loss follows an exponential pattern and that spaced review dramatically improves retention.
  - Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forgetting_curve

- Dunlosky, J., Rawson, K. A., Marsh, E. J., Nathan, M. J., & Willingham, D. T. (2013). Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques. *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*, 14(1), 4–58.
  - Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1529100612453266

- Cepeda, N. J., Pashler, H., Vul, E., Wixted, J. T., & Rohrer, D. (2006). Distributed practice in verbal recall tasks: A review and quantitative synthesis. *Psychological Bulletin*, 132(3), 354–380.

## Caveats noted in literature

- Ebbinghaus's original experiments used meaningless syllables, which is not how authentic learning works. Modern research confirms the effect extends to meaningful material.
- Spaced repetition works best when combined with active retrieval, not passive re-exposure.
