# Workshop Activities: Mindsets of an Educator

These activities are designed for a facilitator running a live workshop based on this chapter. Each activity includes timing, group size, materials needed, and step-by-step instructions.

## Activity 1: Mindset Spectrum

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Whole group, then pairs
**Materials:** None (open floor space helpful but not essential)

### Instructions

1. Read aloud five teaching skills, one at a time: *explaining concepts clearly*, *designing activities*, *reading the room*, *giving feedback*, *managing time*. For each one, ask participants to physically position themselves along an imaginary line across the room — one end represents "I believe I can definitely improve at this" (growth) and the other represents "This is just how I am" (fixed).
2. After each skill, pause briefly and ask one or two people near each end to say a sentence about why they placed themselves there. Keep it light — no one needs to justify their position.
3. Once all five skills have been called, ask participants to pair up with someone who stood in a noticeably different spot for at least one skill. Give pairs three minutes to discuss: *What would it take to shift one of your fixed-leaning beliefs even slightly towards growth?*
4. Bring the group back together. Ask two or three pairs to share one concrete idea that came up.

### Facilitator notes

- If space is tight, use a hand-raise scale (fist = fixed, five fingers = growth) instead of physical movement.
- Some participants may cluster in the middle for every skill. That is fine — prompt them gently: "If you had to lean one way, which would it be?"
- The point is not to shame anyone for fixed-mindset leanings. Frame it as honest self-awareness: you cannot work on something you have not noticed.

## Activity 2: Curse of Knowledge Swap

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Pairs
**Materials:** Blank paper and pens

### Instructions

1. Ask each participant to think of one concept from their own field that they know deeply — something they could explain without thinking. Give them two minutes to write a short explanation of it (four to six sentences), as if for a complete beginner.
2. Have pairs swap explanations. Each person reads their partner's explanation and marks every point where they feel confused, lost, or unsure what a word means. They should not try to be polite — the goal is honest feedback.
3. Return the marked-up explanations. Give each person three minutes to look at the feedback and note which gaps they genuinely did not see coming.
4. Debrief as a whole group. Ask: *Where did the curse of knowledge show up? What kinds of things did you skip over or assume your reader would know?* Collect a few examples aloud.

### Facilitator notes

- Pair people from different disciplines if possible — the further apart their expertise, the more clearly the curse of knowledge surfaces.
- If someone says "But my explanation was fine, my partner just doesn't know the field," use that as a teaching moment: that reaction is exactly what the curse of knowledge feels like from the inside.
- Remind participants this is a low-stakes exercise. The goal is pattern recognition, not perfect writing.

## Activity 3: Feedback Audit

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** Sticky notes (two colours if available), flip chart or large sheet of paper per group

### Instructions

1. Give each group a large sheet of paper divided into a simple 2x2 grid. Label the columns *Fast* and *Slow*, and the rows *Specific* and *Vague*.
2. Ask each participant to write down every source of feedback they currently receive (or expect to receive) on their teaching — one source per sticky note. Include formal and informal sources: evaluations, exam results, hallway comments, peer observations, learner body language, anything.
3. Have each group place their sticky notes into the grid based on how fast the feedback arrives and how specific it is.
4. Ask groups to look at their grids and discuss: *Where are most of your sticky notes clustered? What is missing from the Fast + Specific quadrant?* Give them five minutes for this.
5. Each group shares one observation with the room. Then ask the whole group: *What is one fast, specific feedback source you could start using this week?*

### Facilitator notes

- The Fast + Specific quadrant will usually be nearly empty. That is the point — let the visual do the teaching.
- If participants struggle to think of feedback sources, prompt with examples: "Do you ever ask learners a question mid-session? Do you look at their faces while explaining something? Those count."
- Two-colour sticky notes can be used to distinguish formal sources (e.g. institutional evaluations) from informal ones (e.g. a learner's puzzled expression). This is optional but adds a useful layer.

## Activity 4: Design a Quick Check — Together

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** None

### Instructions

1. Ask each participant to silently choose one key concept from their own training — something they would need their learners to understand before moving on.
2. In their small groups, each person takes a turn (about three minutes each). They briefly describe their concept (one minute), then the group collaboratively designs a quick, low-stakes check for that concept. It could be a question, a short task, a "explain it to your neighbour" prompt — whatever would genuinely reveal whether learners understood.
3. For each check, the group must also answer: *If most learners got this wrong, what would you change about how you taught it?* Push groups to be specific — "explain it better" is not enough.
4. Bring the room together. Ask two or three groups to share their favourite check and what made it effective.

### Facilitator notes

- The most common pitfall is questions that are too easy — they confirm attendance, not understanding. If you see this happening, ask: "Could someone answer this correctly without actually understanding the concept?"
- Encourage groups to think about plausible wrong answers, not just the right one. A good check reveals *how* learners are thinking, not just whether they got it right.
- If time is short, reduce to two turns per group instead of giving everyone a go.

## Activity 5: Diagnose Before You Prescribe — Role Play

**Time:** 25 minutes
**Group size:** Trios
**Materials:** None

### Instructions

1. Organise participants into groups of three. Assign roles: *Teacher*, *Learner*, and *Observer*.
2. The Learner picks a concept they genuinely find tricky (from any field — it does not have to relate to their own training). They should have a real gap in understanding, not perform one.
3. The Teacher's job is to diagnose where the Learner's understanding breaks down — without jumping to a solution. They should break the concept into smaller pieces, ask questions about each piece, and locate the specific point of confusion. Only after identifying the gap should they try to address it.
4. The Observer watches silently and notes: *Did the Teacher ask before telling? Where did they jump ahead? Did they check whether the fix actually worked?*
5. After five minutes, stop the role play. The Observer shares their notes (two minutes). Then rotate roles so everyone gets a turn as Teacher.
6. After all three rounds, debrief as a whole group: *What was hardest about diagnosing before prescribing? What did you notice about your own instincts?*

### Facilitator notes

- Most participants will instinctively start explaining rather than asking. When you see this in the room, do not interrupt — let the Observer catch it. That is more powerful than you pointing it out.
- If a Learner cannot think of a genuine gap, suggest everyday topics: how a car engine works, how interest rates are calculated, how a specific card game is scored. The concept does not matter — the diagnostic process does.
- The debrief is where the real learning happens. Spend at least five minutes on it. Ask specifically: "What did it feel like to hold back from explaining?"
