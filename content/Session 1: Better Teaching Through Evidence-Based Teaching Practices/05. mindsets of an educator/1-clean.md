Question for audience: What are some things that a teacher can do to become a better teacher?

## The Growth Mindset

The answer to that question draws on a concept called the **growth mindset**. If you have a growth mindset about something, you believe your ability in that area can improve with effort and practice. A **fixed mindset** is the opposite — you see your ability as set in stone.

Most people hold a mix of both. You might have a growth mindset about your cooking skills but a fixed mindset about your ability to draw. Mindsets also shift over time: a bad experience can knock someone into a fixed mindset, while a small win can open the door back to growth.

As you work through this workbook and start designing your own training, adopt a growth mindset about your ability to teach. You are not either "a natural teacher" or "not a teacher" — teaching is a set of skills, and skills improve with deliberate practice.

## Seeking Feedback

Teaching involves many different skills: explaining clearly, reading the room, designing useful activities, giving feedback, managing time. You will naturally be stronger in some areas than others — and you may not always know which ones need work.

Earlier in this workbook we looked at illusions of competence — the gap between what we think we know and what we actually know. Teachers are just as susceptible to this as learners. You might believe a session went well because you covered all your slides, while your learners walked away confused about a key concept.

### How do you find out what's working?

We use tests, projects, and other assessments to reveal gaps in our learners' understanding. But how do we get feedback on our own performance?

Most education systems are not built to give teachers useful, timely feedback. A quarterly exam result is a noisy, lagging signal — by the time you see it, weeks have passed and dozens of variables have muddied the picture. If you want to improve steadily, you need feedback loops that are faster and more specific than that.

## Taking Personal Responsibility

One of the most useful feedback sources is learner performance — measured often and skilfully. Frequent, low-stakes checks on understanding do double duty: they help your learners consolidate knowledge, and they show you exactly where your teaching might be falling short.

When a learner struggles, treat it as a diagnostic challenge rather than writing it off. This is not just kindness — it is one of the fastest ways to discover your own blind spots as an educator. Be warned: it is also hard work. But it is the kind of work that compounds.

## Thinking Like a Scientist

When you encounter a struggling learner, the temptation is to jump straight to solutions:

- Explain the concept again in different ways
- Make them sit with some problems and try by themselves
- Give them a motivational talk, or scold them
- Show them how it's done

Any of these might help — or might not. If you want to reduce your guesswork and genuinely improve your craft, start by trying to understand what is happening in the learner's mental model before you intervene.

### Diagnose before you prescribe

The more you understand about how a learner is thinking, the better positioned you are to help. If someone is struggling with a complicated concept, break it down into smaller pieces and check their understanding of each one. Once you identify where the confusion sits, you can address it directly — then reassess to make sure the fix actually stuck.

Throughout this process, protect the learner's confidence. Be transparent about your intention ("I want to figure out where this stops making sense so we can sort it out together"). Avoid anything that makes them feel singled out or inadequate. And when they overcome a hurdle, acknowledge it — that success matters.
