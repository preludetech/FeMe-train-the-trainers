Question for audience: What are some things that a teacher can do to become a better teacher? 

## The Growth Mindset

What we are demonstrating here is a concept known as the Growth Mindset. It’s super important!  If someone has a growth mindset, they believe that their abilities can improve. If someone has a fixed mindset then they believe their abilities can’t improve. 

A person can have a growth mindset about some things and a fixed mindset about others. A person’s mindset can also change over time - a bad experience might knock a person’s confidence. 

Growth versus fixed mindset image.

Of course, it’s important to help students to develop a growth mindset.  I encourage you, as educators, to develop a growth mindset about your ability to educate! 

## Seeking feedback

Teaching is not a simple thing; there are many skills involved. It’s only natural that you’ll be good at some things and bad at others. 

We spoke about illusions of competence in a previous chapter. It's important to know that teachers can have illusions of competence, too!

We can use tests, projects and all sorts of things to see gaps in students’ knowledge and capabilities. We have all sorts of tools for dispelling students’ illusions of competence.  How do teachers get feedback about their performance so they can grow?

Most standard school systems are not designed to give teachers feedback. If a student fails a quarterly exam, how much does that tell you about your own performance? Quarterly exams are a noisy, laggy signal.  If you want to get awesome at teaching, you need to find ways to get feedback more often. You need to know if your teaching is working. 

## Taking personal responsibility

One of the best forms of feedback is student performance - measure it often and skillfully. It will help them grow, and it will show you where there are gaps in your teaching abilities.

If there is a struggling student, take it as a challenge! It’s not just because it’s a nice thing to do for another person; this practice will make you face your deficiencies as a teacher and force you to grow. It’s also a lot of work. 

## Thinking like a scientist

If you have a struggling student and you really want them to succeed, then you might be tempted to:
Explain stuff over and over in different ways
Make them sit with some problems and try by themselves
Give them a motivational talk, or scold them
Show them how it’s done…

These might or might not be helpful.  But if you are interested in improving your craft and reducing your guesswork, then it is best to start off by trying to understand what is going on with the student’s mental model before jumping in with solutions.  

The more you can understand about a person’s mental model, the better! 

If the student is struggling to understand a complicated concept, then break it down into smaller concepts and see if you can gauge the student’s understanding of smaller things. Once you can see where they are confused or where the problem lies, you can address it directly. When you identify a problem, solve the problem and then reassess to make sure it stays solved. 

It’s important to do this in a way that builds confidence. Make sure that the student knows your intention, don’t make them feel dumb, and if they overcome a challenge, then celebrate that.
