# Research: Illusions of Competence

## Summary

"Illusions of competence" is an established term in learning science, originating from metacognition research by Asher Koriat and Robert Bjork. The workbook's definition -- "the gap between what we think we know and what we actually know" -- is an accurate plain-language summary. Research supports that this is a universal cognitive phenomenon affecting teachers and experts, not just learners.

---

## 1. Is "illusions of competence" an established term? Who coined it?

**Yes, it is established.** The term was introduced by **Asher Koriat and Robert A. Bjork** in their 2005 paper:

> Koriat, A., & Bjork, R. A. (2005). Illusions of competence in monitoring one's knowledge during study. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, & Cognition*, 31(2), 187-194.

- [PubMed entry](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15755238/)
- [Full PDF from Bjork Lab, UCLA](https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/Koriat_RBjork_2005.pdf)
- [Semantic Scholar entry](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Illusions-of-competence-in-monitoring-one's-during-Koriat-Bjork/4d25bc4f205114d6026550814c8a78075728a4b5)

The concept was further developed in a follow-up paper:

> Koriat, A., & Bjork, R. A. (2006). Illusions of competence during study can be remedied by manipulations that enhance learners' sensitivity to retrieval conditions at test. *Memory & Cognition*, 34, 578-588.

- [Springer link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/BF03193244)

The term was later popularised for general audiences through Barbara Oakley's Coursera course "Learning How to Learn" and her book *A Mind for Numbers*, both of which draw heavily on Bjork's research.

A broader review of these illusions appears in:

> Bjork, R. A., Dunlosky, J., & Kornell, N. (2013). Self-regulated learning: Beliefs, techniques, and illusions. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 64, 417-444.

- [Full PDF](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/spaced-repetition/2013-bjork.pdf)

## 2. Is the workbook's definition accurate?

**Yes, with nuance.** The workbook says illusions of competence are "the gap between what we think we know and what we actually know." This is a fair plain-language summary.

More precisely, Koriat and Bjork describe the phenomenon as a **metacognitive monitoring failure**: when people study material, they make judgments of learning (JOLs) that are systematically inflated because the study context differs from the test context. Key mechanisms include:

- **Foresight bias**: During study, the answer is visible alongside the question. Learners judge their knowledge with the answer in front of them, but at test time they must retrieve it without that cue. This creates an inflated sense of knowing.
- **Fluency illusion**: When material feels easy to process (e.g., after re-reading, or when items are massed together), learners interpret that processing ease as evidence of learning, when it may simply reflect familiarity with the surface features.
- **Massing illusion**: Kornell and Bjork found that 72% of participants believed massed practice was more effective than spaced practice, even though 90% actually performed better with spacing.

The workbook definition captures the core idea accurately. The only refinement worth noting: it is not just about a static gap in self-knowledge, but about systematic biases in how people monitor and judge their own learning during study.

## 3. Are teachers susceptible, not just learners?

**Yes.** The underlying mechanisms are universal cognitive biases, not specific to students. Research supports teacher susceptibility in several ways:

### The curse of knowledge

The most directly relevant finding for teachers is the **curse of knowledge** (Camerer, Loewenstein, & Weber, 1989): once you know something, you find it difficult to imagine not knowing it. This means teachers systematically underestimate how hard material is for learners.

- Teachers with more advanced subject knowledge are *more* likely to overestimate student understanding and skip foundational concepts.
- Extreme expertise can make someone a less effective teacher unless they have specific training to counteract this bias.

Sources:
- [Camerer, Loewenstein, & Weber (1989) - original paper (PDF)](https://www.cmu.edu/dietrich/sds/docs/loewenstein/CurseknowledgeEconSet.pdf)
- [Evidence Based Education: The Curse of Knowledge](https://evidencebased.education/resource/the-curse-of-knowledge-a-cognitive-bias-all-teachers-should-be-aware-of/)
- [Wikipedia: Curse of Knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curse_of_knowledge)

### Metacognitive biases are universal

Research on psychological obstacles to metacognition highlights that perceptual illusions, cognitive biases, and distortions of memory affect all human beings -- educators included. These are especially problematic in assessments of one's own learning and knowledge.

- [Psychological Obstacles to Metacognition - CIRL (Eton College)](https://cirl.etoncollege.com/psychological-obstacles-to-metacognition/)

### Teacher metacognition research

A 2023 study published in *Teaching and Teacher Education* examined teachers as learners and agents of self-regulated learning, finding that teachers' own metacognitive competence directly influences their ability to promote metacognition in students.

- [Teachers as learners and agents of self-regulated learning (ScienceDirect)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0742051X23000434)

**Bottom line for the workbook**: The claim "Teachers are just as susceptible to this as learners" is well supported. If anything, teachers may face *additional* risks from the curse of knowledge, since their expertise makes it harder to see what is difficult about material they have long since mastered.

## 4. Relationship to the Dunning-Kruger effect and metacognition

### Overlap

Both illusions of competence and the Dunning-Kruger effect are metacognitive failures -- problems with accurately assessing one's own knowledge or skill. They share a core theme: **people often do not know what they do not know.**

### Key differences

| | Illusions of competence (Koriat & Bjork) | Dunning-Kruger effect (Dunning & Kruger, 1999) |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Monitoring learning during study | Self-assessment of skill/ability relative to others |
| **Context** | Specific to learning and study situations | Broader -- applies to any domain of competence |
| **Mechanism** | Foresight bias, fluency illusions, encoding vs. retrieval confusion | Lack of metacognitive skill to recognise one's own incompetence |
| **Who is affected** | Everyone studying anything | Primarily those with low skill (who overestimate); also those with high skill (who underestimate) |
| **Remedy** | Retrieval practice, delayed judgments of learning, spaced practice | Gaining more skill (which also improves self-assessment) |

### The metacognitive connection

The Dunning-Kruger effect rests on the idea that acquiring a skill includes learning to distinguish good performance from bad. People with low skill lack this discriminatory ability, leading them to overestimate themselves. In their 1999 study, participants in the bottom quartile rated themselves near the 62nd percentile on average.

- [Dunning-Kruger effect - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dunning%E2%80%93Kruger_effect)
- [SAGE Musings: The Dunning-Kruger Effect and Metacognition](https://serc.carleton.edu/sage2yc/musings/dunning-kruger.html)
- [Psychology Today: Dunning-Kruger Effect](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/basics/dunning-kruger-effect)

### Recent debates

Recent research has questioned the original metacognitive explanation of Dunning-Kruger, suggesting the effect may partly be a statistical artefact (regression to the mean) rather than purely a metacognitive deficit. However, the broader point -- that people are often poor judges of their own competence -- remains well supported across multiple research traditions.

- [Wise up: Clarifying the role of metacognition in the Dunning-Kruger effect (PubMed)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30802096/)
- [Skill and self-knowledge: empirical refutation of the dual-burden account (Royal Society)](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsos/article/9/12/191727/96372/Skill-and-self-knowledge-empirical-refutation-of)

## Implications for the workbook

The workbook's use of "illusions of competence" is:
- **Terminologically correct** -- it is an established research term from Koriat and Bjork.
- **Definitionally accurate** -- the plain-language summary captures the core idea.
- **Well-grounded in claiming teachers are susceptible** -- this is supported by both the universality of metacognitive biases and specific research on the curse of knowledge in experts/educators.

For the chapter on educator mindsets, the most actionable connections are:
1. **Teachers should practise retrieval** on their own pedagogical knowledge, not just re-read teaching resources.
2. **The curse of knowledge** means teachers need deliberate strategies to see material from a learner's perspective (e.g., asking learners what is confusing, using formative assessment early and often).
3. **Modelling metacognition** -- teachers who are open about their own knowledge gaps normalise this for learners.
