# Research: Deliberate Practice and Teaching as a Skill

## Claim Under Review

"Teaching is a set of skills, and skills improve with deliberate practice."

## Claim-by-Claim Verdicts

| Claim | Verdict |
|-------|---------|
| "Deliberate practice" is a well-established concept in expertise research | **Correct** — Originated in Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer (1993); one of the most cited frameworks in expertise science |
| Deliberate practice differs from mere repetition | **Correct** — The framework specifically requires focused effort on weaknesses, clear goals, expert feedback, and mental model development — not just logging hours |
| Teaching is a set of skills | **Largely correct** — Many teaching activities (questioning, explaining, giving feedback) can be identified, practised, and improved. But teaching also involves judgment, relational work, and contextual decision-making that resist simple decomposition |
| Teaching skills improve with deliberate practice | **Partially correct** — Evidence supports that structured, feedback-rich practice improves specific teaching behaviours. But applying the full formal definition of "deliberate practice" to teaching is contested, and the claim should not be overstated |

## What Is Deliberate Practice?

K. Anders Ericsson (1947–2020) introduced the concept in his landmark 1993 paper studying violinists at a Berlin music academy. The core idea: expert performance is not primarily the result of innate talent, but of sustained, structured practice with specific characteristics.

### The Five Principles (per Ericsson)

1. **Push beyond your comfort zone** — Work at the edge of current ability, not within it
2. **Work toward well-defined, specific goals** — Break performance into components and target them individually
3. **Focus intently on practice activities** — Full concentration, not mindless repetition
4. **Receive and respond to high-quality feedback** — Ideally from a qualified coach or teacher
5. **Develop mental models of expertise** — Build internal representations of what excellent performance looks like

These principles were later summarised in collaboration with Deans for Impact in their 2016 report *Practice with Purpose: The Emerging Science of Teacher Expertise*, which attempted to map Ericsson's framework onto teacher preparation.

## Evidence Supporting Application to Teaching

- **Deans for Impact (2016)** worked directly with Ericsson to translate the five principles into teacher education design. They argued that clinical experiences, student teaching, and professional development should be structured around deliberate practice rather than unstructured classroom hours.
- **TEACH and Repeat (Gaba et al., 2020):** A resident-as-teacher programme incorporating deliberate practice elements (repeated teaching activities with feedback from dedicated coaches) led to sustained increases in residents' confidence in teaching, supervising, and feedback skills.
- **Marzano's framework** applies deliberate practice principles to teacher development, pairing specific instructional strategies with focused practice cycles and feedback.
- **Bronkhorst et al. (IntechOpen, 2022)** review how deliberate practice principles can inform teacher effectiveness when applied with appropriate adaptation to the teaching context.

The general pattern: when teaching sub-skills are clearly defined and practised with expert feedback, teachers do improve measurably. This is most evident for discrete, observable techniques (e.g., cold calling, wait time, checking for understanding).

## Criticisms and Nuances

### 1. Deliberate practice explains less variance than popularly believed

Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald (2014) conducted a major meta-analysis across multiple domains and found deliberate practice explained:

- **26%** of variance in games
- **21%** in music
- **18%** in sports
- **4%** in education
- **<1%** in professions

Their conclusion: deliberate practice is important, but other factors (prior ability, motivation, quality of instruction, contextual factors) matter substantially. The popular "10,000-hour rule" (Gladwell's oversimplification of Ericsson) is misleading.

### 2. Teaching may not fit the "performance profession" model

Hordern, Pratt, Kelly & Evans (2025) argue in the Cambridge Journal of Education that deliberate practice is not an appropriate basis for teacher expertise, for three reasons:

- Teaching is **not a "performance profession"** like music or sport — outcomes depend on learner agency, context, and relationships, not just the teacher's execution
- Teaching activities **cannot all be meaningfully decomposed** into sub-skills for isolated drilling
- The **goals of teaching are not solely about maximising measurable attainment** — they include socialisation, critical thinking, care, and other dimensions that resist quantification

They warn that foregrounding deliberate practice risks promoting a "reductive and technicist view of teaching."

### 3. Definition inconsistencies

Ericsson's own definition of deliberate practice shifted over time, making it difficult to test the theory rigorously. He sometimes required a teacher/coach, sometimes did not; sometimes included solitary practice, sometimes excluded it. This inconsistency has been documented by Macnamara & Hambrick (2020) and complicates direct application of the framework.

### 4. The sub-skill decomposition problem

Deliberate practice works best when skills can be cleanly isolated and drilled (e.g., scales on a piano). Many important teaching capabilities — reading a room, adapting explanations in real time, building trust — are holistic and contextual. They do not decompose neatly.

## What This Means for the Workbook

The claim "teaching is a set of skills, and skills improve with deliberate practice" is a reasonable and motivating framing, but benefits from some honesty about its limits:

- **What holds up well:** The idea that teaching improves through focused, feedback-rich practice (not just experience or time in the classroom) is strongly supported. The five principles are practical and useful guidance.
- **What to be careful about:** Avoid implying that teaching is *only* a set of decomposable skills, or that enough practice hours guarantee expertise. Teaching also involves judgment, relationships, and contextual sensitivity that develop through reflection and experience, not just drilling.
- **Recommended framing:** Use "purposeful practice" or "structured practice" as the working concept, drawing on deliberate practice principles without claiming the full formal framework applies perfectly to teaching. This is both more honest and more practical for the audience.
