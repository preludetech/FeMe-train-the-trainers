## The growth mindset

The answer to that question draws on a concept called the **growth mindset**. If you have a growth mindset about something, you believe your ability in that area can improve with effort, good strategies, and support from others. A **fixed mindset** is the opposite — you see your ability as set in stone.

Most people hold a mix of both. You might have a growth mindset about your cooking skills but a fixed mindset about your ability to draw. Mindsets also shift over time: a bad experience can knock someone into a fixed mindset, while a small win can open the door back to growth. It is not always the experience itself that determines the shift — it is the meaning you assign to it. A failure framed as useful diagnostic information can reinforce a growth mindset, while a success attributed to natural talent can reinforce a fixed one (Dweck & Yeager, 2019).

<!-- Growth versus fixed mindset image. -->

As you work through this workbook and start designing your own training, adopt a growth mindset about your ability to teach. You are not either "a natural teacher" or "not a teacher" — teaching is a set of skills, and skills improve with purposeful practice.

| REMEMBER | _Teaching is not a talent you either have or lack — it is a collection of skills that improve with effort and practice. Adopt a growth mindset about your own ability to teach, just as you would want your learners to adopt one about their ability to learn._ |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Where does your mindset sit?

Think about the different skills involved in teaching: explaining concepts clearly, designing activities, reading the room during a session, giving feedback, managing time. Pick three of these and, for each one, write down whether you currently lean towards a growth mindset ("I can get better at this") or a fixed mindset ("This is just how I am"). For any that lean fixed, write one concrete thing you could try in your next session to start shifting that belief — even something small, like asking a colleague to observe you and give feedback on that specific skill.

## Seeking feedback

Teaching involves many different skills: explaining clearly, reading the room, designing useful activities, giving feedback, managing time. You will naturally be stronger in some areas than others — and you may not always know which ones need work.

Earlier in this workbook we looked at illusions of competence — the gap between what we think we know and what we actually know (Koriat & Bjork, 2005). Teachers are just as susceptible to this as learners. You might believe a session went well because you covered all your slides, while your learners walked away confused about a key concept. In fact, teachers face an additional risk: the **curse of knowledge** — once you know a topic deeply, it becomes harder to see what makes it difficult for someone encountering it for the first time (Camerer et al., 1989).

| REMEMBER | _You are not immune to blind spots. The curse of knowledge means that the better you know a topic, the harder it is to see where learners will get stuck. Assume you have blind spots and actively look for them._ |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Spot the curse of knowledge

Pick one concept from your training that you know inside out — something you could explain in your sleep. Now imagine a learner encountering it for the very first time. Write down three things about that concept that might confuse or trip up a newcomer: jargon you take for granted, steps you skip because they feel obvious, or background knowledge you assume everyone has. If you are not sure, ask someone outside your field to read your explanation and tell you where they get lost.

### How do you find out what's working?

We use tests, projects, and other assessments to reveal gaps in our learners' understanding. But how do we get feedback on our own performance?

Most education systems are not built to give teachers useful, timely feedback. A quarterly exam result is a **lagging signal** — it arrives weeks after the teaching happened, so you cannot connect it back to any specific thing you did. It is also a **noisy signal** — by the time it reaches you, dozens of other variables (student motivation, home circumstances, other courses) have muddied the picture, making it hard to tell what was caused by your teaching and what was not (Mathematica Policy Research, 2010; Economic Policy Institute, 2010). If you want to improve steadily, you need feedback loops that are faster and more specific than that.

| REMEMBER | _End-of-term results arrive too late and are shaped by too many other factors to tell you much about your teaching. To improve, you need feedback that is fast and specific — close enough to the moment of teaching that you can still act on it._ |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Map your feedback sources

Draw two columns on a page. In the left column, list every source of feedback you currently have (or expect to have) on your teaching — formal evaluations, learner results, verbal comments, peer observations, anything. In the right column, note how quickly each one reaches you and how specific it is. Circle the ones that are both fast and specific. If you have few or none circled, that is your gap — and the next section will help you fill it.

## Taking personal responsibility

One of the most useful feedback sources is learner performance — measured often and skilfully. Frequent, low-stakes checks on understanding do double duty: they help your learners consolidate knowledge through retrieval practice, and they show you exactly where your teaching might be falling short (Black & Wiliam, 1998).

When a learner struggles, treat it as a diagnostic challenge rather than writing it off. This is not just kindness — it is one of the fastest ways to discover your own blind spots as an educator. Be warned: it is also hard work. But it is the kind of work that compounds.

| REMEMBER | _Low-stakes checks on understanding serve you as much as they serve your learners. They are your fastest feedback loop — use them to spot where your teaching is landing and where it is not._ |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Design a quick check

Choose one key concept from a session you are planning (or have recently delivered). Write a single question — not a trick question, just a clear one — that would tell you whether learners have understood that concept. It could be a short-answer question, a multiple-choice question with plausible wrong answers, or a prompt like "Explain this idea to a partner in two sentences." Then write down what you would do differently in your session if most learners got it wrong. The goal is not just to test them — it is to give yourself something concrete to act on.

## Thinking like a scientist

When you encounter a struggling learner, the temptation is to jump straight to solutions:

- Explain the concept again in different ways
- Make them sit with some problems and try by themselves
- Give them a motivational talk
- Scold them and tell them to try harder
- Show them how it's done

Any of these might help — or might not. If you want to reduce your guesswork and genuinely improve your craft, start by trying to understand what is happening in the learner's mental model before you intervene.

### Diagnose before you prescribe

The more you understand about how a learner is thinking, the better positioned you are to help. If someone is struggling with a complicated concept, break it down into smaller pieces and check their understanding of each one. Once you identify where the confusion sits, you can address it directly — then reassess to make sure the fix actually stuck.

Throughout this process, protect the learner's confidence. Be transparent about your intention ("I want to figure out where this stops making sense so we can sort it out together"). Avoid anything that makes them feel singled out or inadequate. And when they overcome a hurdle, acknowledge it — that success matters.

| REMEMBER | _When a learner is struggling, resist the urge to jump straight to a fix. Instead, break the concept into smaller pieces and find where their understanding breaks down. Diagnose first, then intervene — and check afterwards that it worked._ |
| :---- | :---- |

### Activity: Walk through a diagnosis

Think of a concept in your training that learners commonly find difficult. Break it into three to five smaller building blocks — the pieces someone needs to understand before the full concept makes sense. For each building block, write one question you could ask to check whether a learner has grasped it. Now imagine a learner who answers the first two correctly but stumbles on the third. Write down what you would say to them and what you would try next. Practice the full sequence: check, locate the gap, intervene, then check again.

## Glossary

**Curse of knowledge** — A cognitive bias where knowing a topic well makes it harder for you to remember what it was like not to know it. This can lead you to skip over explanations that your learners actually need.

**Feedback loop** — Any process that gives you information about how well something is working so you can adjust. In teaching, a fast feedback loop means you find out quickly whether learners understood what you just taught.

**Fixed mindset** — The belief that your ability in a given area is innate and unchangeable. Contrast with growth mindset.

**Growth mindset** — The belief that your abilities can develop through effort, good strategies, and input from others. In this section, we use it specifically about your capacity to improve as a teacher.

**Illusions of competence** — The gap between how well you think you understand something and how well you actually understand it. We are all prone to overestimating our own knowledge — and teachers are no exception.

**Lagging signal** — Feedback that arrives long after the event it relates to, making it hard to connect the result back to any specific action you took. End-of-term exam results are a typical example.

**Low-stakes checks** — Quick, informal ways to test understanding that carry little or no consequences for the learner. They serve two purposes: helping learners practise recall, and showing you where gaps remain.

**Mental model** — The internal picture a learner builds of how something works. When a learner struggles, the issue is often a flaw in their mental model rather than a lack of effort.

**Noisy signal** — Feedback that is muddied by too many other variables, making it difficult to tell what caused the result. A learner's exam score, for example, reflects far more than just your teaching.

**Retrieval practice** — The act of pulling information from memory rather than passively re-reading it. It strengthens learning and also reveals what a learner does and does not know.
