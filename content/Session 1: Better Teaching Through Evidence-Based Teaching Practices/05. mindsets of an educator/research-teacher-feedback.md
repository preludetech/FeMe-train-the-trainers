# Research: Teacher Feedback and Low-Stakes Assessment Claims

## Claim-by-Claim Verdicts

| Claim | Verdict |
|-------|---------|
| Most education systems are not built to give teachers useful, timely feedback | **Correct** — Multiple large-scale studies (OECD TALIS, RAND, Brookings) confirm that teacher feedback is typically infrequent, evaluative rather than developmental, and poorly coordinated |
| A quarterly exam result is a noisy, lagging signal | **Correct** — Well-supported by psychometric research. Value-added estimates from test scores have error rates of ~25-35%, and teacher rankings are highly unstable year to year |
| Frequent low-stakes checks help learners consolidate knowledge | **Correct** — Strong evidence base from retrieval practice / testing effect research and Black & Wiliam's landmark review (effect sizes of 0.4-0.7 SD) |
| Frequent low-stakes checks show teachers where their teaching is falling short | **Correct** — Supported, though the claim could be stated more precisely. Formative assessment data is only useful for teachers if they systematically interpret and act on it |

## 1. Education Systems Lack Good Teacher Feedback Mechanisms

### What the research says

**Infrequent observations:** Tenured teachers in the US are observed an average of 2.4 times per evaluation cycle, with some districts requiring as few as one observation per cycle. Non-tenured teachers average 3.3 observations. Formal observations tend to be brief, cover too many categories at once, and are tied to evaluation rather than development.
- Source: [How are districts observing and providing feedback to teachers? (NCTQ)](https://www.nctq.org/research-insights/how-are-districts-observing-and-providing-feedback-to-teachers/)

**Feedback perceived as unhelpful:** A RAND study found that only one in four teachers felt evaluation feedback helped them improve their practice. Teachers reported that feedback was too generic and did not promote professional growth.
- Source: [How Do Teachers Perceive Feedback and Evaluation Systems? (RAND)](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_briefs/RB10023.html)

**Systemic coordination problems:** When multiple people provide feedback, lack of coordination can lead to conflicting messages or more ideas than a teacher can manage. Evaluation criteria are often vague in terms of purpose, procedures, and performance standards.
- Source: [Developing an Effective Teacher Feedback System (Carnegie Foundation)](https://www.carnegiefoundation.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/CF_Feedback_90DC_2014.pdf)

**OECD TALIS findings:** While 88% of teachers across TALIS countries report receiving some form of feedback, there is wide variation (55% in Iceland to 99% in England). Critically, much of this feedback is evaluative rather than formative. Feedback is most effective when delivered in multiple forms, yet many systems rely on a single method.
- Source: [TALIS 2024 Results (OECD)](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/results-from-talis-2024_90df6235-en.html)

**Brookings analysis:** Research explicitly argues that better teacher feedback needs to happen outside the formal evaluation process, since evaluation-linked observations are too infrequent and high-stakes to serve a developmental purpose.
- Source: [Better teacher feedback can happen outside of the evaluation process (Brookings)](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/better-teacher-feedback-can-happen-outside-of-the-evaluation-process/)

### Bottom line

The claim is well-supported. While teachers do receive some feedback, the dominant mechanisms (annual/biannual observations tied to evaluations, end-of-term test results) are too infrequent, too generic, and too evaluative to drive meaningful instructional improvement.

## 2. Exam Results as "Noisy, Lagging Signals"

### What the research says

**High error rates:** A study by the National Center for Education Evaluation (Mathematica Policy Research) found that value-added estimates based on student test scores have Type I and Type II error rates of approximately 25% with three years of data and 35% with one year of data. This means roughly one in three single-year judgements about a teacher's effectiveness is wrong.
- Source: [Error Rates in Measuring Teacher and School Performance (IES/NCEE)](https://ies.ed.gov/national-board-education-science/2025/01/error-rates-measuring-teacher-and-school-performance-based-student-test-score-gains)

**Unstable rankings:** Across five large urban US districts, among teachers ranked in the top 20% of effectiveness in one year, fewer than a third remained in the top group the following year. Another third dropped to the bottom 40%. This instability demonstrates the "noise" in these signals.
- Source: [Problems with the use of student test scores to evaluate teachers (EPI)](https://www.epi.org/publication/bp278/)

**Expert consensus:** There is broad agreement among statisticians, psychometricians, and economists that student test scores alone are not sufficiently reliable or valid indicators of teacher effectiveness, even using sophisticated value-added modelling.
- Source: [Problems with the use of student test scores to evaluate teachers (EPI)](https://www.epi.org/publication/bp278/)

**Lagging by definition:** Grades and exam results are lagging indicators — they measure what students have already learned (past tense). They are easy to measure but difficult to improve upon, because by the time data is available, the instructional moment has passed.
- Source: [Leading and Lagging Indicators in Learning (The LearnWell Projects)](https://thelearnwellprojects.com/leading-and-lagging-indicators-in-learning-an-economic-perspective-on-improving-student-academic-performance/)

### Bottom line

The characterisation of exam results as "noisy, lagging signals" is accurate and well-supported. Test-based performance measures contain substantial measurement error (25-35% error rates), are highly unstable across time, and arrive too late to inform in-the-moment instructional decisions.

## 3. Frequent Low-Stakes Assessments as a Feedback Tool for Teachers

### What the research says

**Black and Wiliam (1998) — the landmark review:** This meta-review of over 250 studies found that strengthening formative assessment produced learning gains of 0.4-0.7 standard deviations — among the largest effects found in educational research. Critically, their definition of formative assessment explicitly includes the teacher's use of information to modify teaching: "all those activities undertaken by teachers... that provide information to be used as feedback to modify teaching and learning activities."
- Source: [Assessment and Classroom Learning, Black & Wiliam 1998](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/0969595980050102)

**Dual purpose confirmed:** Frequent, low-stakes formative assessments serve two functions simultaneously: (1) they help students consolidate learning through retrieval practice, and (2) they provide teachers with real-time data on student misconceptions, struggles, and learning gaps, enabling instructional adjustment.
- Source: [Formative Assessments (University of Illinois Chicago)](https://teaching.uic.edu/cate-teaching-guides/assessment-grading-practices/formative-assessments/)

**Teacher instructional adjustment:** An assessment practice is only truly "formative" if the data is used to inform instructional practice. Teachers use this information to "make decisions about the next steps in instruction that are likely to be better, or better founded, than the decisions they would have taken in the absence of this information."
- Source: [Supporting Teachers in Improving Formative Decision-Making (Frontiers in Education)](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2022.925352/full)

**Frequency matters:** A 2025 study in the Journal of Computer Assisted Learning found that frequent formative assessments are effective because continuous feedback allows students to make incremental adjustments, and simultaneously provides teachers with ongoing diagnostic information about where instruction needs adjustment.
- Source: [The impact of frequency and stakes of formative assessment on student achievement (Wiley)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/jcal.13087)

**Low stakes reduce distortion:** When students perceive assessments as low-stakes opportunities for learning rather than high-stakes evaluation, they engage more authentically and produce more honest signals of understanding. Low-stakes assessments also show lower rates of cheating and plagiarism.
- Source: [Frequent Low-Stakes Assignments (Carnegie Mellon University)](https://www.cmu.edu/teaching/online/assessinglearning/remoteassessment/studentlearning/lowstakes.html)

### Important caveat

The research supports the claim, but with a qualification: formative assessment data only serves as useful teacher feedback when teachers are trained and supported to interpret and act on it. Simply running quizzes without analysing the results does not create a feedback loop. Professional development in formative assessment practices is a key enabler.
- Source: [Formative assessment: A systematic review of critical teacher prerequisites (ScienceDirect)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0883035520300082)

### Bottom line

The claim that frequent low-stakes checks "do double duty" — consolidating student learning and revealing gaps in teaching — is well-supported by a large body of research. The key insight is that the same data stream serves both learner and teacher, but only if the teacher actively uses it to adjust instruction.

## Summary

All three claims in the workbook passage are supported by research:

1. **Teacher feedback systems are inadequate** — observations are infrequent (2-3 times per cycle), perceived as unhelpful by 75% of teachers, and tied to evaluation rather than development.
2. **Exam results are noisy and lagging** — 25-35% error rates in test-based teacher effectiveness measures, extreme year-to-year instability in rankings, and inherent delays that prevent timely instructional adjustment.
3. **Frequent low-stakes assessments serve as teacher feedback** — strong evidence base (Black & Wiliam effect sizes of 0.4-0.7 SD), with the critical caveat that teachers must actively interpret and act on the data for the feedback loop to work.

The passage's framing is sound and appropriately informal for the target audience. No corrections needed.
