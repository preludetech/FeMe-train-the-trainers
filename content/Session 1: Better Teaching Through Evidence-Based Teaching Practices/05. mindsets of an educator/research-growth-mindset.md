# Research: Growth Mindset Claims (Educator Chapter)

This file verifies specific claims made in the "Mindsets of an Educator" workbook chapter against Carol Dweck's published work and subsequent research, including criticisms.

---

## Claim 1: Growth mindset = belief ability can improve with effort and practice; fixed mindset = ability is set in stone

> "If you have a growth mindset about something, you believe your ability in that area can improve with effort and practice. A fixed mindset is the opposite — you see your ability as set in stone."

### Verdict: Accurate, with one nuance

This is a fair plain-language summary of Dweck's core definitions. In her original academic work, these were called "incremental theory" (growth) and "entity theory" (fixed) of intelligence. Dweck defines a growth mindset as the belief that abilities "can be developed through personal effort, good learning strategies, and lots of mentoring and support from others," and a fixed mindset as viewing ability as "a deep-seated, fixed attribute of the self" (Dweck & Yeager, 2019).

### Nuance worth noting

The phrase "effort and practice" in the claim is fine but slightly incomplete. Dweck has explicitly warned against reducing growth mindset to effort alone. In a 2015 Education Week piece, she acknowledged that she and her colleagues "may have originally put too much emphasis on sheer effort." A complete picture includes effort *plus* trying new strategies, seeking help, and adapting approaches. The workbook phrasing is acceptable for an introductory framing, but trainers should be aware that "just try harder" is the single most common misapplication of the concept.

### Misconception alert

The "false growth mindset" (Dweck, 2015): praising effort without addressing ineffective strategies. Saying "great effort!" to a student who is working hard but not learning is not a growth mindset practice — it is a feel-good platitude that can actually harm learning.

## Claim 2: Most people hold a mix of both mindsets; you might have growth mindset about cooking but fixed mindset about drawing

> "Most people hold a mix of both. You might have a growth mindset about your cooking skills but a fixed mindset about your ability to draw."

### Verdict: Accurate and well-supported

This claim aligns with both Dweck's later clarifications and a growing body of research on domain-specific mindsets.

**Dweck's own position:** In her 2015 revisit, Dweck acknowledged that earlier work "talked too much about people having one mindset or the other rather than portraying people as mixtures." She now explicitly states that everyone holds a mixture of growth and fixed beliefs.

**Domain-specificity research:** A 2024 systematic review (Kyler et al.) identified 140 distinct mindset domains studied in the literature, confirming that mindsets are not a single general trait. Research supports that people can hold growth beliefs in one area (e.g., social skills) and fixed beliefs in another (e.g., mathematics). A bifactor model study found that both a global mindset factor and domain-specific mindset factors independently predict outcomes (Burgoyne et al., 2021).

**Quantitative evidence:** Research on student populations suggests roughly 40% endorse a growth mindset, 40% a fixed mindset, and 20% are "mixed" regarding general intelligence. In language learning contexts, 57.7% of learners fell into a mixed profile endorsing elements of both mindsets (Lou & Noels, 2020).

### Misconception alert

The cooking/drawing example is vivid and appropriate. The key insight is that mindsets are not a personality type — they are belief clusters that vary by domain, context, and time. Treating "growth mindset person" as an identity label is itself a fixed-mindset framing.

## Claim 3: Mindsets shift over time — a bad experience can push someone toward fixed, a small win can open the door back to growth

> "Mindsets also shift over time: a bad experience can knock someone into a fixed mindset, while a small win can open the door back to growth."

### Verdict: Accurate and consistent with current theory

Dweck's more recent work (2019) emphasises that mindsets are situational and fluid, not stable traits. The research supports that a person's dominant mindset varies on a continuum between growth and fixed extremes, with the precise position shifting by situation.

**Situational triggers:** Research identifies specific triggers that can shift someone toward a fixed mindset:
- Feeling judged or evaluated
- Being in an environment where mistakes are not tolerated
- Facing tasks perceived as threatening to identity or self-worth
- Encountering a domain where one has experienced repeated failure

Conversely, environments that support challenge-seeking, normalise mistakes, and provide constructive feedback promote shifts toward a growth mindset. The "small win" idea is supported by research on mastery experiences — successfully navigating a challenge reinforces the belief that improvement is possible.

**Concrete example from research:** A department chair may hold a growth mindset when learning a new email system (low stakes) but shift to a fixed mindset when learning to handle confrontational colleagues (high stakes, identity-threatening). This illustrates how the same person's mindset fluctuates by context and emotional stakes.

### Nuance worth noting

While the claim is directionally correct, the triggers are not always as simple as "bad experience = fixed, win = growth." The meaning a person assigns to the experience matters more than the experience itself. A failure framed as diagnostic information can actually reinforce a growth mindset, while a success attributed to innate talent can reinforce a fixed one. The quality of the interpretive framing matters as much as the event.

## Claim 4: Growth mindset applies to teaching ability specifically

### Verdict: Supported, though the research base is still developing

There is clear theoretical and emerging empirical support for the idea that educators can hold growth or fixed mindsets about their own teaching ability, and that this matters for both their professional development and their students.

**Teacher mindset affects students:** Teachers' growth mindsets have a mild positive and statistically significant association with the development of students' growth mindsets (Mesler et al., 2021). Students endorsed more of a growth mindset in classrooms where teachers used guided inquiry and in schools emphasising social-emotional development.

**Teacher mindset affects teacher behaviour:** Teachers with growth mindsets about their own abilities are more likely to embrace challenges, persist through setbacks, learn from feedback, and engage in ongoing professional development. They report higher job satisfaction and manage job demands better, reducing burnout.

**Teacher mindset shapes pedagogy:** Teachers' beliefs influence their pedagogical decisions. Teachers with fixed mindsets are more likely to differentiate tasks by perceived ability (tracking), and students developed stronger fixed mindsets in those classrooms. Teachers with growth mindsets are more likely to use formative feedback, normalise struggle, and adapt their teaching strategies.

### Important caveat

While the concept clearly applies to teaching, **teacher-focused growth mindset interventions have not yet shown strong results.** The Yeager & Dweck (2021) review notes that teacher-focused interventions "have not worked so far, even though they were developed with great care and were labor-intensive." This does not mean teachers cannot develop growth mindsets about teaching — it means we do not yet have a reliable scalable method for inducing this change through short interventions. Growth mindset about teaching likely develops through sustained experience, mentoring, reflective practice, and environmental support rather than through a workshop or training module.

### Misconception alert: the "false growth mindset" in educators

Dweck specifically flagged that some educators adopt the language of growth mindset without the substance. Warning signs include:
- Praising effort without addressing ineffective strategies
- Telling learners they "can do anything" without providing scaffolding or guidance
- Displaying growth mindset posters while maintaining fixed-mindset classroom practices (e.g., rigid ability grouping)
- Ignoring actual achievement gaps in the name of positivity

## Overall Controversies and Replication Issues

### What has replicated well

- The basic correlation between growth mindset beliefs and academic achievement replicates across large samples, including the National Study of Learning Mindsets (N=12,490) and OECD data from 555,458 students across 74 nations.
- The "meaning system" — how mindsets influence goals, effort beliefs, and attributions — replicates in large pre-registered studies.
- Mindsets are domain-specific and situational, not fixed personality types.

### What is contested

- **Intervention effect sizes are small:** Macnamara & Burgoyne's 2023 meta-analysis (63 studies, N=97,672) found an overall effect of d=0.05, which was nonsignificant after correcting for publication bias. Defenders argue this is because effects are concentrated in specific subgroups (lower-achieving students in supportive environments), where effects reach 0.11-0.25 SD.
- **Financial conflicts of interest:** Researchers selling growth mindset programmes reported effects approximately 2.5x larger than independent researchers.
- **Scalability:** The concept is robust, but scalable interventions that reliably shift mindsets remain elusive, particularly for teachers.
- **Cultural variation:** Some cultures (e.g., mainland China, Czech Republic) show weaker mindset-achievement associations, suggesting the framework may be more applicable in some cultural contexts than others.

### The consensus position

The concept of growth vs. fixed mindset is well-established and replicable. The controversy is primarily about whether brief interventions can change mindsets and whether the resulting effects are large enough to matter at scale. The core ideas — that beliefs about ability shape learning behaviour, that these beliefs are domain-specific and situational, and that they can change — are well-supported.
